Dandruff Treatment: What the AAD Actually Recommends (and What They Miss)

Tina Mui March 23, 2026 9 min read

The American Academy of Dermatology publishes clear, evidence-based guidelines for treating dandruff at home. Their recommendations cover which active ingredients to look for, how long to leave shampoo on your scalp, and when to see a specialist. What the AAD does not address is the broader scalp environment, including water quality, microbiome balance, and barrier health, that determines whether those treatments actually work long-term.

What the American Academy of Dermatology Actually Says About Dandruff

The American Academy of Dermatology is the largest dermatology organization in the world, representing more than 20,000 physicians. When they publish treatment guidelines, those guidelines reflect the consensus of peer-reviewed research and clinical practice. For dandruff, their public-facing treatment page is straightforward and practical.

The AAD defines dandruff as a common, non-contagious skin condition that causes white or yellowish flakes on the scalp. They attribute it to a yeast called Malassezia that lives naturally on everyone's skin but proliferates in some people, triggering inflammation and accelerated skin cell turnover. The flakes you see are clusters of dead skin cells shedding faster than normal.

Their core recommendation is simple: use an over-the-counter dandruff shampoo containing one of five specific active ingredients, apply it correctly (with adequate contact time), and give it four to six weeks before evaluating results. If one ingredient does not work, try a different one. If none of them work, see a board-certified dermatologist.

This is solid, evidence-based advice. But it treats dandruff as an isolated scalp condition, separate from the broader environment your scalp operates in every day. Before we get to what the AAD leaves out, let us look closely at what they get right.

The AAD's Recommended Active Ingredients

The AAD identifies five active ingredients that have been clinically shown to reduce dandruff symptoms. Each works through a different mechanism, which is why the AAD recommends switching to a different ingredient if the first one you try is not effective. Here is how they compare.

Active Ingredient How It Works OTC Strength Best For Watch For
Pyrithione zinc Antifungal and antibacterial; slows Malassezia growth 1-2% Mild to moderate dandruff; first-line treatment Generally well tolerated; rare scalp dryness
Selenium sulfide Reduces yeast growth and slows skin cell turnover rate 1% (OTC), 2.5% (Rx) Moderate dandruff with significant flaking Can discolor blond, gray, or chemically treated hair
Ketoconazole Broad-spectrum antifungal that directly kills Malassezia 1% (OTC), 2% (Rx) Persistent dandruff that does not respond to zinc or selenium More expensive; some report scalp dryness at higher strengths
Salicylic acid Exfoliates built-up scales; helps other actives penetrate 2-3% Heavy scale buildup; often used with another active Can dry the scalp; may worsen flaking if used alone without moisturizing
Coal tar Slows skin cell production and reduces inflammation 0.5-5% Stubborn cases; also used for scalp psoriasis Strong odor; can darken light hair; may increase UV sensitivity

The AAD's recommendation to rotate between these ingredients is worth noting. Malassezia can develop a tolerance to certain antifungals over time, so alternating between two products with different active ingredients can maintain effectiveness. For example, you might use a ketoconazole shampoo twice a week and a pyrithione zinc formula on alternate wash days.

Research Note

Why the AAD lists five ingredients, not one: A 2015 comprehensive review found that no single antifungal agent is universally effective against all Malassezia species. The fungus exists in at least 14 recognized species, and individual scalps may harbor different combinations. This species variation explains why one person responds to pyrithione zinc while another needs ketoconazole, and why the AAD recommends trying alternatives rather than assuming the first choice will work.

Borda LJ, Wikramanayake TC. "Seborrheic Dermatitis and Dandruff: A Comprehensive Review." Journal of Clinical and Investigative Dermatology. 2015;3(2). | AAD: How to Treat Dandruff

It is also worth noting what the AAD does not recommend. They do not endorse "natural" dandruff remedies like tea tree oil or apple cider vinegar as primary treatments. While some studies suggest mild antifungal properties in these ingredients, the evidence is not strong enough for the AAD to include them in their guidelines. If you want to follow the AAD's protocol, stick with one of the five approved actives.

How to Use Dandruff Shampoo: The 5-Minute Protocol

This is the detail that most people miss, and it is the single most searched aspect of the AAD's dandruff guidelines. The American Academy of Dermatology specifically recommends leaving dandruff shampoo on the scalp for approximately five minutes before rinsing. This is not a suggestion. It is the difference between a treatment that works and one that does not.

Most people apply dandruff shampoo the same way they use regular shampoo: lather, rinse, done, in under sixty seconds. At that speed, the active ingredients never reach effective concentration on the scalp. They get diluted by water and rinsed away before they can suppress yeast growth or reduce inflammation.

The AAD's Step-by-Step Protocol

  • Wet your hair thoroughly - Start with warm (not hot) water. Hot water can strip protective oils from your scalp, which the AAD notes can worsen irritation.
  • Apply the dandruff shampoo directly to the scalp - Focus on the scalp, not the hair lengths. Use your fingertips (not nails) to massage the product into the scalp surface.
  • Leave it on for five minutes - This is the critical step. Set a timer if needed. The contact time allows active ingredients like ketoconazole and pyrithione zinc to penetrate the scalp surface and reach the Malassezia colonies beneath.
  • Rinse thoroughly - Make sure no residue remains, as leftover product can cause additional irritation or buildup.
  • Follow with conditioner on the lengths - The AAD notes that dandruff shampoos can be drying to hair. Apply conditioner from mid-shaft to ends, avoiding the scalp.

Frequency Matters Too

The AAD makes an important distinction based on hair type. For Caucasian and Asian hair types, they recommend using dandruff shampoo daily or every other day until symptoms improve, then reducing to two or three times per week for maintenance. For Black hair, they recommend dandruff shampoo only once per week, as more frequent use of these formulas can cause significant dryness and breakage in coarser, curlier hair textures.

This hair-type distinction is one of the more nuanced aspects of the AAD's guidelines, and it is often overlooked in generic dandruff advice. The underlying science is the same, but the application protocol needs to match the hair's structural needs.

I tell every client the same thing: if you are not leaving your dandruff shampoo on for five full minutes, you are essentially just using an expensive regular shampoo. The contact time is everything.

Seborrheic Dermatitis vs. Common Dandruff: The AAD's Distinction

The American Academy of Dermatology treats dandruff and seborrheic dermatitis as points on the same spectrum rather than completely separate conditions. Both are caused by the same Malassezia yeast overgrowth. The difference is severity.

Common dandruff, in the AAD's framework, is the mild end of the spectrum: white or yellowish flakes, mild itching, limited to the scalp. It responds well to over-the-counter dandruff shampoos used consistently with the five-minute protocol described above.

Seborrheic dermatitis is the more severe presentation. According to the AAD, it can involve thick, greasy scales that are yellow or white, significant redness and inflammation on the scalp, and spread to other areas of the body, particularly the eyebrows, sides of the nose, behind the ears, and the chest. When dandruff-like symptoms appear in these non-scalp locations, the AAD considers it seborrheic dermatitis rather than simple dandruff.

The practical distinction matters because seborrheic dermatitis often requires prescription-strength treatment. While mild dandruff usually responds to OTC pyrithione zinc or ketoconazole, seborrheic dermatitis may need prescription 2% ketoconazole shampoo, topical antifungals, or short courses of low-potency topical corticosteroids to control the inflammation.

The AAD also notes that seborrheic dermatitis tends to be chronic and cyclical. It may improve for weeks or months and then flare again, often in response to stress, cold weather, or illness. Understanding this pattern helps set realistic expectations: treatment is about management, not cure. The goal is to minimize flares and control symptoms when they do appear.

If you are unsure whether you are dealing with common dandruff or seborrheic dermatitis, the AAD recommends trying OTC treatment first for four to six weeks. If there is no improvement, or if symptoms are severe from the start, see a dermatologist for a proper diagnosis. What the AAD Doesn't Address: The Scalp Environment

The AAD's guidelines are excellent at what they cover: identifying the yeast, choosing the right active ingredient, and using it properly. What they do not address is the broader scalp environment that determines whether dandruff keeps coming back after you stop treatment.

Think of it this way. The AAD approach is like treating a weed by spraying herbicide. It works while you keep spraying. But if the soil conditions favor weed growth, the problem returns the moment you stop. For dandruff, the "soil" is your scalp environment, and there are several environmental factors the AAD's guidelines simply do not mention.

Water Quality

About 85% of US households have hard water, meaning high concentrations of calcium and magnesium in the tap. These minerals deposit on the scalp over time, raising its natural pH from the ideal range of 4.5-5.5 to a more alkaline state. Research has shown that an elevated scalp pH creates a more favorable environment for Malassezia proliferation. So while you are using the AAD's recommended ketoconazole shampoo to kill the yeast, your hard water may be creating the exact conditions that help it grow back.

Scalp Microbiome Balance

The AAD frames dandruff exclusively as a Malassezia problem. But the scalp microbiome is a complex ecosystem where hundreds of bacterial and fungal species interact. When the overall microbiome is balanced, Malassezia is held in check by competing organisms. When the microbiome is disrupted, by harsh cleansers, environmental stress, or chronic inflammation, Malassezia loses its natural competitors and proliferates. The AAD's recommended antifungal shampoos are effective at reducing Malassezia, but they can also disrupt the broader microbiome, which is one reason dandruff often returns after people stop using medicated shampoo.

Scalp Barrier Integrity

Many of the AAD's recommended active ingredients, particularly selenium sulfide and salicylic acid, can be drying to the scalp. Over time, regular use without barrier support can compromise the lipid layer that protects your scalp. A compromised barrier leads to increased transepidermal water loss, which triggers inflammation and, paradoxically, can create conditions that favor the very yeast overgrowth you are trying to treat.

Environmental Stressors

UV exposure, air pollution, indoor heating, and seasonal humidity changes all affect the scalp environment in ways that influence dandruff severity. None of these factors appear in the AAD's guidelines. A person living in a dry, high-altitude climate with hard water and heavy air pollution faces a fundamentally different scalp challenge than someone in a humid coastal area with soft water, even if both have the same Malassezia levels.

This is not a criticism of the AAD. Their guidelines are intentionally focused on what can be universally recommended based on clinical evidence. But if you have been following their protocol correctly and dandruff keeps returning, these environmental factors are the most likely reason. When Dandruff Treatment Isn't Working: When to See a Dermatologist

The AAD is clear about when self-treatment should stop and professional evaluation should begin. If you have been using over-the-counter dandruff shampoo correctly, with the five-minute contact time and at the recommended frequency, for four to six weeks without improvement, it is time to see a board-certified dermatologist.

Specifically, the AAD recommends seeking professional care if you experience any of the following:

  • No improvement after 4-6 weeks of consistent OTC treatment - You may need prescription-strength formulations or a different diagnosis entirely.
  • Thick, crusty, or greasy scales - This may indicate seborrheic dermatitis rather than common dandruff, requiring stronger intervention.
  • Significant redness or swelling on the scalp - Inflammation beyond mild irritation suggests a more aggressive form of the condition.
  • Symptoms spreading beyond the scalp - Flaking on the eyebrows, nose, ears, or chest indicates seborrheic dermatitis that benefits from targeted prescription treatment.
  • Hair loss in affected areas - Dandruff itself does not typically cause hair loss. If you are losing hair, another condition may be involved.
  • Severe itching that affects your daily life - A dermatologist can prescribe anti-inflammatory treatments that provide relief beyond what OTC options offer.

A dermatologist can also help distinguish dandruff and seborrheic dermatitis from conditions that look similar but require completely different treatment: scalp psoriasis, contact dermatitis from hair products, fungal infections beyond Malassezia, and even some autoimmune conditions that present with scalp flaking.

The AAD maintains a searchable directory of board-certified dermatologists on their website if you need help finding a provider in your area.

Research Note

Dandruff misdiagnosis is more common than you might think: A study published in the Indian Journal of Dermatology found that dandruff is the most commercially exploited skin disease, with many people self-treating conditions that are not actually dandruff. Scalp psoriasis, contact dermatitis, and dry scalp are frequently mistaken for dandruff, and treating them with antifungal shampoos either has no effect or makes the underlying condition worse.

Frequently Asked Questions

What dandruff shampoo does the AAD recommend?

The American Academy of Dermatology recommends dandruff shampoos containing one of five active ingredients: pyrithione zinc, selenium sulfide, ketoconazole, salicylic acid, or coal tar. The AAD advises trying different active ingredients if the first one you use does not improve symptoms within four to six weeks.

How long should you leave dandruff shampoo on according to the AAD?

The AAD recommends leaving dandruff shampoo on the scalp for approximately five minutes before rinsing. This contact time allows the active ingredients to penetrate the scalp surface and work effectively against the yeast and inflammation that cause dandruff. Most people rinse too quickly, which significantly reduces the treatment's effectiveness.

What is the difference between dandruff and seborrheic dermatitis?

According to the AAD, dandruff and seborrheic dermatitis exist on a spectrum. Dandruff is a milder form limited to the scalp, producing white or yellowish flakes. Seborrheic dermatitis is the more severe form that can cause thick scales, significant redness, and may spread beyond the scalp to the eyebrows, sides of the nose, and ears. Both are caused by the same Malassezia yeast overgrowth.

Can you use AAD-recommended dandruff shampoo on color-treated hair?

The AAD notes that some dandruff shampoo ingredients, particularly selenium sulfide and coal tar, can discolor light or color-treated hair. For color-treated hair, the AAD suggests using pyrithione zinc or ketoconazole-based shampoos, which are generally gentler on hair color. Always follow with a conditioner on the lengths to minimize dryness.

How often should you use dandruff shampoo?

The AAD recommends different frequencies depending on hair type. For Caucasian and Asian hair, shampooing with a dandruff shampoo daily or every other day is generally advised, reducing frequency once symptoms improve. For Black hair, the AAD recommends using dandruff shampoo only once a week, as more frequent washing can cause excessive dryness.

When should you see a dermatologist for dandruff?

The AAD recommends seeing a dermatologist if dandruff does not improve after four to six weeks of consistent over-the-counter treatment, if you develop thick yellow or greasy scales, significant redness or swelling, if symptoms spread beyond the scalp, or if you experience hair loss in affected areas. A dermatologist can prescribe stronger formulations or rule out other conditions like psoriasis.
