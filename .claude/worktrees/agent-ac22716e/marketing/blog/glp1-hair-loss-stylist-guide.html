GLP-1 Hair Loss Is Filling Your Chair: What Every Stylist Needs to Know

Tina Mui March 26, 2026 12 min read
Scope Note: This article is for professional education only. It does not constitute medical advice and is not intended for client distribution. For a client-facing resource on this topic, see our companion article: GLP-1 Medications and Your Hair.

One in eight American adults is now taking a GLP-1 medication. For women aged 50 to 64, the highest-spending salon demographic, that number climbs to one in five. Dermatologists estimate that 25 to 33% of GLP-1 weight loss patients experience noticeable hair shedding. If you are behind the chair, you are already seeing this. Here is what is happening, what you can do about it, and, critically, what you should never say.

The Numbers Behind the Trend

This is not a niche concern. The scale of GLP-1 adoption has turned what was once an occasional clinical observation into a daily salon reality.

According to KFF Health Tracking Poll data from late 2025, approximately 28 million American adults are currently taking a GLP-1 receptor agonist, a category that includes semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound). RAND research from August 2025 found that uptake is highest among women aged 50 to 64, a demographic that also represents the highest per-visit spending in professional salons. One in five women in that age bracket is now on a GLP-1.

Here is the back-of-napkin math. If you see roughly 200 unique female clients per year, somewhere between 25 and 30 of them are likely taking a GLP-1 medication right now. With published clinical trial shedding rates of 3 to 6% and practitioner-reported estimates of 25 to 33%, that translates to 8 to 10 clients who are actively experiencing noticeable hair changes. And "female hair loss" searches are up 125% in 2025, making it the most-searched beauty topic of the year.

Research Note

Clinical trial rates likely undercount real-world shedding. The 3 to 6% figures from pharmaceutical company trials use specific diagnostic criteria. Practitioner estimates consistently run higher. A Cleveland Clinic dermatologist cited 25 to 33% of weight loss patients experiencing visible hair changes. An FDA adverse event analysis found that semaglutide and tirzepatide had the strongest hair loss associations among all GLP-1 receptor agonists, with rates of shedding correlating directly to the amount of weight lost.

Suchonwanit P, et al. "Alopecia associated with the use of semaglutide and tirzepatide: A disproportionality analysis using the FAERS." J Am Acad Dermatol. 2024. PubMed: 38925559

The GLP-1 market is projected to exceed $100 billion by 2030. New indications beyond weight loss (cardiovascular protection, sleep apnea, liver disease) mean the prescribing base will only widen. This is not a trend that peaks and fades. This is a permanent shift in the salon landscape.

What Is Actually Happening

Understanding the mechanism matters because it shapes how you talk to clients, what services you offer, and when you refer. You do not need a dermatology degree for this, but you do need more than "it's the medication."

Telogen Effluvium: The Stress Shed

The type of hair shedding associated with GLP-1 medications is, in most cases, telogen effluvium, a temporary, diffuse shedding that occurs when the body diverts resources away from non-essential functions under physiological stress. Hair follicles are shifted prematurely from anagen (active growth) into telogen (resting), then shed roughly three to four months later.

This is not new biology. Telogen effluvium has been documented for decades after pregnancy, major surgery, high fevers, crash diets, and other metabolic disruptions. GLP-1 medications are the newest and largest-scale trigger, but the underlying process is identical. The shedding is driven primarily by the speed and magnitude of weight loss, not by a direct pharmacological effect of the drug on the follicle.

"In 40 years, I have seen telogen effluvium triggered by pregnancy, surgery, extreme diets, and now GLP-1 medications. The biology is the same. What is different is the scale - this is happening to millions of people at once."

Tina Mui, Trichologist and Co-Founder, AWARE Hair

The Nutritional Spiral

GLP-1 medications work partly by suppressing appetite. Many clients eat significantly less without realizing the nutritional consequences. Hair is 95% keratin, a structural protein, and hair matrix cells divide every 23 to 72 hours, making them among the most metabolically demanding cells in the body. When protein, iron, zinc, vitamin D, and omega-3 fatty acids drop, the hair growth cycle is one of the first things to suffer.

Nutrient Role in Hair Health What to Suggest
Protein Building block of keratin; deficiency directly triggers telogen effluvium "Your doctor can help you make sure you are getting enough protein during weight loss."
Iron Oxygen transport to follicles; most research-linked nutrient for hair health "It might be worth asking your doctor to check your ferritin levels."
Zinc Essential for hair tissue growth and repair "Zinc is one of the nutrients that can drop during calorie changes."
Vitamin D Helps create new follicle growth sites; deficiency is common generally "A lot of people are low in vitamin D. Your doctor can test for it."
Biotin (B7) Supports keratin production; suboptimal levels may increase fragility "B vitamins support hair strength. Your healthcare provider can advise."
Chair-Side Takeaway

You are not a nutritionist. Your role is to normalize the conversation and point the client toward their healthcare provider for nutritional guidance. You can mention that nutritional factors play a role. You should never prescribe specific supplements or dosages.

The Timeline Clients Need to Hear

This is the single most reassuring piece of information you can share with a client, and it is well-supported by clinical data:

  • Onset: 2 to 6 months after starting the medication (aligned with the weight loss period)
  • Peak shedding: 3 to 6 months after onset begins
  • Resolution: Shedding typically subsides as weight stabilizes, even if the client continues the medication
  • Full regrowth: 6 to 12 months after the shedding trigger resolves

The key message: this is almost always temporary and reversible. The vast majority of clients will see their hair return to its previous density. That single fact, delivered calmly and with confidence, is often the most valuable thing you can offer.

Research Note

The tirzepatide paradox. Three published case studies have documented instances where tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound) actually improved hair density in patients with insulin resistance. The proposed mechanism: by reducing insulin resistance, tirzepatide may prevent the follicle miniaturization associated with androgenetic alopecia. The same class of drug can simultaneously trigger telogen effluvium from weight loss while improving underlying pattern loss. This is an evolving area of research, and it underscores why blanket statements about "GLP-1 medications and hair" are unreliable.

Case studies cited in Branyiczky C, et al. "Effects of GLP-1 Receptor Agonists on Hair Loss and Regrowth: A Systematic Review." Int J Dermatol. 2025. DOI: 10.1111/ijd.70133

What You Can Do Behind the Chair

You cannot stop telogen effluvium. It is a systemic process driven by metabolic change. What you can do is support the scalp environment, reduce compounding stressors, manage the client's experience through the shedding phase, and position yourself as the professional they trust during a vulnerable time.

Scalp Support Services

Scalp health services are the natural professional response to GLP-1 hair changes. They are within your scope of practice, they address a real need (dehydration and barrier compromise are common side effects of reduced caloric intake), and they create a recurring revenue opportunity. Our guide on adding scalp treatments to your salon menu covers specific service tiers and pricing. Frame these as wellness support, never as hair loss treatment.

  • Hydrating scalp treatments - dehydration is one of the most common side effects of appetite suppression, and the scalp is directly affected. Treatments with humectants like hyaluronic acid address this at the surface level
  • Gentle exfoliation - support the follicle environment with gentle enzymatic or physical exfoliation. Avoid aggressive detox or deep-cleansing protocols that could further compromise a stressed barrier
  • Scalp massage - evidence supports that regular scalp massage increases blood flow and may support nutrient delivery to follicles. It also reduces client stress, which compounds the cortisol contribution to shedding
  • Progress documentation - before-and-after scalp photos or scalp scope imaging give the client objective evidence of improvement over time, building trust and reinforcing the value of continued treatment

"You don't need to diagnose. You need to recognize, reassure, and support. That is what being behind the chair means."

Tina Mui, Trichologist and Co-Founder, AWARE Hair

Styling Strategies During the Shedding Phase

Your cut and styling choices can make a meaningful difference in how a client feels about their hair during a shedding phase, even when you cannot change the underlying biology:

  • Cuts: Layers over one-length cuts. A chin-length bob creates the illusion of more volume. Internal layering adds movement without sacrificing perimeter density
  • Blow-dry technique: Direct airflow against the natural growth direction at roots. Flip the head upside down during drying. Use a medium round brush for lift at the crown
  • Products: Dry shampoo at the roots for texture and volume. Lightweight volumizing mousse rather than heavy creams. Avoid anything that weighs hair down or adds buildup
  • What to avoid: Tight styles (ponytails, braids, extensions) that add traction stress to already compromised follicles. Excessive heat. Heavy silicone-based products
Chair-Side Takeaway

The "less is more" principle applies. During a shedding phase, reduce mechanical and chemical stress across the board. This is not the time for a major color transformation, aggressive heat styling, or tight protective styles. Simplify the routine and let the scalp recover.

The Take-Home Conversation

When it comes to retail recommendations for clients going through GLP-1-related shedding, restraint is your most powerful tool. Recommend one product, not a regimen. Overwhelm kills compliance. Lead with scalp-focused products rather than hair-focused ones, framing the recommendation as "supporting your scalp during this transition" rather than "fixing your hair loss."

The goal is to bridge from in-salon treatment to at-home maintenance. A single, well-chosen product that the client actually uses consistently will outperform a five-product system that sits untouched on the bathroom shelf.

What You Should Never Say

This is the section that matters most. Scope of practice is not just a legal concept; it is the line between being a trusted professional and being a liability. Every statement below has been heard in real salons. Each one crosses a boundary that can damage your credibility, your client relationship, or both.

Never Say Say Instead
"Ozempic is causing your hair loss." "Some clients going through weight changes notice differences in their hair texture and thickness. It is really common right now."
"You have telogen effluvium." "What you are describing sounds like something your dermatologist would call telogen effluvium. It would be worth mentioning at your next appointment."
"You need to take biotin" or "You need more iron." "A lot of people find it helpful to talk to their doctor about nutrient levels during weight changes. There are some simple blood tests that can check."
"This product will regrow your hair." "This supports a healthy scalp environment, which is the foundation for healthy hair growth."
"Maybe you should lower your dose" or "Have you thought about stopping?" "Your prescriber would be the best person to talk to about how your medication might be related to what you are seeing with your hair."
"I've seen a lot of people lose hair on that medication." "Changes in hair can happen during any significant body change. The good news is it is usually temporary."
"You should try [specific supplement brand]." "There is some great research on nutrients that support hair. Your doctor or a dietitian can help you figure out if anything would help."
Chair-Side Takeaway

The pattern is simple. You can describe what you observe. You can normalize the experience. You can support the scalp. You cannot diagnose, prescribe, or advise on medication. When in doubt, redirect to the client's healthcare provider. This is not a limitation; it is what makes your support credible.

When to Refer

Recognizing when a situation exceeds your scope is one of the most professional things you can do. Refer the client to a dermatologist or trichologist if:

  • Shedding continues beyond 6 months without any sign of improvement
  • Loss is patchy rather than diffuse, which could indicate alopecia areata or another condition
  • The scalp shows scarring, unusual inflammation, or visible lesions
  • The client is in significant emotional distress about their hair changes
  • Shedding began before the client started weight loss medication

A referral is not a failure. It is evidence that you understand the boundaries of your role and care enough about the client to ensure they get the right level of support.

The Business Opportunity

There is a real, recurring revenue opportunity in GLP-1-related scalp wellness, but it requires sensitivity in how you build, market, and deliver these services. The stylists who get this right will differentiate themselves in a market where most salons have no answer at all.

Building a Service Around This

Consider a tiered scalp wellness offering that gives clients options based on their needs and budget:

Service Tier What It Includes Price Range
Single Treatment Scalp analysis, hydrating treatment, styling consultation $85 - $150
3-Visit Series Initial assessment, monthly scalp treatments, progress tracking $225 - $400
Ongoing Wellness Monthly treatment, take-home product, quarterly scalp check-in $150 - $200/month

Name the service around scalp wellness, not weight loss. "Scalp Renewal Treatment" or "Scalp Wellness Consultation" works. "Ozempic Hair Loss Package" does not. The framing should be broad enough that any client with scalp concerns, not just GLP-1 users, can engage with the service without feeling singled out.

Marketing It Sensitively

This is where most businesses will stumble, and where you can distinguish yourself by getting it right:

  • Lead with "scalp health," never with "weight loss hair loss." Your marketing should focus on what you do (support scalp wellness), not what the client is going through (medication-related shedding)
  • Never initiate the conversation about weight or medication. Let the client bring it up. Many will, proactively. When they do, meet them with calm expertise
  • Provide a private consultation space for thinning conversations. These are vulnerable moments. A crowded salon floor is not the right environment
  • Create take-home materials that clients can read privately. A one-page guide on what to expect during a shedding phase, with your salon's branding, gives the client a resource and positions you as the expert
  • Frame as empowerment: "There is a lot we can do to support your scalp during this transition." The emphasis is on agency and action, not on the problem

Frequently Asked Questions

Do GLP-1 medications directly cause hair loss?

Current evidence suggests the shedding is primarily telogen effluvium triggered by rapid weight loss and nutritional changes, not a direct pharmacological effect of the medication on the hair follicle. Clinical trials for semaglutide (Wegovy) reported hair loss in roughly 3% of participants versus 1% on placebo. For tirzepatide, rates reached 5 to 6% at higher doses. Crucially, shedding rates correlate with the amount of weight lost, not the medication dose alone.

How many of my clients might be affected?

With approximately 28 million American adults currently on GLP-1 medications, and adoption rates highest in your core demographic (women aged 50 to 64), the math is straightforward. For every 200 unique female clients you see per year, roughly 25 to 30 are likely on a GLP-1. Of those, 8 to 10 may experience noticeable shedding. These numbers will continue to grow as prescriptions increase.

Is GLP-1-related hair shedding permanent?

In the vast majority of cases, no. Telogen effluvium is self-resolving. Once the body adjusts to its new weight and nutritional intake stabilizes, hair follicles return to their normal growth cycle. Most clients notice regrowth beginning within 3 to 6 months after the shedding trigger resolves, with full density restoration taking up to a year.

What is the best scalp treatment for clients on weight loss medications?

Focus on hydrating, gentle treatments that support the scalp environment. Hydrating treatments with humectants like hyaluronic acid address the dehydration that accompanies appetite suppression. Gentle scalp massage supports blood flow and nutrient delivery. Barrier-supportive products help maintain the scalp's protective lipid layer during nutritional stress. Frame all services as scalp wellness support, not hair loss treatment.

How do I bring up hair changes without making my client uncomfortable?

The simplest approach: do not initiate. Let the client bring it up. Many will. When they do, normalize the experience immediately: "A lot of clients going through body changes have asked me about this. It is really common, and there is a lot we can do to support your scalp." This validates their concern without making assumptions about their health decisions or medication choices.
