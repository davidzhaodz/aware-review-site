AWARE Hair 4R Protocol Quick Reference

The 4R Scalpcare Protocol

A sequential system for resetting the follicular microenvironment. The order matters.

Step 1

Reset (Purify)

9:1 Professional Shampoo
  • Focus on scalp, not lengths
  • Double cleanse: first lifts, second treats
  • Fingertip pads, never nails
  • Warm water, not hot
  • Sulfate-free transition: 2-4 weeks
Step 2

Repair (Restore)

9:1 Professional Scalp Mask
  • Hyaluronic acid: 1,000x water retention
  • Green tea EGCG: antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory
  • Supports microbiome balance
  • Scalp mask 1-2x/week for intensive repair
  • Consistency over product cycling
Step 3

Reinforce (Protect)

9:1 Professional Leave-In
  • UV protection for scalp and hair
  • Barrier defense against pollution
  • Hard water mineral protection
  • Change part occasionally
  • Loose styles over tight ponytails
Step 4

Reactivate (Stimulate)

Sandalwood Scalp Gua Sha
  • 3-5x per week, 3-5 minutes
  • 4 meridian points: hairline, crown, temples, nape
  • Light to medium pressure
  • Move the skin, not just the hair
  • 4x micro-circulation increase

Daily Routine

Wash Days (2-3x/Week)

Double cleanse with shampoo (Reset). Apply scalp mask to scalp, conditioner through lengths (Repair). Rinse, apply leave-in to scalp and lengths (Reinforce).

Daily (3-5x/Week)

Gua sha for 3-5 minutes using the 4-step sequence (Reactivate). Best before washing or as part of evening routine. The most important thing is consistency.

AWARE Hair Scalp Symptom Reference Guide

Scalp Symptoms at the Chair

What you'll see, what's happening, and your role. This guide is for observation, not diagnosis.

What You See What's Happening Your Action
Dandruff / Flaking
White or yellowish flakes on the scalp and hair. May be dry and powdery or oily and clumped. Client may mention itching. Persistent despite washing.
Could be simple dryness, product buildup, or seborrheic dermatitis (yeast-driven). Oily yellow flakes with redness at the hairline suggest seb derm rather than simple dryness.
"I'm noticing some flaking at your hairline." Consistent gentle cleansing helps mild cases. Refer to derm if persistent, oily, or accompanied by significant redness.
Redness / Sensitivity After Services
Localized redness, itching, or burning where products were applied. Appears after color, bleach, relaxer, or a new product. Client may report heat or tightness.
Could be a simple irritant reaction (dose-dependent) or an allergic response (immune-mediated - PPD in hair dye is a common trigger). Can appear hours to days after exposure.
Document exactly what was used and when the reaction appeared. Critical for future services. Introduce patch testing. Refer if reaction is severe, spreading, or recurring.
Thick Scaling / Plaques
Thick, silvery-white scales with sharply defined borders. Often extends beyond the hairline onto the forehead and neck. Differs from dandruff: thicker, more adherent, well-demarcated.
May indicate scalp psoriasis (autoimmune). Not contagious and not caused by hygiene. The thicker, more silvery appearance and sharp borders distinguish it from seborrheic dermatitis.
Be gentle. Never scrape or pick at affected areas. Refer to a dermatologist if not already under care. This is a case to refer - not to treat at the chair.
Bumps / Tenderness
Small red bumps or pustules around follicle openings. Looks like tiny pimples on the scalp. Client may report tenderness, soreness, or "bumps" when washing.
Could be bacterial or fungal folliculitis, or from occlusion (tight braids, heavy products, sweating under headwear). Blocked follicle openings create an infection opportunity.
Avoid tight styling over affected areas. If recurring in extension or braid clients, discuss traction load. Maintain sanitation protocols. Refer if widespread or recurring.
Shedding / Thinning
Excess hair in the shampoo bowl, on the floor, or caught in the brush. Wider part over time. Finer hairs at the crown. Client may mention noticing more in the drain.
Many possible drivers: stress, hormones, nutrition, styling tension, or genetic pattern. Traction shedding (from ponytails, braids, extensions) can be reversed if caught early. Progressive miniaturization is not reversible without medical intervention.
Document what you see visit to visit. "I'm noticing your part looks a little wider than last time." Ask about recent life changes. Recommend early dermatologist evaluation - the sooner the better.

5 Questions to Ask Your Client

1. What products are you using at home?

4. Are you exercising regularly?

2. Have you been under a lot of stress recently?

5. Has anything changed recently? (medications, products, routine)

3. How has your diet been?

Remember: observe, note, communicate. "I'm noticing X, have you noticed that?" Your role is to be the first to see it, not the one to diagnose it.

AWARE Hair Sandalwood Gua Sha Technique

The 4-Step Gua Sha Technique

Move the scalp tissue, not just the hair. Light to medium pressure. 8-10 strokes per area. 3-5 minutes total.

Step 1

Hairline

GV24 (Shenting) - "Spirit Court"

Start at the center of the forehead hairline. Broad, slow strokes moving front to back toward the crown. 8-10 strokes. Releases the frontalis muscle and opens circulation from the face into the scalp.

Step 2

Crown

GV20 (Baihui) - "Hundred Meetings"

Move to the crown. Circular motions, or radial strokes outward from the center point. 8-10 strokes per direction. The galea is thickest here - where tension accumulates most.

Step 3

Temples

GB8 (Shuaigu) - "Leading Valley"

Broad strokes from temples upward toward crown. Also small circles on the temple. 8-10 strokes each side. Where jaw tension meets the scalp via the temporalis and its fascial connection.

Step 4

Nape

GB20 (Fengchi) - "Wind Pool"

Strokes from nape upward toward crown, and outward along the base of the skull. 8-10 strokes. The occipital region connects to the neck and shoulders where tension originates.

For Your Clients at Home

3-5x per week, 3-5 minutes. Best before washing or as evening routine. Light pressure. Wipe with soft cloth after use. Store in pouch away from moisture. Sandalwood is naturally antimicrobial.

For Your Service Menu

Use during the Repair step after scalp mask is applied. Same 4-step technique, standing behind the chair. The gua sha helps botanicals absorb while stimulating circulation. Sanitize between clients per standard protocol.

Why Sandalwood?

Naturally smooth grain glides without catching hair. Antimicrobial - stays cleaner between uses. Warm to the touch - won't shock a sensitive scalp like jade or metal. Traditional gua sha material for centuries.

Evidence: Gua sha produces a 4x increase in surface micro-perfusion (Nielsen et al., 2007) and upregulates heme oxygenase-1 antioxidant enzyme (Kwong et al., 2009).

Page 4 of 5 - When to Refer

AWARE Hair Medical Referral Checklist

When to Recommend Medical Follow-Up

Knowing when to refer is as valuable as knowing how to care. It protects your client and protects you.

Refer Promptly
  • Sudden patchy hair loss - could indicate alopecia areata (autoimmune)
  • Pain, burning, or tenderness at the scalp without recent chemical service
  • Scarring or smooth, shiny patches where follicles appear absent (scarring alopecia)
  • Pustules, crusting, or non-healing sores - possible infection requiring treatment
  • Scalp lesions that look different from surrounding scalp or are changing in appearance
Monitor and Refer if Persistent (4+ Weeks)
  • Chronic flaking that doesn't respond to 4-6 weeks of gentle, consistent cleansing
  • Progressive thinning that's accelerating visit to visit
  • Persistent redness that doesn't correlate with products used or services performed
  • Itching that disrupts daily life or is keeping the client awake at night

How to Have the Conversation

The Observation Approach

"I've noticed some changes at your hairline over the last few visits. It's probably nothing, but I'd feel better if you had a dermatologist take a look. You want someone who can rule out anything medical while we keep up the scalp care routine."

The Partnership Approach

"What you're describing sounds like it might benefit from a dermatologist's input. I can keep supporting your scalp health from the salon side, but the medical piece needs a doctor. Think of it as a team effort."

Ground Rules

Do

Describe what you observe. Use "I'm noticing..." language. Position referral as additive. Document changes visit to visit.

Don't

Diagnose conditions. Alarm or scare. Say "you have alopecia." Compare to other clients. Recommend stopping medications.

How to Approach Medicated Products

Try the natural approach first. Start with consistent, gentle cleansing and the 4R routine - many clients see real improvement before they need anything stronger.

If a doctor has already prescribed something, keep using it. Don't suggest stopping or replacing a prescription. Instead, cycle in gentler maintenance products on alternate days.

One important note: medicated shampoos can be very drying. Clients on these products often need extra attention to moisture and barrier support - that's exactly where the AWARE routine fills the gap.

When a Client Asks About Head & Shoulders

"Zinc pyrithione targets a specific yeast that causes dandruff - it's a good tool for that problem. I'd still try a gentle routine first and see how your scalp responds. If the flaking doesn't clear up, then layer in the medicated option."

When a Client Is on Prescription Shampoo

"Keep using what your doctor prescribed - that's targeting a specific condition. On the days between uses, a gentler routine helps maintain the overall environment. Medicated shampoos can be drying, so the scalp mask is a good complement."
