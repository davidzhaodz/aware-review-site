Strategic Decisions

What Changed from V2

Based on 3-agent LinkedIn trend research + 2-agent critique session. The core insight: the bottleneck was never content quality. It was execution.

Resequenced

Salon Math Leads, AI Follows

The most differentiated content (B2B beauty unit economics) launches first. AI system introduced after the audience knows who David is. "9 AI Employees" moves to EP5 as the payoff.

Reframed

Problem-First, Not Framework-First

Decision Audit angle rejected as consultant content. Every post now leads with a visceral problem the reader recognizes in their gut. The framework is earned, not assumed.

New Pillars

Tina's Conviction + Category Creation

Critics identified two blind spots: no co-founder content (40 years of trichology credibility) and no scalp-as-category positioning. Both added as recurring pillars.

Killed

"What I'd Stop Doing" + 8-Episode Arc

Generic founder advice series killed (oversaturated, any founder could post it). AI ops trimmed from 8 to 5 episodes - unproven cadence doesn't justify 8 weeks.

Hybrid Audience

Every post serves two audiences simultaneously

Audience A (beauty press, investors, distributors): sees category authority, distribution integrity, and business proof. Audience B (founders, operators): sees relatable constraints, transferable strategy, and honest numbers. The beauty context is the differentiator - without it, David is just another AI-ops founder.

01

The Stylist Math

Tuesday Apr 8 · Salon Math
I chose B2B before DTC. Every piece of advice I got said to do it the other way. Build the Instagram following first. Run Meta ads. Find an influencer. Build the DTC flywheel. Instead, I drove to salons with a sample kit. Here's what I keep coming back to: one stylist who believes in your product is worth more than 10,000 Instagram impressions. A stylist sees 200 clients a month. She touches their hair. She answers their questions. When she says "try this," they trust her, because she's the one who fixed their color when the last salon ruined it. That recommendation is earned, not bought. You can't A/B test your way into it. You can't scale it with ad spend. Six months in, we have 50+ salon partners. 96% of them reorder. Zero paid ads. The math is slower. The compounding is different. But the retention is real. The unsexy path is just quiet on LinkedIn.

02

The Reorder Rate

Tuesday Apr 15 · Salon Math
96% of our salon partners reorder. I've been thinking about why that number is so high, and I keep coming back to one thing: exclusivity. When a client can only get the product through their stylist, the stylist's recommendation carries real weight. The client comes back to the salon to repurchase. The stylist builds trust. The salon builds revenue. When that same product shows up on Amazon for $3 less, the whole dynamic breaks. The stylist stops recommending it. The client stops asking. The product just sits on the shelf. We've seen it happen to brands we respect. We don't plan to let it happen to us. Not every brand can make that commitment. Some need the volume that open distribution provides. I get it. But if your model depends on your salon partners being the reason clients come back, then protecting that relationship is the strategy. Everything else follows from it.

03

Distribution Integrity

Thursday Apr 17 · Contrarian Bets
Every brand says "salon-exclusive." Most of them don't mean it. Here is what happens when a professional brand expands into open distribution: Your clients find it online for less. They stop buying at the chair. The margin that funded your education, your backbar, your ability to offer something differentiated - it erodes. Slowly, then all at once. Salon-exclusive is not a marketing phrase. It is a supply chain decision. It means the brand chooses not to pursue the faster revenue that comes from mass retail and open distributor networks. It means accepting slower growth in exchange for protecting the people who built the brand in the first place. At AWARE Hair, every product is sold through professional partners. Not on Amazon. Not through open distribution. Not anywhere a client can purchase it without their stylist. That is not a launch strategy. It is a permanent commitment. The salons that thrive long-term are the ones that align with brands making the same commitment. The ones that treated exclusivity as a marketing phase are the ones whose partners are now competing with their own retail shelves.

04

The Daily Tradeoff

Tuesday Apr 22 · Founder Reality
I can either serve my existing 50 salons or find new ones. I've never been able to do both on the same day. Every morning is a version of the same decision. Do I follow up with the salon that hasn't reordered in 45 days? Or do I reach out to the three new prospects I found yesterday? Both matter. Both are urgent. Both have real consequences if they don't get done. I used to try to do both. Check Shopify for reorder gaps in the morning. Send outreach emails in the afternoon. Update the pipeline spreadsheet at night. What actually happened: I'd get halfway through the reorder check, see something that needed a follow-up call, spend 40 minutes on the phone, and by then the outreach window was gone. This is not a time management problem. This is a structural problem. One person cannot do two full-time jobs in the same day. The answer, for me, was building systems that handle one of those jobs so I can focus on the other. But that's a story for another post. For now, I'm curious: if you run a small B2B operation, how do you split time between retention and acquisition?

05

Tina's Rule

Thursday Apr 24 · Tina's Conviction
My co-founder made one rule before we launched. "The product never goes anywhere a client can buy it without their stylist." Tina has been in salons for over 40 years. Stylist. Trichologist. She's watched what happens when brands go mass market. The stylist stops recommending them because the client already bought it online for less. The salon loses the retail conversation entirely. When we were building AWARE, someone suggested we sell on Amazon right away. "That's where the volume is." Tina said no. Not "not yet." Just no. That decision has cost us revenue in the short term. We turned down opportunities that would have doubled our sales overnight. We've had distributors tell us we're leaving money on the table. But our salon partners reorder at 96%. I think those two things are connected. When a stylist recommends AWARE to a client, that recommendation means something. The client can't go home and find it cheaper online. The only way to get it is to come back to the salon. That's not a limitation. That's the whole point.

06

Zero Ad Spend

Tuesday Apr 29 · Salon Math
We have never run a paid ad. We have also never had a salon ask for a discount. I think those two things are connected. When you acquire salon partners through paid channels, the relationship starts with a transaction. They found you because of an ad, and the implicit question is: what's the deal? When you acquire salon partners through a handshake, a sample kit, and a conversation about what their clients actually need, the relationship starts with trust. Every salon we work with came to us through one of three channels: I drove there, a stylist referred us, or they found us through the Scalp Environment Report on our website. Zero of them came from a paid ad. And because the relationship didn't start with a discount, it doesn't depend on one. Six months in: 50+ salon partners. $0 in ad spend. 96% reorder rate. The math is different when your acquisition channel is trust.

07

The Category Moat

Thursday May 1 · Category Creation
Scalp care is not haircare. That distinction is our entire moat. Your scalp is skin. It has its own microbiome, its own sebum production cycle, its own relationship with the environment around it. UV radiation at the part line. Hard water mineral deposits. Humidity shifts between seasons. Air quality settling on the follicle. None of this is addressed by a hair-focused product. The category shift is already happening. Scalp services are among the fastest-growing line items in professional salons. Trichology consultations, once obscure, are being requested by clients who are connecting their hair outcomes to scalp health for the first time. The brands and salons that understand this distinction are building a real moat. The ones that add a "scalp shampoo" to a haircare line are not. We built AWARE specifically because this distinction matters. Every formulation decision starts from scalp physiology, not from hair fiber chemistry. If you are in the professional beauty industry and are not thinking about scalp as a separate service category, this is the conversation to have in 2026.

Reference

Voice Rules

Always

  • Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences)
  • Contractions (we're, I'm, doesn't)
  • Specific numbers over vague claims
  • Educational first, sell second
  • Links in first comment, not body
  • Max 3 hashtags, in first comment
  • Respond to comments for 90 min

Never

  • Em dashes (use commas or dashes)
  • Emojis of any kind
  • AI phrases: leverage, streamline, delve
  • Exclamation marks in post body
  • "Excited to share" / "Here's what I learned"
  • Medical claims about products
  • Amazon, Sephora, or mass retail suggestions
