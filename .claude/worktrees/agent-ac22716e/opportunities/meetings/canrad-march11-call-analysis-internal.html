Language Red Flags - Never Say These Again
1
"They're not poor"
"Our clients, they're not poor, of course, but they are not like Beverly Hills clients"
Why this is damaging

Frames your customer base in terms of what they lack. Kenny is trying to figure out if this product works for his salons. Hearing "not poor" makes him think "so maybe too expensive for my salons." It's also dismissive of the exact customer CanRad serves. His entire portfolio is this demographic. You just told him his clients might not be good enough.

Say instead
"Our Pasadena clients are professionals, families - they're spending on quality, not luxury labels. They reorder because they feel the difference."
2
"Normal people" / "just normal salons"
"All the rebuys are from just normal people"
"Now we're finding a lot of just normal salons"
Why this is damaging

Creates a hierarchy where Beverly Hills is "real" success and everything else is "normal." Kenny's entire network is "normal." This accidentally tells him his salons are the lesser tier - the afterthought, not the target. That's the opposite message we need to send the guy who controls access to 10,000 salons.

Say instead
"Our strongest reorder rates are actually from salons outside Beverly Hills - Pasadena, independent suites, mid-market salons. That's where the product really proves itself."
3
"I don't want to claim we're the best"
"I'm not saying we're, I don't want to claim we're the best, but of course to us, we feel we're the best"
Why this is damaging

Hedging undermines confidence. Kenny is deciding whether to bet on this brand. He needs conviction, not qualifications. The sentence also contradicts itself in real-time - "I don't want to claim" followed immediately by "we feel we're the best." It sounds uncertain, like someone who isn't sure of their own product.

Say instead
Nothing. Just describe what the product does. Confidence doesn't need disclaimers.
4
"No way you could sell a $50 bottle of shampoo"
"If you are charging less than $50 in a haircut, there's no way you could sell a $50 bottle of shampoo, even though some has"
Why this is damaging

Kenny's portfolio includes salons at every price point. You just told him a chunk of his network can't sell your product. Then immediately contradicted yourself with "even though some has." Now you've both narrowed the market AND undercut your own rule. He's left thinking: so which is it?

Say instead
"The salons that do best with us are the ones investing in the client experience - typically $60+ services. But we also have suite stylists and independents who do well because they're building personal relationships with their clients."
5
"How much is your health worth?"
"It doesn't become the price. It's like how much is your health worth?"
Why this is damaging

Sounds defensive about pricing. Kenny is asking a legitimate business question about sell-through economics. Responding with a philosophical question about health doesn't address his concern - it dismisses it. It can also read as condescending, like you're suggesting he doesn't understand the product's value. He's the president of a $40-50M distribution company. He understands value. He's asking about his customers.

Say instead
Nothing. Let David handle all pricing framing. This is his lane entirely.
6
"I can't even start with all the stories"
"I mean, I can't even start with all the stories that I've gotten"
Why this is damaging

A tease with no payoff. You just claimed you have incredible stories, then didn't tell a single one. This wastes time and sounds like filler. Worse, it trains the listener to stop expecting substance after claims.

Say instead
Pick ONE story. "One of our stylists in Pasadena - after three months, her client's dermatologist asked what changed. That's the kind of feedback we get." Specific, short, lands.

Phrase Count Problem
"I don't know" 4 You do know. This signals you're unsure of your own expertise.
"Kind of" / "that's kind of" 5 Softens every statement it touches. "That's kind of what we did" vs. "That's what we did."
"I mean" 6 Verbal throat-clearing. Adds nothing. Just say the thing.

Structural Problems Beyond Word Choice

1. No Awareness of the Room

Kenny said "I'm short on time, I have a hard stop at 4:00" at 26:45. By that point, Tina had already consumed 10+ minutes. The pricing conversation, next steps, and terms discussion - the things that actually move this deal forward - never happened. We ran out of time because we spent it on storytelling.

2. Answered Questions Nobody Asked

Stuart said "we'd love to hear about how all that's going and see where the next steps are." Tina responded with her full origin story, personal philosophy, and career history. That wasn't the question. Stuart was asking about sales momentum and logistics. He got a 6-minute monologue about passion.

3. Stepped on Jade's Story

"I don't know, we should share the story about Jade, but that was that final push..." - then told a vague version herself instead of letting Jade tell her own story with the emotional impact that only she can deliver. Jade never got to reclaim it. This robbed the call of what would have been its most emotionally compelling moment.

4. Undermined David's Positioning at the Worst Moment

Kenny's pricing question was THE moment of the call. His concern: "is this too expensive for our market?" David was building toward "this is premium and that's why it works." Tina jumped in with "they're not poor" and "just normal people" and "how much is your health worth" - all of which either undercut the premium framing or sounded defensive. This is the single most damaging moment of the call.

5. Self-Contradictions in Real Time

"I don't want to claim we're the best, but of course we feel we're the best" - pick one. "There's no way you could sell a $50 bottle of shampoo... even though some has" - now you've undercut your own rule. These aren't small things. A distributor president is evaluating whether this brand has its messaging together. Contradictions in the same sentence say it doesn't.

6. Cosmoprof Awards Fumble

"Having our peers vote us as the top 10 brands was a so, I don't know, what is that word, very rewarding." If you're citing awards in a business meeting, know the details. The name of the award, who gave it, what it was for. Fumbling through it makes it sound less credible, not more. Either prepare the citation or skip it entirely.

The Uncomfortable Truth

What's Really Going On

Tina is the heart of this brand and the reason CanRad is interested. Stuart said the founder story is what hooked them. That's real and irreplaceable.

But on this call, the storytelling actively hurt us. We ran out of time before getting to business. Kenny's core concern was partially addressed at best. The premium positioning was undercut by language choices. Jade's contribution was compressed and her personal story was taken from her.

The issue isn't passion. The issue is that Tina doesn't distinguish between a salon consultation (where she has 30+ minutes of 1-on-1 time with someone who's already sitting in her chair) and a business meeting with executives (where every minute is a resource and nobody owes you their attention). These are fundamentally different contexts that require different communication styles.

If this isn't addressed before the next call, the same thing will happen again. The playbook document gives you a diplomatic way to structure this. Use the 10-minute pre-call alignment to walk through roles and the call flow. If Tina starts to drift during the actual call, use the transition phrases to redirect.

The worst case scenario isn't that Tina feels constrained. It's that Kenny runs out of time again and the deal stalls because we never got to terms.
