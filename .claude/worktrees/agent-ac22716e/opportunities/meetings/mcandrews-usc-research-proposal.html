Executive Summary

The scalp microbiome is a growing area of research where our understanding is still limited, and the studies that exist overwhelmingly focus on Caucasian and East Asian populations. There is very little published data on people with darker skin tones and textured hair. At the same time, nearly all intervention studies test pharmaceutical-grade actives against homogeneous participant pools, leaving real gaps in both ingredient science and population diversity. This is not just a research curiosity - it limits what dermatologists and trichologists can confidently recommend to an increasingly diverse patient base.

This proposal outlines a 12-week randomized controlled trial examining the effects of a plant-based scalp formulation on microbiome composition, barrier integrity, and hydration across diverse hair types and ethnicities. The study addresses at least five identifiable gaps in the current literature and offers publication potential across multiple manuscripts from a single dataset. It also creates structured opportunities for medical student scholarly projects, summer research fellowships, and clinical translational research theses.

AWARE Hair LLC, a professional scalp care company based in Los Angeles, proposes to provide all product supply and contribute partial funding toward study costs. We are also interested in exploring collaborative funding models, including departmental research support, NIH R21/R03 mechanisms, or pilot grant programs that align with the study's focus on health equity and microbiome science. AWARE commits to supporting publication regardless of outcome.

Scientific Rationale

The proposed study is grounded in five specific gaps in the scalp health literature. Each represents a question that has either not been asked or not been adequately answered in published research to date.

Gap 1
Diversity Deficit in Scalp Microbiome Research
Existing scalp microbiome studies have been "mostly limited to US populations with little or no indication of the ethnic background of the studied subjects" (Perez Perez et al., 2016). No comprehensive study stratifies results by hair texture, curl pattern, or self-identified ethnicity beyond basic skin type classification. This limits how useful these findings are for the broader population and leaves dermatologists without evidence-based guidance for patients with textured hair. Los Angeles's demographic diversity and the department's documented interest in healthcare disparities position this study to fill a gap that most institutions cannot.
Cf. Perez Perez et al., 2016 [1] - investigation of skin microbiota across six ethnic groups found prior literature lacked ethnic background reporting.
Gap 2
Plant-Based vs. Pharmaceutical Actives
Nearly all published scalp microbiome intervention studies test pharmaceutical-grade ingredients such as zinc pyrithione and ketoconazole. Only one pilot study (2025/2026, n=40, Ziziphus bark extract) has examined a plant-based formulation's effect on scalp microbial communities [2]. The growing consumer preference for "clean" formulations has outpaced the clinical evidence base, creating a disconnect between what patients want and what dermatologists can confidently recommend.
Cf. Polak-Witka et al., 2020 [3]; Lee et al., 2025 (Plantago asiatica, MDPI Cosmetics) [4].
Gap 3
Microalgae (Isochrysis galbana) - No Independent Scalp Health Data
Isochrysis galbana, a Tahitian microalgae, has demonstrated anti-inflammatory glycolipid activity (de Los Reyes et al., 2016) and antibacterial activity against C. acnes and S. epidermidis in culture (Abdoul-Latif et al., 2021). A supplier-conducted clinical study (n=30, 3 months, phototrichogram) showed a 10.5% increase in anagen phase vs. placebo, and a separate ex-vivo study showed 46% growth stimulation on excised scalp tissue. However, no independent peer-reviewed study has examined this ingredient's effects on scalp microbiome, barrier function, or hydration. The existing clinical data covers hair cycle endpoints only - the proposed study would generate the first data connecting microalgae application to broader scalp health parameters.
Cf. de Los Reyes et al., 2016 (I. galbana glycolipid anti-inflammatory activity) [5]; Abdoul-Latif et al., 2021 (I. galbana antibacterial activity vs. C. acnes and S. epidermidis) [5b]; Data on file, Symrise (clinical hair cycle study, n=30; ex-vivo hair elongation) [6].
Gap 4
Dual-Weight Hyaluronic Acid on Scalp Skin
Hyaluronic acid has been extensively studied on facial skin and in one injectable scalp application, but no published study examines topically applied dual-weight HA on scalp barrier function or hydration. Facial skin research demonstrates that different molecular weights of HA produce distinct effects: low-MW HA (20-300 kDa) penetrates the stratum corneum, while high-MW HA (>1000 kDa) does not (Essendoubi et al., 2016), and lower-MW formulations show superior hydration and wrinkle-depth outcomes (Pavicic et al., 2011). Whether those findings translate to scalp skin, which exhibits functionally distinct barrier properties, different corneocyte turnover rates, and unique sebaceous activity compared to facial skin (O'Goshi et al., 2000), remains untested.
Cf. Pavicic et al., 2011 (HA molecular weight and skin hydration) [7]; Essendoubi et al., 2016 (HA penetration by molecular weight) [8]; O'Goshi et al., 2000 (scalp stratum corneum distinct from facial skin) [8b].
Gap 5
Vegan Keratin Amino Acids and Scalp Outcomes
Published research on keratin-derived peptides and amino acids has focused on hair fiber properties - tensile strength, cuticle integrity, breakage resistance, and moisture retention in the shaft (Malinauskyte et al., 2021; Fan et al., 2025). No study connects hydrolyzed keratin amino acids to scalp-level outcomes such as barrier integrity, epidermal hydration, or microbiome modulation. This is a meaningful omission given that scalp health directly influences the hair growth environment.
Cf. Malinauskyte et al., 2021 (keratin peptide penetration and tensile effects on hair fiber) [9]; Fan et al., 2025 (hydrolyzed keratin cuticle repair and photoprotection) [10].
Why AWARE's Formulation

Our formulation uses several ingredients with supplier-level efficacy data but no published human scalp validation. This study would provide the independent data that currently does not exist. The in-vitro evidence is promising, but until it is tested in a controlled human study, it remains supplier data rather than peer-reviewed evidence.

Full supplier technical dossiers for SymHair Force 1631 (Symrise) and ResistHyal (Givaudan), including raw in-vitro data, are available for PI review upon request under NDA.

Ingredient A - Addresses Gap 3
SymHair Force 1631 (Tahitian Microalgae)
Supplier: Symrise  |  Active: Isochrysis galbana extract
Existing supplier data: 82% improvement in breakage resistance (hair fiber), 10.5% increase in anagen phase vs. placebo (clinical, n=30, 3 months), 46% growth stimulation (ex-vivo scalp tissue).
The gap: The supplier clinical study measured hair cycle endpoints only. No study, supplier or independent, has examined this ingredient's effects on scalp microbiome, barrier function, or hydration. This study would generate that data under independent, peer-reviewed conditions.
Data on file, Symrise [6].
Ingredient B - Addresses Gap 4
ResistHyal (Dual-Weight Hyaluronic Acid)
Supplier: Givaudan  |  Active: High-MW + low-MW sodium hyaluronate
Existing supplier data: 189x more hydration vs. placebo ex-vivo, pollution barrier effect on skin models.
The gap: No published topical scalp studies. Facial HA research is robust, but scalp application remains completely untested in the literature.
Data on file, Givaudan [11].
Ingredient C - Addresses Gap 5
Supporting Actives (Vegan Keratin, Green Tea, Turmeric, Beetroot)
Actives: Hydrolyzed keratin amino acids, Camellia sinensis extract, Curcuma longa extract, Beta vulgaris extract
The gap: No scalp-level outcome data for plant-derived keratin. Published keratin research demonstrates effects on hair fiber mechanical properties but not on scalp skin itself. Green tea, turmeric, and beetroot have published anti-inflammatory and microcirculation evidence, but none is scalp-specific. This study would be the first to measure their combined effect on scalp barrier and microbiome parameters.
Cf. Malinauskyte et al., 2021 [9]; Fan et al., 2025 [10].
Proposed Study Design
Design Randomized controlled, single-blind, parallel group
Duration 12 weeks active + 2-week washout period
Assessments Baseline, Week 4, Week 8, Week 12
Sample 40-60 participants, stratified by hair type and self-identified ethnicity
Population Adults 25-55 with self-reported scalp concerns, diverse skin types with intentional oversampling of darker skin tones and textured hair
Control Matched 3-product commercial regimen (shampoo, conditioner, leave-in) without the actives under investigation. Final comparator selection is deferred to the PI to ensure matched product count, application frequency, and base formulation similarity. AWARE can provide an inert-base version of its products if the PI prefers a vehicle-controlled design.
Intervention Full AWARE 3-product regimen (shampoo, conditioner, leave-in treatment)
Co-Primary Endpoints 16S rRNA sequencing (microbiome alpha diversity and composition), corneometry (scalp hydration). Primary hypothesis: the AWARE regimen will produce measurable changes in scalp microbiome alpha diversity and scalp hydration relative to control at 12 weeks.
Secondary TEWL (barrier function), sebumometry, validated patient-reported outcome questionnaire
Exploratory Standardized clinical photography, phototrichogram (hair density and thickness)
Safety Monitoring Adverse event documentation at each study visit per standard dermatology research protocols. Safety monitoring structure (independent DSMB or PI-led review) to be determined with the Principal Investigator and IRB.
Note on sample size: The proposed range of 40-60 participants is based on published precedent in comparable cosmetic scalp intervention studies (Barry et al., 2025: n=40 [2]; Lee et al., 2025: n=40 [4]; Li et al., 2025: n=30). AWARE defers to the PI for formal power analysis based on clinically meaningful effect sizes for the co-primary endpoints. All study parameters are proposed as a starting point for discussion - the Principal Investigator owns final protocol design, including enrollment criteria, randomization methodology, and endpoint selection.
Publication commitment: AWARE commits to supporting publication of results regardless of outcome. Negative or null results are scientifically valuable and will not be suppressed. Full ingredient lists (INCI) are available under NDA for inclusion in methodology sections.
Governance & Independence

AWARE recognizes that scientific credibility depends on clear separation between sponsor and investigator roles. The following commitments are proposed as the basis for a formal data access agreement to be executed prior to study initiation:

Data Ownership The Principal Investigator and USC retain full ownership of all study data. AWARE will have no access to individual participant-level data.
Interim Analysis AWARE will have no access to interim results or unblinded data during the active study period. Any interim analysis will be conducted at the PI's discretion.
Publication Rights The PI and co-investigators retain sole authority over manuscript content and submission decisions. AWARE may review for factual accuracy regarding product composition (30-day review window) but holds no veto over findings or conclusions.
COI Disclosure All investigators will disclose the industry sponsorship per ICMJE standards. AWARE's funding role will be transparently reported in all publications and presentations.
Safety Oversight An independent data and safety monitoring structure will be established in accordance with the PI's recommendation and IRB requirements.
These terms are standard for industry-sponsored academic research and are offered as a starting point. Final governance language will be formalized through the USC Clinical Trials Office and incorporated into the study agreement.

Academic Value

A single well-designed dataset from this study can support two to three distinct publications: a primary manuscript on microbiome response to plant-based intervention across diverse hair types, a secondary analysis establishing scalp health baselines for underrepresented populations (a documented gap in the literature), and a potential commentary connecting clean beauty consumer trends to clinical evidence standards.

The study also creates structured training opportunities within the Keck School of Medicine. The project scope is well-suited for the mandatory scholarly project required of all Keck medical students, and the timeline aligns with the summer research fellowship program (6 weeks full-time between MS1/MS2). Students pursuing the MS in Clinical Translational Research would find this study appropriate for thesis work, particularly given its intersection of microbiome science, health equity, and industry partnership.

The study aligns with broader institutional priorities: healthcare disparities research, emerging methodology in scalp microbiome science, and the growing infrastructure for industry-sponsored partnerships. It represents an opportunity to lead in a nascent field rather than replicate established paradigms.

Comparable Published Studies (2024-2026)

Ziziphus Joazeiro Bark Shampoo

2025 - Int J Cosmetic Science, n=40, open-label pilot study

Open-label single-center pilot study of a plant-based shampoo measuring scalp microbiome response and barrier function. Barry et al. Closest published precedent to the proposed AWARE study: plant-derived active, microbiome endpoints, and cosmetic product category.

Plantago Asiatica Shampoo

2025 - MDPI Cosmetics, n=40, double-blind RCT

Double-blind randomized placebo-controlled clinical study measuring hair thickness, density, and shedding reduction. Lee et al. Plant-based cosmetic shampoo in same product category as AWARE. Published in peer-reviewed journal with full methodology disclosure.

Caffeine/Adenosine Shampoo

2025 - Int J Cosmetic Science, n=30, 12 weeks

Li et al. Randomized single-blind study incorporating a multi-omics approach (microbiome + lipidome analysis). Demonstrates growing editorial expectation for scalp microbiome endpoints in cosmetic intervention studies.

Scalp Microbiome & Skin Barrier

2020 - Experimental Dermatology, Polak-Witka et al.

Comprehensive review establishing the relationship between scalp microbiome composition, barrier function, and hair follicle biology. Identifies diversity and intervention gaps in scalp microbiome research. Widely cited in subsequent scalp health literature.

Pattern across published studies: Sample sizes of 30-40 participants are standard and sufficient for publication in peer-reviewed dermatology and cosmetic science journals. A duration of 8-12 weeks is typical. Study designs range from open-label pilot studies to double-blind RCTs. Scalp microbiome endpoints are increasingly expected by reviewers. Plant-based formulations remain underrepresented relative to pharmaceutical actives, suggesting editorial appetite for studies in this space.
