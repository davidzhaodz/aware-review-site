Scalp Science

Your Scalp Is Skin

The most overlooked skin on your body deserves the same attention as your face.

Your scalp has more blood vessels, nerve endings, and sebaceous glands per square inch than almost any other skin on your body.

Yet most people spend 30+ minutes on their facial skincare routine and less than 60 seconds on their scalp.

Hair growth depends on the environment around the follicle - like soil for a plant.

Scalp Cross-Section
Scalp Science

Micro-circulation

Blood flow to the follicle determines how well your hair can grow.
Healthy Circulation
Nutrients and oxygen flow freely to follicles. Scalp feels supple and moves easily. Hair grows at its natural rate with strength and shine.
Restricted Flow
Tension, inflammation, and buildup limit blood flow. Scalp feels tight, sometimes tender. Follicles are starved of what they need.
The good news: You can improve circulation starting today.
Scalp Science

The Connected Body

Tension in your jaw and temples directly affects your scalp. It's all connected through fascia.
Fascia is the connective tissue that runs beneath your skin in continuous sheets. When it tightens in one area, the whole chain is affected. This is why gua sha doesn't just work on the scalp - it releases the entire tension pattern.

Why Gua Sha

An ancient practice backed by modern understanding of circulation and fascia.

Gua sha ("gwah shah") has been used in traditional Chinese medicine for thousands of years. The broad, smooth tool is designed to move tissue, not scratch.

Applied to the scalp, it follows meridian pathways - specific energy lines that connect to different body systems.

Improves Circulation
Increases blood flow to follicles, delivering nutrients and oxygen
Releases Tension
Loosens tight fascia along the jaw-temple-scalp chain
Supports Lymphatic Drainage
Clears buildup and reduces inflammation around follicles

Step 1 of 4

Hairline

Meridian Point: Shenting GV24
01
Reset - Shenting GV24
Glide Upward from Hairline to Crown
Place the flat edge of the gua sha at your hairline. Using gentle, even pressure, glide upward toward the crown. Repeat 5-8 times.
Why here: The Shenting point is where tension from the forehead meets the scalp. Releasing this area improves circulation to the entire front of the head.
Step 2 of 4

Crown

Meridian Point: Baihui GV20
02
Repair - Baihui GV20
Circular Pressure at the Crown
Pause at the crown. Apply light circular pressure for 10-15 seconds. You should feel a gentle warmth building.
Why here: Baihui is the "hundred convergences" point - where multiple meridians meet. In traditional practice, it's the central hub for circulation to the entire head.
Step 3 of 4

Temples

Meridian Point: Shuaigu GB8
03
Reinforce - Shuaigu GB8
Sweep Along Both Temples
Use the curved edge. Start above the ear and sweep in small, firm strokes along the temple. Repeat on both sides.
Why here: The temples are where jaw tension transfers to the scalp. If you clench, grind, or hold stress in your face, this step makes the biggest difference.
Step 4 of 4

Nape

Meridian Point: Fengchi GB20
04
Reactivate - Fengchi GB20
Press and Hold at the Base of the Skull
Find the two hollows at the base of your skull. Press the gua sha gently into each point and hold for 10-15 seconds. Breathe.
Why here: Fengchi is called the "wind pool" - a point traditionally used to relieve headaches, neck tension, and to promote circulation through the back of the head.
