2. How the Scalp Works
10:10 - 10:22 12 min Tina Slides 4-6
Purpose Teach the real science. Don't dumb it down - these are licensed professionals. But give them explicit permission to learn it over time: "You don't have to memorize this today. Take notes. Study it later. But I'm going to give you the full picture so you know it exists."

Pause briefly on Slide 4 (divider). Let it breathe.

Slide 5 - Scalp Anatomy
Talk Point - Framing "I'm going to give you the science version of this first. You don't have to memorize it all today, but I want you to understand it, and I'd encourage you to take notes so you can go back and study it. Then I'll give you the simple version, the one you can use with your clients right away."
Talk Point - The Science "Picture a cross-section of the scalp. On top, the epidermis - this is the scalp barrier. It's about 20 cell layers thick, and its job is to protect everything underneath. Below that, the dermis, where the blood vessels, nerve endings, and collagen live. Then the subcutaneous layer. Sitting in all of that are the hair follicles - each one connected to blood vessels that feed it, a sebaceous gland that produces oil, and everything is interconnected."
Talk Point "The scalp barrier is critical. When the barrier is healthy, it protects the follicles, regulates moisture, and keeps irritants out. When the barrier is compromised - from harsh products, over-processing, environmental damage - that's when problems start."
Talk Point - The Client Version "Here's how you explain this to someone in your chair: 'Think of your scalp like a garden. The hair is what grows above the surface, but everything depends on the soil underneath. If the soil is dry, depleted, or compacted, nothing thrives. If the soil is healthy, everything grows better.'"
Talk Point "Both versions are important. The science is for you, so you truly understand what you're working with. The garden analogy is for your clients, because they don't need the anatomy lesson, they just need to understand why it matters."
Slide 6 - Micro-Circulation + Fascia

Micro-Circulation

Talk Point "Every hair follicle needs its own blood supply. Blood vessels feed the follicle nutrients and oxygen. When blood flow is restricted - from chronic tension, inflammation, or environmental damage - the follicle doesn't get what it needs. Growth slows, hair gets thinner, and you start seeing the problems your clients complain about."
Talk Point "Research shows that blood flow directly controls follicle size and how long the growth phase lasts. More blood flow means larger follicles and longer growth phases. That's the clinical basis for everything we're going to do with the gua sha today."

Fascia Connection - Interactive Moment

Talk Point "Here's something that surprises people. Put your hands on the sides of your head, just above your ears. Now clench your jaw. Feel that?"

(Have everyone do the jaw clench with hands on temples.)
Talk Point "The tension chain runs like this: your jaw muscles connect into the fascia at your temples. That fascia continues up over the crown as a thick sheet called the galea. And the galea connects down the back to your muscles at the nape. Jaw, temples, crown, nape - one continuous system."
Talk Point - For the Chair "When your client says 'I carry all my stress in my neck and shoulders,' that stress is traveling up through this chain to the top of their head. Chronic scalp tension compresses blood vessels and restricts circulation to the follicles. This is why massage and gua sha actually make a difference - it's not just relaxation, it's restoring blood flow."
Advance "So now you understand the foundation. Let's talk about what your clients are actually telling you." Advance to Slide 7 (divider).
3. What Your Clients Are Telling You
10:22 - 10:34 12 min Tina Slides 7-9
Purpose Make this relatable and empowering, not intimidating. Start with the SYMPTOMS clients describe (not clinical diagnosis names) so stylists feel "I know exactly what she's talking about." Then teach them WHAT TO ASK before trying to understand the problem. Frame everything as caring for the client, never as selling.
Slide 8 - The Symptoms You Hear
Talk Point "Let's talk about the things your clients are actually saying to you. Not the clinical names, but the real complaints you hear at the chair."

Walk through each symptom. Keep the language simple and the framing empowering.

1 "I'm shedding" / "My hair is thinning"
Talk Point "This is the big one. You'll hear it more than anything else. A client notices more hair in the drain, their ponytail feels thinner, their part looks wider. Before you panic them or yourself, you need to ask some questions, because shedding can mean a lot of things. It could be stress, it could be hormonal, it could be traction from tight styling, or it could be that the scalp environment isn't supporting healthy growth."
2 "I have dandruff" / "My scalp is flaking"
Talk Point "When a client says 'I have dandruff,' it could be simple dryness, it could be build-up flaking off, or it could be something more specific going on with the scalp's natural yeast balance. You can't know which one until you ask and look. The key is, not all flaking is the same. Dry white flakes are different from oily yellowish flakes, and they need different approaches."
3 "My scalp itches"
Talk Point "Itchy scalp is so common. And clients are embarrassed by it. They think it means they're not clean. But itching is usually a sign of inflammation or a reaction to something. It could be build-up, it could be a product sensitivity, it could be stress-related. The point is, when a client tells you their scalp itches, that's a diagnostic clue. Don't dismiss it."
4 "I have a lot of build-up" / You can see build-up at the bowl
Talk Point "Build-up is the one you can actually see and feel. Heavy product residue, sebum accumulation, hard water minerals. You see it at the shampoo bowl all the time. This one is directly in your wheelhouse because a good cleansing protocol addresses it immediately. And it's a natural conversation starter about scalp care."
5 "My scalp is red" / "It burns after color"
Talk Point "Redness and sensitivity, especially after services. This could be a reaction to a specific product, or it could be that the scalp barrier is already compromised and the service pushed it over the edge. This is why it's so important to know what your client is using at home and what their scalp looks like before you start a service."
Slide 9 - The 5 Questions to Ask
Talk Point "Before you try to figure out what's going on, you need to ask your client some questions. This is what any good clinician does - gather information first. Here are the five questions you should be asking."
Q1 "What products are you using at home?"
This is the most revealing question. If they're using something that's clogging their pores or stripping their scalp, you've already found a big piece of the puzzle.
Q2 "Have you been under a lot of stress recently?"
Stress directly affects the scalp through cortisol, inflammation, and tension. Major life changes, work stress, health stress - it all shows up on the scalp.
Q3 "How's your diet been?"
Nutrition matters. Iron, vitamin D, protein, hydration - deficiencies show up as thinning and brittle hair. You don't need to be a nutritionist, but asking opens the door.
Q4 "Are you exercising regularly?"
Exercise improves circulation everywhere, including the scalp. Sedentary clients often have more sluggish scalp environments.
Q5 "Has anything changed recently? New medications, new products, new routine?"
Change is often the trigger. A new medication, a new shampoo, a new styling routine - something shifted and the scalp is reacting.
Talk Point "These questions aren't about selling anything. They're about understanding your client. When you ask these questions, your client feels cared for. They feel like you're paying attention to them as a whole person, not just their hair. And the answers give you real information you can use."
Talk Point "'I'm noticing X, have you noticed that?' is the most powerful sentence in your scalp care vocabulary. You don't need to diagnose. You need to observe, ask, and communicate."
Advance "Now let me show you what all of this looks like when you can actually see it." Advance to Slide 10.

5. The 4R Framework
10:44 - 11:00 16 min Tina + Jade Slides 11-15
Purpose Teach the PHILOSOPHY first, products second. Talk about what the scalp needs without naming AWARE until the end. Let the logic build so that when the products are introduced, they feel like a natural conclusion, not a sales pitch.

Pause on Slide 11 (divider). Let it breathe.

Slide 12 - The Philosophy
Talk Point "Okay, so now you understand the scalp, you know how to spot problems, you know what questions to ask. The next question is: what do you actually DO about it?"
Talk Point "We developed a framework called the 4Rs. It's simple, it's sequential, and it works as a baseline for every client, regardless of their specific issue. Think of it as the foundation of scalp health."
Talk Point "The four steps are: Reset, Repair, Reinforce, Reactivate. The order matters. You clear the environment first, then restore it, then protect it, then stimulate it."
Slide 13 - Reset + Repair
Step 1 Reset
Talk Point "You've got to start with a clean slate. All that build-up we talked about - product residue, sebum, environmental deposits, hard water minerals - it's suffocating the follicle opening and disrupting the scalp's natural pH."
Talk Point "The key is how you cleanse. Focus on the scalp, not the lengths. Add water and emulsify longer than you think. Double cleanse: first wash lifts the surface debris, second wash actually treats the scalp. Use fingertip pads, never nails."
Talk Point - Common Mistake "Clients lather for 10 seconds and rinse. Or they skip washing because someone told them to 'train their hair.' After heavy exposure days - city pollution, workouts, pool, beach - they need to cleanse."
Step 2 Repair
Talk Point "Once the scalp is clean, you need to restore moisture and nourishment to the barrier. This is where ingredients like hyaluronic acid matter - it holds up to 1,000 times its weight in water and supports the barrier's hydration. Green tea extract is antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory, which helps the scalp's natural microbiome get back in balance."
Talk Point "The microbiome is important for stylists to understand. A balanced scalp microbiome regulates pH, controls inflammation, defends the barrier. When it's disrupted, that's when chronic problems start. Repair is about creating the conditions for the microbiome to self-regulate."
Slide 14 - Reinforce + Reactivate
Step 3 Reinforce
Talk Point "Growth means nothing if the hair breaks before it's visible. Reinforce is about protecting the scalp and hair from daily stressors."
Talk Point "UV radiation directly affects the scalp - especially along the part line. Air pollution causes oxidative stress. Hard water deposits build up over time. Your clients need daily defense, the same way they protect their face with SPF."
Step 4 Reactivate
Talk Point "This is where the gua sha comes in. Reactivate stimulates circulation, releases that fascial tension we talked about, and activates blood flow to the follicles. Research shows a fourfold increase in surface blood flow from gua sha. That's not a small effect."
The Product Reveal
Talk Point "So that's the framework: Reset, Repair, Reinforce, Reactivate. Clear the scalp, restore the barrier, protect from stressors, stimulate circulation. Any client can follow this, regardless of what products they use."
Talk Point "Now, our line - AWARE - was built specifically around this framework. Our shampoo is the Reset. Our scalp mask is the Repair. Our leave-in is the Reinforce. And our gua sha is the Reactivate. Every product maps to a step. But the philosophy works even if someone isn't using our products. The framework is what matters."

6. Gua Sha Demo + Practice
11:00 - 11:14 14 min Tina + Jade Slides 16-18
Purpose The most hands-on section. Teach the technique, have everyone practice, then add the professional layer: how to offer this at the bowl as a service. Introduce the stainless steel tool for professional use.

Pause on Slide 16 (divider). Let it breathe.

Slide 17 - The 4 Steps
Talk Point - Introduce the Tools "Pick up the gua sha in front of you. This is our sandalwood gua sha - naturally smooth so it glides without catching hair, antimicrobial, warm to the touch. This is for retail, for clients to use at home."
Talk Point "For professional use at the bowl, we also have stainless steel gua shas. They're easier to sanitize between clients and they hold up to daily salon use. This is what you'd incorporate into your service."
Talk Point - Key Technique Principle "The most important thing: we're moving the scalp tissue, not scratching the surface. Light to medium pressure. The broad flat surface should make full contact with the skin. If it feels like scraping, ease up. This should feel good."

Walk through each step. Demonstrate on yourself first, then have everyone practice before moving to the next step.

Step 1 - Hairline
Talk Point "Start at the center of the forehead hairline. Broad, slow strokes moving from front to back toward the crown. 8-10 strokes. This releases the frontalis muscle and opens circulation from the face into the scalp."
(Demonstrate. Everyone practices.)
Step 2 - Crown (Baihui)
Talk Point "Move to the crown. Circular motions, or radial strokes outward from the center point. 8-10 strokes per direction. The crown is where the galea is thickest and tension accumulates most."
Talk Point - Credibility Tip "This point is called Baihui in traditional Chinese medicine - it means 'hundred meetings.' If you can remember just one meridian name, remember this one. When you tell a client 'I'm going to work the Baihui point at your crown,' it sounds like you know exactly what you're doing. Because you do."
(Demonstrate. Everyone practices.)
Step 3 - Temples
Talk Point "Broad strokes from the temples upward toward the crown. Also small circles directly on the temple. 8-10 strokes each side. This is where most people hold tension - the jaw-to-scalp connection we talked about earlier. Remember the clench test."
(Demonstrate. Everyone practices.)
Step 4 - Nape
Talk Point "Strokes from the nape upward toward the crown, and also outward along the base of the skull. 8-10 strokes. The occipital region connects to the neck and shoulders where tension commonly originates."
(Demonstrate. Everyone practices.)
Slide 18 - Hands-On Practice
Talk Point "Take three minutes and run through all four steps on yourself. Feel the difference between light and medium pressure. Notice which areas feel tighter than others."
During Practice Tina walks the room and corrects technique. Jade assists individual stylists who need help. Both provide coaching as needed.
Talk Point - After Practice "Now let me tell you how to offer this at the bowl. The technique is identical, but you're standing behind the client. You can incorporate it during a conditioning treatment or scalp mask while it processes. The gua sha helps the product absorb while stimulating circulation. Sanitize between clients with your standard protocol."
Talk Point "You can offer this as part of an add-on service, or honestly, just do it during the conditioning step for a bigger tip. Once your client feels it, they're going to ask for it every time."
Talk Point - If Someone Asks About Meridians "In traditional Chinese medicine, these points are where energy pathways converge. Modern research shows that these same locations correspond to major blood vessel supply zones and fascial attachment points on the scalp. Whether you frame it as meridian work or circulatory stimulation, the anatomy supports both."

7. When It's Beyond Your Chair + Medicated Products
11:14 - 11:22 8 min Tina Slides 19-21
Purpose Stylists need to know BOTH: when to send a client to a dermatologist, AND what to recommend in the meantime. This builds trust and credibility. Not most people want to go see a doctor yet, so if you can help them with the first step, that's what a stylist should do.
Slide 19 - Red Flags: When to Say "See a Dermatologist"
Talk Point "This is one of the most important things I can teach you today. Knowing when a problem is beyond what you can help with - that builds more trust than anything else."
Refer to a Dermatologist If You See Any of These
  • Sudden patchy hair loss - could be autoimmune
  • Pain, burning, or tenderness at the scalp
  • Scarring or smooth patches where follicles are gone
  • Pustules, crusting, or non-healing sores
  • Big, thick scales that are connected to the scalp (likely seborrheic dermatitis requiring medicated treatment)
  • Any condition that doesn't improve after 4-6 weeks of consistent scalp care
Talk Point - How to Have the Conversation "'I'm noticing some changes that I think a dermatologist should take a look at. I'll keep supporting your scalp health from my side, but the medical piece needs a doctor. Think of it as a team effort.'"
Talk Point "Never diagnose. Never alarm. Describe what you see, not what it is clinically. And always position the referral as additive - you're adding a team member, not giving up on them."
Slide 20 - Medicated Products: What Your Clients Are Using
Talk Point "Your clients are going to ask you about this all the time: 'What about Head and Shoulders? Should I use Nizoral? My doctor prescribed this medicated shampoo, should I stop using it?'"
Talk Point "The answer is simple: if your doctor prescribed a medicated shampoo, absolutely keep using it. Those products are treating a specific condition. What you can recommend is to cycle in a gentler, more nourishing shampoo alongside it - maybe every other wash - because the problem with most medicated shampoos is they can be really drying for the scalp and the hair."

Run through the chart. Focus on salon compatibility - that's what matters most to this audience.

Active Ingredient Common Brands What It Does Salon Compatibility
Zinc pyrithione Head & Shoulders Antifungal. Reduces Malassezia. Generally safe with color. Can leave residue - thorough rinse before a service.
Ketoconazole Nizoral (OTC 1%, Rx 2%) Broad-spectrum antifungal. Can dry the scalp and hair. Advise conditioning well after.
Selenium sulfide Selsun Blue Slows cell turnover, antifungal. Can discolor light or color-treated hair. Flag for blonde clients.
Salicylic acid Neutrogena T/Sal Dissolves flakes and build-up. Good bridge product between salon visits. Color-safe.
Coal tar Neutrogena T/Gel Used for psoriasis. Strong odor, can stain light hair. Not cosmetically elegant.
Talk Point "The key framing for your clients: the 4R protocol is your daily maintenance, your proactive scalp wellness routine. Medicated products are targeted interventions for specific conditions. They're not competitors - they serve different purposes. You might use both."
Talk Point "Try the natural approach first, because it's gentler and it won't compromise the hair. But if the problem is severe or persistent, that's when you recommend they see a doctor for something more targeted. And if they're already on a prescription, don't tell them to stop - just help them take care of the rest of their scalp."
Slide 21 - Scripts for Sensitive Conversations
Script - For a Referral "I'm noticing something at your scalp that I think you should have a professional look at. It might be nothing, but I'd feel better if you got a second opinion. In the meantime, let's keep up your scalp care routine."
Script - For Medicated Products "For what you're describing, you might want to try a zinc pyrithione shampoo like Head and Shoulders a couple times a week. It targets that specific issue. And then on your other wash days, use something gentler so your hair doesn't dry out."
Script - When They Ask "Should I Stop My Prescription?" "Absolutely not. Keep using what your doctor prescribed. You can use a gentler shampoo on alternating days to keep your hair from getting too dry, but don't stop your treatment."

Slide 22 - Q&A
Talk Point "What questions do you have? Anything from your own practice, things your clients have asked, things you're not sure about."

Pre-Loaded Answers

"How often should clients use the gua sha?"
3-5 times per week, 3-5 minutes. Consistency matters more than intensity.
"Can I use it on a sensitive scalp?"
Yes, lighter pressure. If it's severely inflamed, wait until it calms down.
"Does this cure hair loss?"
Scalp care supports the environment where hair grows. Hair loss has many causes. For significant changes, always recommend a dermatologist alongside good scalp care.
"What about clients on minoxidil?"
Different mechanisms. The 4R framework maintains the scalp environment. Minoxidil targets follicle miniaturization. They complement each other. Never advise stopping medication.
"How does this work with extensions?"
Adapt the gua sha around attachment points. The scalp under extensions needs more attention because it's under constant tension.
"Wholesale minimum?"
David has all the details on that. He's right here, grab him after and he'll walk you through everything. No pressure.
