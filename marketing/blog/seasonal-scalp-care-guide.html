Seasonal Scalp Care: How to Adjust Your Routine Year-Round

Tina Mui February 21, 2026 9 min read

Your scalp needs different care in each season because UV exposure, humidity, temperature, and pollen levels directly affect your scalp's protective barrier and hair growth cycle. According to research published in the British Journal of Dermatology, seasonal changes in daylight and temperature significantly influence hair shedding patterns, with most people losing more hair in late summer and early fall. AWARE Hair founder and trichologist Tina Mui recommends adjusting your scalp care routine seasonally - the same way you adjust your skincare - to prevent months of unnecessary damage from environmental stressors.

Why Your Scalp Responds to Seasons

Your scalp is skin, and like the skin on your face, it reacts to changes in temperature, humidity, and sunlight. The difference is that most people adjust their facial skincare with the seasons but never think to do the same for their scalp. That oversight can lead to months of unnecessary dryness, oiliness, or sensitivity.

The scalp barrier, a thin layer of lipids and skin cells that sits on the surface, is the first line of defense against whatever the environment throws at it. In high heat and humidity, the barrier deals with excess sweat, oil production, and UV radiation. In cold, dry air, it struggles to hold onto moisture. Each season creates a different set of challenges, and the barrier can only adapt so far on its own before it starts to show signs of stress.

There is also a hormonal component. Your body's production of melatonin and vitamin D shifts with daylight exposure, and both of these influence the hair growth cycle. Research has consistently shown that hair shedding follows a seasonal rhythm, with most people losing more hair in late summer and early fall than at any other time of year.

Research Note

UV and oxidative stress on hair fibers: UV radiation does not just affect the scalp surface. It generates reactive oxygen species (free radicals) that break down the proteins and lipids in both the scalp barrier and the hair shaft itself. This oxidative damage weakens the hair from root to tip, leading to dryness, color fading, and increased breakage. The effect is cumulative, meaning damage from one summer carries into the following seasons.

Trueeb RM. "The impact of oxidative stress on hair." International Journal of Cosmetic Science. 2015;37 Suppl 2:25-30.

The good news is that once you understand the pattern, the adjustments are straightforward. You do not need four completely different routines. You need one solid routine with seasonal tweaks, the same way you might switch from a light moisturizer in summer to a richer one in winter for your face. Your scalp microbiome also shifts with the seasons, as temperature and humidity changes alter the balance of bacteria and fungi on your scalp.

Summer Scalp Care

Summer hits your scalp from multiple angles. UV radiation breaks down the protective oils in your scalp barrier through oxidation. Higher temperatures increase sweating, which can clog follicles and shift the scalp's pH. And if you are spending time in pools or the ocean, chlorine and saltwater strip moisture in ways that take days to recover from.

UV Protection for Your Scalp

Most people apply sunscreen to their face, arms, and shoulders, but forget that the scalp, especially along the part line, gets direct UV exposure all day long. You cannot easily apply traditional sunscreen to your scalp without making your hair greasy and flat. Instead, the best approach is a combination of physical coverage (hats, scarves) during peak UV hours (10am to 4pm) and a leave-in product that contains UV-protective antioxidants.

Green tea extract, for example, is rich in polyphenol antioxidants that neutralize the free radicals UV generates on your scalp. Applying a leave-in treatment with these kinds of ingredients after washing creates a lightweight protective layer that does not weigh your hair down.

Managing Sweat and Oil

When temperatures rise, your scalp produces more sweat and more oil. The natural instinct is to wash more often, but this can backfire. Frequent washing with the wrong shampoo strips the barrier, triggering even more oil production as your scalp tries to compensate. The cycle accelerates.

A better approach: use a sulfate-free shampoo that cleanses effectively without stripping, and rinse with cool or lukewarm water (never hot). On days when you are sweating but do not need a full wash, a water-only rinse can remove salt and sweat without disrupting the barrier. Focus any cleanser on the scalp itself, not the lengths of your hair.

Chlorine and Saltwater

Both chlorine and saltwater are highly drying. Chlorine bonds to the proteins in your hair and scalp, raising the pH and weakening the barrier. Saltwater draws moisture out through osmosis. If you are swimming regularly in summer, wet your hair with fresh water before getting in the pool or ocean. Hair that is already saturated absorbs less chlorine and salt. After swimming, rinse thoroughly and apply a hydrating leave-in to seal moisture back in.

Research Note

Seasonal hair shedding peaks in late summer: A study analyzing Google Trends data across 8 countries found that searches for "hair loss" consistently peaked in summer and early fall, suggesting a widespread seasonal shedding pattern. Earlier clinical research confirmed this, showing that the percentage of hair follicles in the resting (telogen) phase peaks around July and August, with the resulting shedding visible 2 to 3 months later. This is a normal cycle, not a sign of a problem.

Kunz M, et al. "Seasonality of hair shedding in healthy women." British Journal of Dermatology. 2009;161(1):8-14. | Google Trends seasonal analysis of "hair loss" search volume.
  • Wear a hat during peak hours - UV exposure on the scalp is highest between 10am and 4pm, and your part line gets direct sun exposure
  • Use a leave-in with antioxidants - green tea and organic beetroot extracts help neutralize UV-generated free radicals on the scalp
  • Rinse before swimming - pre-saturating hair with fresh water reduces chlorine and saltwater absorption
  • Switch to lighter products - heavy conditioners and styling creams can trap sweat and clog follicles in heat
  • Do not over-wash - a water rinse between proper washes keeps the scalp fresh without stripping the barrier

If you live in an area with hard water, summer's increased wash frequency can also accelerate mineral buildup on your scalp, compounding the damage from UV and sweat.

Key Takeaway: Summer scalp care means protecting against UV (hats + antioxidant leave-ins), managing sweat without over-washing (water rinses between full washes), and pre-wetting hair before pools or ocean. The key is gentle, consistent protection - not aggressive cleansing.

Winter Scalp Care

If summer is about protection from external aggressors, winter is about holding onto what you have. Cold outdoor air holds less moisture. Indoor heating drops humidity even further, sometimes below 20%, which is drier than the Sahara Desert. Your scalp barrier, which relies on a certain level of ambient moisture to stay intact, starts losing water faster than it can replace it.

The Dry Air Problem

When humidity drops, water evaporates faster from your scalp surface. The barrier's lipid layer, the "mortar" between skin cells, dries out and develops tiny gaps. These gaps allow even more moisture to escape, creating a cycle of increasing dryness. You might notice tightness after washing, flaking that was not there in warmer months, or hair that feels rough and static-prone.

The first line of defense is environmental. A humidifier in your bedroom can keep indoor humidity between 40% and 60%, which makes a real difference for your skin and scalp overnight. It is one of the simplest and most effective things you can do for winter scalp health.

Heavier Hydration

Winter is the time to lean into more moisturizing products. If you normally use a lightweight conditioner, consider switching to a richer formula or adding a leave-in treatment to seal in moisture after washing. Look for ingredients that both hydrate and form a protective barrier on the surface.

Dual-weight hyaluronic acid is especially effective here. The smaller molecules penetrate the scalp to hydrate from within, while the larger molecules create a breathable film on the surface that slows water loss. Think of it as replacing the moisture your barrier cannot hold onto on its own.

Wash Less, Wash Gentler

Your scalp produces less oil in winter, so you can often extend the time between washes. Going from every other day to every two or three days gives the barrier more time to rebuild its protective oil layer. When you do wash, use lukewarm water (not hot, no matter how tempting) and a sulfate-free formula. Hot water dissolves barrier oils and can leave your scalp feeling stripped and tight within minutes.

The biggest mistake I see is people using the same routine year-round. Your scalp in July is not the same scalp in January. A few small adjustments each season can prevent months of unnecessary damage.

Spring and Fall Transitions

The in-between seasons are when most people first notice scalp problems, precisely because conditions are shifting and your routine has not caught up. Spring brings rising humidity, pollen, and allergens. Fall brings dropping humidity and the natural shedding cycle that peaks in August and September. Both require a gradual transition rather than an abrupt change.

Spring: Pollen and Allergies

Pollen is one of the most overlooked scalp irritants. It lands on your hair and scalp throughout the day, and if you do not rinse it off before bed, it sits against your skin for hours. For people with seasonal allergies, this contact can trigger scalp inflammation, itchiness, redness, and increased sensitivity.

If you notice your scalp becoming more reactive in spring, a gentle evening rinse can make a significant difference. You do not need to shampoo every night. A thorough water rinse removes the majority of pollen without stripping your barrier. On days when pollen counts are especially high (your local weather service tracks this), a light cleanse with a sulfate-free shampoo focused on the scalp is worth it.

Spring is also when humidity starts climbing again. If you have been using heavier winter products, this is the time to gradually lighten up. Continuing to use a rich, occlusive leave-in as humidity rises can lead to a greasy, weighed-down feeling and may contribute to follicle congestion.

Fall: Shedding Season and Humidity Drops

Seeing more hair in your brush in September or October can feel alarming, but in most cases it is completely normal. Research shows that a higher percentage of hair follicles enter the resting phase (called telogen) during mid-summer, and the resulting shedding becomes visible about 2 to 3 months later. This is your body's natural rhythm, not a sign of hair loss.

The best thing you can do during fall shedding season is support the new growth that is already underway. That means keeping the scalp environment healthy: well-hydrated, properly nourished, and free from inflammation. Ingredients like organic turmeric extract, a natural anti-inflammatory, can help calm a reactive scalp during this transition. Tahitian microalgae strengthens the hair fiber itself, with clinical data showing significant improvement in breakage resistance after regular use.

As humidity drops in fall, start reintroducing the moisture-sealing steps you will rely on through winter. A leave-in treatment applied after washing creates a protective layer that slows water loss as the air becomes drier. Making this shift gradually, rather than waiting until your scalp is already flaking in December, keeps the barrier ahead of the curve.

Research Note

Humidity and hair fiber behavior: Hair is hygroscopic, meaning it absorbs and releases water from the surrounding air. At high humidity, hair fibers swell and become more elastic (and frizz-prone). At low humidity, they contract, become more brittle, and generate static. These physical changes in the hair shaft mirror what is happening at the scalp level: the barrier is also absorbing or losing water depending on ambient conditions. This is why the same product can feel perfect in one season and completely wrong in another.

Robbins CR. "Chemical and Physical Behavior of Human Hair," 5th Edition. Springer, 2012. Chapter 2: Morphological, Macroscopic, and Mechanical Properties.

How to Transition Gradually

Abrupt product changes can confuse your scalp. Instead of swapping your entire routine overnight, make one change at a time and give it 2 to 3 weeks before adjusting further. A good approach:

  • Spring transition (March-April) - lighten your leave-in or conditioner first, keep wash frequency the same, add an evening rinse on high-pollen days
  • Summer shift (May-June) - switch to your lightest products, add UV protection, adjust wash frequency based on activity level
  • Fall transition (September-October) - reintroduce a richer leave-in, do not panic about shedding, focus on anti-inflammatory ingredients
  • Winter shift (November-December) - use your richest hydration, extend time between washes, set up a humidifier before the heating season

Frequently Asked Questions

Does hair really fall out more in certain seasons?

Yes. Research shows that hair shedding follows a seasonal pattern, with most people losing more hair in late summer and early fall. A large-scale study published in the British Journal of Dermatology found that hair loss peaks around August and September, likely because the body holds onto more hair during summer to protect the scalp from UV, then releases it as the days shorten. This is a normal cycle and not a sign of permanent hair loss.

Should I wash my hair more often in summer?

It depends on your activity level, not the calendar. If you are sweating more, swimming in chlorinated pools, or spending time in saltwater, you may need to wash more frequently to remove buildup. However, the goal is still gentle cleansing that does not strip your barrier. Use a sulfate-free shampoo and focus on the scalp rather than the lengths. If you are not sweating heavily, a water-only rinse between washes can be enough to keep your scalp fresh.

Can cold weather cause dandruff?

Cold weather itself does not cause dandruff, but the conditions that come with it can make dandruff worse. Indoor heating dramatically lowers humidity, which dries out your scalp and weakens the barrier. When the barrier is compromised, the yeast Malassezia (which naturally lives on everyone's scalp) can overgrow and trigger the flaking and irritation associated with dandruff. Keeping your scalp hydrated through the winter months is the best way to prevent seasonal flare-ups.
