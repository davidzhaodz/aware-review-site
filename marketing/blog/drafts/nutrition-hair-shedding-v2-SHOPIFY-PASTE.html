Most cases of diet-driven hair shedding trace back to three nutrients: iron, protein, and vitamin D. Everything else sold in the hair supplement aisle is either unnecessary for most people, unproven in well-designed trials, or in a few cases capable of causing the shedding it claims to prevent. This guide walks through what the evidence actually supports, how to know if your diet is likely the cause, what to ask your doctor to test, and why buying a supplement before your bloodwork is usually the wrong first move.

The Short Version
  • Three nutrients matter most: iron (ferritin 40 to 70 ng/mL), protein (0.8 to 1.2 g per kg of body weight), and vitamin D.
  • "Normal" ferritin misleads. Lab range starts at 12 ng/mL. Your hair often needs 40 or higher.
  • The lag is three to six months. You will not see reduced shedding for one full hair cycle, even after correcting the cause.
  • Most supplements do not help. Biotin shows no effect in trials. Selenium and vitamin A can cause the shedding they claim to prevent.
  • Test before you supplement. Ferritin, 25-hydroxyvitamin D, CBC, TSH, B12.

First, What Is Actually Happening to Your Hair

Every hair on your head cycles through three phases. Anagen is the active growth phase, which can last two to seven years. Catagen is a short transitional phase of about two weeks. Telogen is the resting phase that lasts three to four months, at the end of which the hair falls out and a new one starts growing in its place.

On a normal head of hair, roughly eighty to ninety percent of follicles are in anagen at any given moment and about ten to fifteen percent are in telogen. Losing fifty to one hundred hairs a day is normal shedding. It is what healthy hair turnover looks like.

What most people call "diet-related hair loss" is a specific condition called telogen effluvium. It happens when a stressor of some kind, metabolic, nutritional, hormonal, or psychological, pushes a larger than normal portion of your follicles out of anagen and into telogen all at once. Three to four months later, those follicles reach the end of the resting phase and shed. That is why the shedding you are noticing today often traces back to something that happened in your body a season ago.

The Hair Cycle

Why the lag matters: Because hair follicles have to complete the telogen phase before the old hair sheds, the gap between a trigger and visible shedding is typically two to four months. The same lag applies in reverse: when you correct a nutritional cause, you will not see reduced shedding for three to six months, and full cosmetic recovery usually takes twelve to eighteen months. Almost every mistake people make with their hair comes from not understanding this timeline.

Hughes EC, Saleh D. "Telogen Effluvium." StatPearls, National Library of Medicine. Cleveland Clinic, "Telogen Effluvium."

The hair shaft itself is not alive. By the time a strand reaches the surface of the scalp, it is a column of dead, keratinized protein. Nothing you eat or put on it after that point changes its quality. The part of your hair that is actually responding to your diet lives underneath the scalp in the hair follicle, and the follicle only reflects what your body has been dealing with for the past few months.

The Three Nutrients That Actually Move the Needle

A lot of nutrients are discussed in the context of hair, but the evidence is not equal. Iron, protein, and vitamin D are the three that come up most consistently in peer-reviewed dermatology literature on shedding. If your shedding has a nutritional cause, it is almost certainly one of these three, or a combination.

Iron and Ferritin: The Number Most Articles Do Not Name

Iron is required for the rapidly dividing cells in the hair follicle matrix. When iron runs low, your body prioritizes essential functions like oxygen transport and energy metabolism, and hair production drops down the priority list. Follicles move into the resting phase early.

The standard way to measure iron stores is a blood test called ferritin. Here is where most consumer articles stop short: the "normal" ferritin range on your lab report can run as low as twelve or fifteen ng/mL. That is the threshold for not being frankly anemic. Hair has a higher bar. Trichology practice and the dermatology literature both suggest that ferritin of forty ng/mL or higher is associated with healthier hair cycling, with many clinicians targeting a range of forty to seventy ng/mL before expecting shedding to normalize.

This gap matters. A 2022 meta-analysis of thirty-six studies covering more than ten thousand participants found significantly lower ferritin in women with non-scarring hair loss compared with controls. Frank iron-deficiency anemia was present in only about eight percent of hair loss patients. But when ferritin thresholds in the thirty to forty ng/mL range were used, deficiency rates climbed to nearly sixty percent. In other words, many people are being told their iron is "fine" while their hair is telling them otherwise.

Research Note

What to ask your doctor: If you are experiencing increased shedding, ask for a serum ferritin test, not just a basic iron panel. Then ask for the actual number, not just "normal." Bring the forty to seventy ng/mL target range into the conversation. A ferritin of fifteen is technically in range for avoiding anemia but may be insufficient for normal hair cycling.

Ma X, Du H, Hou Y, et al. "The Efficacy of Iron Deficiency and Ferritin in Women with Non-Scarring Alopecia: A Meta-analysis." National Library of Medicine, 2022.

The best food sources of iron are heme iron from animal foods, which the body absorbs two to three times more efficiently than non-heme iron from plants. Lean red meat, oysters, sardines, and liver are the densest sources. Non-heme iron in lentils, beans, tofu, spinach, pumpkin seeds, and fortified grains still counts, especially when paired with vitamin C to improve absorption. If you are vegetarian or vegan and experiencing shedding, iron status is one of the first things worth checking.

Protein: The Building Block Most People Underestimate

Hair is roughly ninety-five percent keratin, a structural protein. The hair follicle is one of the most metabolically active tissues in the body, and it needs a steady supply of amino acids to keep building the protein that becomes the hair shaft. When protein intake drops below adequate for long enough, follicles enter the resting phase early and shedding follows.

The general adult target is 0.8 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day, which works out to about fifty-five grams for a one-hundred-fifty pound adult. That is the floor. Many researchers now argue that 1.0 to 1.2 grams per kilogram is a better target for active adults, people over fifty, and anyone in a period of caloric restriction or recovery. If you are eating significantly below that for weeks or months, your hair is one of the first tissues to register the shortage.

Protein quality matters too. Complete proteins, which contain all nine essential amino acids in appropriate proportions, come from animal foods and soy. Plant proteins are typically complete when combined across the day. Leucine, one of the essential amino acids, is particularly important for the body's protein-building machinery, and intakes below about two to three grams of leucine per meal may blunt the anabolic response. Easy sources include eggs, dairy, fish, lean meat, tofu, tempeh, and lentils.

Vitamin D: Well-Studied, Not Always the Answer

Vitamin D receptors are present in the cells of the hair follicle, and vitamin D signaling appears to be required for normal follicle cycling. Multiple studies have found lower serum vitamin D levels in people with various forms of non-scarring hair loss compared with controls.

Here is where honesty is important. The association is consistent, but the evidence that supplementing vitamin D in someone who is not clearly deficient actually restores hair is weaker. A recent systematic review in the journal Frontiers in Nutrition concluded that conclusive studies demonstrating a benefit of vitamin D administration for correcting hair loss are still lacking. What the evidence supports is testing your level, correcting a real deficiency if one exists, and not expecting vitamin D to do the work of iron or protein.

Good food sources are fatty fish like salmon and mackerel, egg yolks, and fortified dairy. Modest sun exposure supports synthesis in the skin, though the amount varies by latitude, season, and skin tone. Supplementation is reasonable when a blood test confirms low levels, which your doctor can check with a simple 25-hydroxyvitamin D panel.

In forty years behind the chair, I have watched the same pattern play out. A client panics about shedding, buys three supplements, and comes back six months later thinner than when she started. Nine times out of ten, the answer is not in the supplement aisle. It is bloodwork, one or two real deficiencies, and time.

The Other Nutrients Worth Knowing About

Beyond the three above, a handful of other nutrients get discussed in the context of hair. Most of them matter in specific situations rather than as general supplementation targets.

Zinc

Zinc is involved in enzymes that support hair follicle cycling. Zinc deficiency can contribute to shedding, but in the general population the association is modest. Where zinc becomes genuinely important is in people with malabsorption conditions like inflammatory bowel disease, celiac disease, or a history of bariatric surgery, where deficiency rates can reach forty percent. Supplementation in someone who is not deficient carries its own risk: excess zinc can cause copper deficiency, which itself worsens hair loss.

Vitamin B12

The relationship between B12 and hair is weaker than it is often presented. A thorough review in Dermatology and Therapy concluded that the evidence for B12 screening or supplementation specifically for hair loss is not strong enough to recommend routinely. B12 matters most for long-term vegans, people with pernicious anemia, and adults on long-term metformin, where deficiency can build up over years.

Omega-3 Fatty Acids

The evidence on omega-3s and hair is genuinely conflicted. A 2015 trial in women showed a reduction in telogen hair percentage after six months of a supplement containing omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids. A more recent cohort study in men suggested a potentially harmful signal. There is no basis for a general omega-3 hair recommendation across sexes at this point in the research.

Biotin

Biotin deserves a separate word. It is the most heavily marketed hair supplement on the shelf, and the research does not support its use for hair growth in people who are not biotin-deficient. The one high-quality double-blind trial showed no difference between biotin and placebo. A 2024 systematic review concluded that there is no evidence of benefit from biotin supplementation outside of documented deficiency, which is rare in adults without specific risk factors. More on this in the supplements section.

Knowing which nutrients matter is only half the picture. The more useful question is what is creating the deficiency in the first place. Match the pattern to the cause, and the fix usually follows. Supplements: When They Help, When They Do Not, When They Hurt

The hair supplement aisle is enormous and the messaging is consistent: take this, and your hair will get better. The evidence does not support that framing. Here is the whole field at a glance, then the details.

Hair Supplements at a Glance
Helps if deficient Iron, Vitamin D, Protein Strongest evidence when correcting a real, lab-confirmed deficiency. Ineffective and sometimes counterproductive in replete adults.
Context-dependent Zinc, B12, Omega-3 Useful in specific situations (IBD, long-term vegan, long-term metformin, postpartum). Not a routine recommendation.
Weak evidence Collagen, Hair supplement blends Small studies, often industry-funded. Do not expect a large effect. Consider food-first approaches first.
No benefit replete Biotin Placebo-controlled trials show no effect in healthy adults. Can interfere with thyroid and cardiac lab tests.
Can cause shedding Selenium, Vitamin A (retinol) Both cause hair loss at excess intake. Dietary sources are safe and usually sufficient. Skip the supplement.

Why Biotin Does Not Earn Its Shelf Space

Biotin is in almost every hair and nail product on the market. The one high-quality double-blind trial showed no difference between biotin and placebo for hair growth. A 2024 systematic review concluded there is no evidence of benefit outside documented deficiency, which is uncommon in adults who eat a varied diet. Biotin supplementation also interferes with common lab tests including thyroid panels and troponin, which can distort diagnosis of real medical problems.

Why Selenium and Vitamin A Can Make It Worse

This is the category most people do not expect. Both are essential nutrients with narrow therapeutic windows, and both cause hair loss at excess. The NIH Office of Dietary Supplements sets selenium's tolerable upper intake at 400 micrograms per day; above that, selenosis develops, and its most recognizable symptom is, ironically, hair loss. Vitamin A toxicity (specifically from preformed retinol, not beta-carotene in plants) is documented by the World Health Organization as a cause of alopecia. Most Western diets already provide adequate selenium and vitamin A without supplementation.

Why "Hair Supplements" as a Category Is Weakly Supported

A 2026 systematic review in the Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology analyzed fourteen studies of commercial oral hair supplements covering 967 participants. The conclusion was that while specific products show signals of potential benefit, the evidence base is limited by small sample sizes, industry funding, lack of head-to-head comparisons, and short follow-up. Some specific products may help some specific people. The category-level claim that "hair supplements work" is not well supported.

The Rule That Actually Works

Supplement for a deficiency you have confirmed with a blood test. Do not supplement for a deficiency you assume. The first version corrects a real problem. The second version adds cost, sometimes adds risk, and does nothing for the actual cause of your shedding.

Alanazi BS, et al. "Evaluating the Effectiveness of Commercial Oral Supplements for Hair Growth." Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology, 2026.

Why Fixing Your Diet Does Not Feel Like It Is Working

This is the part most people never hear. When you correct a nutritional cause of shedding, you will not see a change in shedding for three to six months. You will not see a change in visible thickness for twelve to eighteen months. The reason is the hair cycle itself: follicles already committed to telogen will finish that phase and shed no matter what you change today.

This lag creates a predictable mistake. Someone notices shedding, adjusts their diet, sees no change at four to six weeks, concludes it did not work, and gives up. The shedding they are seeing was set in motion before their changes had time to register. What they changed today will show up months later.

What to watch instead of the mirror:

  • A rough daily count of hairs in the shower drain or on the pillow over a consistent week each month. Trend over months, not day to day.
  • Ponytail circumference, measured in the same spot, once a month. Small changes are easier to notice this way than in the mirror.
  • A photograph taken in the same place, same lighting, same angle, once a month. Store them in a single album for comparison.

Do not rely on a daily mirror check. Hair looks thinner when it is wet, when you are tired, when the lighting is different, and when you are worried about it. Emotional readings of the mirror are not data.

Key Takeaway Measure in months, not weeks. The hair cycle does not respond to impatience.

When to See a Doctor and What Labs to Request

Not every cause of shedding is nutritional. Before making dietary changes the central strategy, it is worth knowing when to bring a doctor in and what information to gather.

Signs it is probably not just diet:

  • Shedding that localizes to patches, the temples, or the crown rather than a diffuse pattern across the whole scalp.
  • Hair loss accompanied by visible scalp changes: redness, flaking, pain, or scarring.
  • A family history of patterned hair loss (androgenetic alopecia) on either side, especially with gradual thinning over years.
  • Shedding that began suddenly and is progressing rapidly.
  • Symptoms beyond hair: persistent fatigue, cold intolerance, weight changes, mood changes, skin changes, or brittle nails.

If you are seeing any of those, a physician visit comes before a supplement order. Your primary care doctor is a reasonable starting point. A dermatologist can examine the scalp directly. A certified trichologist can assess hair and scalp patterns as a complement to, not a replacement for, medical care.

Labs worth asking for, in the context of shedding that you suspect has a nutritional component:

Lab What It Tells You What To Ask For
Serum ferritin Iron stores, not just current circulating iron The actual number; target 40 to 70 ng/mL for hair
Complete blood count (CBC) Anemia, infection, general blood health Hemoglobin, hematocrit, MCV
25-hydroxyvitamin D Vitamin D status The number; most clinicians target 30 to 50 ng/mL minimum

Vitamin B12 Important if vegan, on metformin, or over 60 Consider methylmalonic acid if B12 is borderline

The reason self-supplementation without testing can backfire is that some nutrients compete with each other and others are frankly toxic at excess. Taking zinc without copper can produce a copper deficiency that worsens hair loss. Taking selenium when you are already replete can cause the shedding you were trying to prevent. A test costs a small copay. A year of the wrong supplement costs more in time than in money.

The Scalp Environment: The Other Half of the Equation

Diet is one lever. The scalp itself is another. Think of it this way: nutrition is the internal supply chain for the hair follicle, and the scalp is the physical environment that the follicle lives in. Both matter, and they work on different timelines.

A healthy scalp has an intact barrier, a balanced microbial ecology, and a low baseline level of inflammation. When any of those is off, follicles perform worse even when nutrition is adequate. Emerging research on the scalp microbiome suggests that the same dietary factors that influence gut health also shape scalp microbial balance, creating a bridge between what you eat and what happens at the follicle locally. That is an area still being mapped out in the research, but it is a useful frame: nutrition works upstream, scalp care works at the site.

While you are making dietary corrections and waiting out the hair cycle, the scalp is the other thing you can actually act on in the meantime. Gentle, sulfate-free cleansing. Minimizing products that irritate or sensitize. Supporting scalp hydration and barrier function. None of that replaces addressing the nutritional cause, but it does keep the local environment as supportive as possible during the months your follicles need to recover.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can poor diet cause hair loss?

Yes, but usually only when the diet creates a specific nutritional deficiency. Iron, protein, and vitamin D are the three nutrients most commonly involved in diet-driven hair shedding. Eating above adequacy does not produce additional hair, but correcting a real deficiency can restore a normal shedding rate within three to six months.

What vitamin deficiency causes hair loss most often?

Iron deficiency, measured through ferritin, is the most common nutritional cause of shedding in premenopausal women. Vitamin D deficiency is the next most studied, though the evidence for treatment is weaker than for iron. Protein insufficiency matters in the context of rapid weight loss or highly restrictive eating patterns.

How long does it take for diet changes to show in hair growth?

The hair cycle means dietary corrections typically take three to six months to appear as a reduced shedding rate. Full cosmetic recovery, where thickness visibly returns, often takes twelve to eighteen months. This is why people who improve their diet and see no change at four weeks often conclude the effort did not work when the real issue is timing.

Do hair supplements actually work?

The evidence base for commercial hair supplements is limited. A 2026 systematic review of fourteen studies found that the overall quality of evidence is low, sample sizes are small, follow-up is short, and many studies are industry-funded. Specific supplements may help when a deficiency is present, but there is no strong evidence that they benefit people who are nutritionally replete.

Is biotin good for hair?

Biotin is one of the most heavily marketed hair supplements, but the research does not support its use in adults without a documented biotin deficiency. A placebo-controlled trial showed no difference between biotin and placebo for hair growth. True biotin deficiency is rare in adults who eat a varied diet.

Does Ozempic cause hair loss?

Some people on GLP-1 medications like semaglutide and tirzepatide experience increased shedding, typically two to four months after rapid weight loss begins. The most likely cause is caloric and protein restriction rather than a direct drug effect. In clinical trials of semaglutide for weight management, hair loss was reported in 2.5 percent of treated patients versus 1.0 percent on placebo, with rates correlating to the degree of weight loss.
