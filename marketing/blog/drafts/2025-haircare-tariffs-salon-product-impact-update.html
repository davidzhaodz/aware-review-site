What Happened: From Liberation Day to the Supreme Court

On April 2, 2025, sweeping tariffs were announced under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA). The plan was aggressive: a 10% baseline tariff on all imports, with higher country-specific rates targeting nations with trade imbalances. The EU faced 20%. Japan, 24%. China, 34%.

Within a week, markets crashed. The S&P 500 dropped over 10%. The administration paused the country-specific rates above 10% for 90 days, though the baseline held. Over the following months, deals were struck with several countries. Japan and the EU negotiated down to 15%. A temporary arrangement with China brought rates to 30%.

In August 2025, the modified country-specific rates resumed. Professional haircare brands from France, Italy, Germany, and Japan were all subject to 15% import tariffs on top of existing duties. Salons and distributors absorbed the increase or passed it through to service pricing.

Then, in February 2026, the legal challenge reached the Supreme Court. In Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump, the Court ruled that the use of emergency powers to impose these tariffs exceeded presidential authority. The decision was sweeping: every IEEPA tariff was invalidated. The government had collected approximately $166 billion from more than 330,000 businesses under tariffs that were now deemed unconstitutional.

Legal Context

The Section 122 replacement: Within days of the Supreme Court ruling, the administration imposed a new 10% global import surcharge under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974. This authority allows temporary tariffs of up to 15% to address balance-of-payments problems. Unlike the IEEPA tariffs, Section 122 is not country-specific. It applies a flat rate to all imports and is authorized for a maximum of 150 days.

White House, "Imposing a Temporary Import Surcharge." February 22, 2026. EY, "US implements global 10% import tariff."

Where Things Stand Now (April 2026)

The current tariff picture is simpler than what came before, but it carries its own uncertainty. Two separate tariff regimes are in play for professional haircare products.

The Section 122 Surcharge (All Imports)

A flat 10% ad valorem surcharge applies to nearly all imported goods entering the United States. It took effect on February 24, 2026, and it expires on July 24, 2026 unless Congress votes to extend it. This surcharge covers professional haircare products from every country, with limited exemptions for energy, pharmaceuticals, critical minerals, and USMCA-qualifying goods from Canada and Mexico.

Section 301 Tariffs (China Only)

These tariffs predate the Liberation Day announcement and were not struck down by the Supreme Court, because they operate under different legal authority. Section 301 tariffs of 25% apply to Chinese imports under HS code 3305 (hair care preparations) and related categories. For salons, this means tools, equipment, hair extensions, foils, gloves, and other supplies sourced from China face both the 10% surcharge and the 25% Section 301 tariff.

Which Salon Brands Are Affected

The impact breaks down by where each brand's products are manufactured, not where the parent company is headquartered. A brand owned by a French company but manufactured in the U.S. would not be subject to import tariffs.

European Brands (10% Surcharge)

Kerastase and L'Oreal Professionnel are manufactured in France. L'Oreal quantified the tariff impact at approximately $95 million across the company, roughly 20 basis points of gross margin. Davines is manufactured in Parma, Italy. CEO Davide Bollati has publicly stated that Davines is absorbing the tariff cost rather than raising prices, and the company's U.S. salon sales grew 10.5% in the first half of 2025 while the broader market fell 3%. Schwarzkopf Professional and Goldwell are manufactured in Germany. These brands now face a 10% surcharge, down from the 15-20% rates they were paying under IEEPA.

Worth noting: Matrix and Redken, both L'Oreal-owned, are manufactured in Canada. Under USMCA, qualifying Canadian-manufactured goods are exempt from the Section 122 surcharge, making these lines relatively tariff-insulated compared to their European counterparts.

Japanese Brands (10% Surcharge)

Milbon manufactures primarily in Japan. Their products carried a 24% IEEPA tariff before the SCOTUS ruling, now reduced to 10% under Section 122. Milbon is widely used in Asian-specialty salons and has been expanding distribution in the U.S., making the tariff reduction particularly relevant for that segment.

Chinese-Sourced Products (35% Combined)

This is where the cost impact remains heaviest. Products manufactured in or sourced from China, including many professional tools (blow dryers, flat irons, shears), disposable supplies (foils, gloves, capes), and virtually all real human hair extensions, face both the 10% Section 122 surcharge and 25% Section 301 tariffs. The total effective rate around 35% is lower than the peak of nearly 60% in late 2025, but still substantial.

Industry Data

Hair extensions face outsized impact: The United States imported $1.6 billion in wigs, weaves, and hairpieces in 2024, the vast majority from China. With combined tariffs around 35%, salons specializing in extensions are among the hardest hit by the remaining tariff structure.

St. Louis American, "Hair stylists among first to feel tariff impact." July 7, 2025.

Domestic Brands (No Tariff Exposure)

Brands that formulate and manufacture in the United States carry no tariff risk. This includes newer professional lines built on domestic supply chains. For salon owners looking to reduce exposure to future tariff uncertainty, domestic sourcing is the only category where the rate is guaranteed to stay at zero regardless of what Congress or the courts decide next.

Tariffs come and go, but the underlying lesson for salon owners stays the same: the less your business depends on any single supply chain, the more resilient it becomes. This is a good moment to diversify, not panic.

How Tariffs Are Actually Hitting Salon Costs

The theoretical rates matter less than what salon owners are actually paying at their distributors. Here is where the impact is being felt across different product categories.

Professional Color Lines

Some of the most trusted color systems are manufactured in Europe. With the current 10% surcharge (down from 15-20%), distributors should be passing through partial relief. If your distributor raised color prices in the second half of 2025 and has not adjusted them downward since the SCOTUS ruling, it is worth asking why. The tariff rate they cited as justification has been cut in half.

Hair Extensions and Human Hair

Real human hair, sourced almost exclusively from abroad, remains the most tariff-exposed category. With China-sourced extensions facing roughly 35% in combined tariffs, salons that specialize in this service have seen material cost increases. One Houston-based stylist reported product costs increasing over 40% since tariffs began.

Tools, Equipment, and Disposables

Blow dryers, diffusers, curling irons, Japanese shears, salon chairs, wash stations, foils, gloves, bowls, and towels are predominantly imported. For high-volume salons, even a 10% increase in disposables adds up across hundreds of client visits per month. The math changes meaningfully when these small per-unit increases compound.

Packaging and Ingredients

Even products formulated in the U.S. may rely on imported packaging components: plastic pumps, glass bottles, metal caps, printed labels. If these are sourced from China or Southeast Asia, the tariff applies to the components. Indie brands and salon private labels with imported packaging have faced difficult decisions about reformulating their packaging supply chain or absorbing the cost.

What Salon Owners Should Do Now

The tariff landscape has been volatile for over a year. Rather than reacting to each headline, the most effective approach is building a supply strategy that works across multiple scenarios.

1. Ask Your Distributor About IEEPA Refunds

The Supreme Court ruling invalidated $166 billion in tariffs collected under IEEPA authority. U.S. Customs and Border Protection is developing a refund process. If your distributor passed through elevated tariff costs between August 2025 and February 2026, ask whether they are pursuing refunds on those duties, and whether savings will be passed back to you. Not all distributors will proactively share this information.

2. Audit Your Supplier Geography

Map your top 10 products by country of manufacture. Anything from China carries the highest combined rate (35%). European and Japanese products face 10%, but that rate expires in July. Domestic products carry zero risk. Knowing your actual exposure helps you make informed decisions about where to consolidate or diversify.

3. Explore Domestic-First Alternatives

U.S.-manufactured brands are not just tariff-insulated. They typically offer faster replenishment, fresher inventory, and more transparency about what goes into each formula. The tariff environment makes the economic case for domestic sourcing stronger than it has been in years. Ask your distributors which professional lines are made in the U.S.

4. Plan for the July 2026 Expiration

The current 10% surcharge expires on July 24, 2026. Three scenarios are plausible: Congress extends it, the administration finds alternative authority, or the surcharge lapses entirely. Do not lock into long-term supply commitments based solely on the current rate. Build flexibility into your purchasing timeline so you can adjust as the picture clarifies in Q3.

5. Be Transparent with Clients

If tariff-driven costs have already led you to adjust service prices, communicate the reason clearly. Clients understand that imported products cost more when tariffs are in play. Transparency builds trust, particularly when you can point to specific policy changes driving the adjustment. For guidance on structuring these pricing conversations, see our salon pricing strategy guide.

6. Join or Form a Cooperative Buying Group

Pool orders with neighboring salons to negotiate volume pricing from distributors. Bulk purchasing on color, disposables, and backbar products can offset per-unit tariff increases. This approach works especially well for commodity supplies like foils, gloves, and towels where brand differentiation matters less.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the current tariff rates on professional haircare products?

As of April 2026, a flat 10% Section 122 surcharge applies to all imported goods, including professional haircare products from Europe and Japan. Products from China also face a separate 25% Section 301 tariff on top of the surcharge. The Section 122 surcharge is set to expire on July 24, 2026.

Were the Liberation Day tariffs overturned?

Yes. In February 2026, the Supreme Court ruled in Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump that the use of IEEPA emergency powers to impose the Liberation Day tariffs was unconstitutional. The original country-specific rates (20% on EU, 24% on Japan, 34% on China) are no longer in effect. They were replaced by a 10% Section 122 surcharge.

Can salon owners get refunds on tariffs already paid?

Potentially, yes. The government collected approximately $166 billion from over 330,000 businesses under the IEEPA tariffs that the Supreme Court found unconstitutional. U.S. Customs is working on a system to process refunds. Salon owners and distributors who paid elevated IEEPA rates between August 2025 and February 2026 should check with their importers or customs brokers about refund eligibility.

Which professional haircare brands are affected by tariffs?

Major professional brands manufactured overseas face the 10% Section 122 surcharge. This includes Kerastase and L'Oreal Professionnel (France), Davines (Italy), Milbon (Japan), Schwarzkopf Professional (Germany), and Goldwell (Germany/Japan). Tools, equipment, and hair extensions sourced from China face both Section 122 (10%) and Section 301 (25%) tariffs.

What happens when the Section 122 surcharge expires in July 2026?

The Section 122 surcharge is authorized for 150 days and expires on July 24, 2026. Congress could vote to extend it, the administration could seek alternative legal authority, or the surcharge could simply lapse. Salon owners should plan for multiple scenarios and avoid locking into long-term supply decisions based on the current rate alone.
