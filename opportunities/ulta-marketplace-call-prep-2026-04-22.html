Language Discipline

Do Say

  • "We'll need to evaluate this against our current channel priorities."
  • "Can you send over the deck and sample agreement for us to review internally?"
  • "We'd want to loop in our co-founder and come back with a clear answer."
  • "What's the right cadence to stay in touch if timing shifts later this year?"
  • "What's the incentive if we're asking directly?"

Don't Say

  • "We're considering this for May."
  • "We really want to make this work."
  • "What do we need to do to get in?"
  • "We're excited to launch" (before anything is evaluated).
  • Anything confirming urgency on your side.

Pushback Scenarios

Recruiters are trained to close. Here are the plays they'll run and how you hold your line without burning the relationship.

Push 1 — Manufactured Urgency
We'd want to move fast — slots in this cohort are limited.
What they want
A rushed yes driven by artificial scarcity.
"Understood. We'd need to do proper diligence and loop in our co-founder — that's not a decision we'd make on a compressed timeline. If this cohort has limited space, we'd rather wait for the next window than rush into something we can't execute well. What does the onboarding cadence typically look like after this one?"
Why this works: Reframes "scarcity" as "cohort timing" and signals you operate at a level where rushed decisions aren't acceptable. Forces them to relax the pressure, sweeten the terms, or confirm a future window (which always exists).
Push 2 — FOMO / Social Proof
Brands like [X] wish they'd entered sooner — they're crushing it.
What they want
Anecdotal success stories to trigger fear of missing out.
"That's helpful context. Can you share what the path to that performance looked like — starting inventory, media spend, timeline to break-even? We want to calibrate a realistic ramp for our stage, not just the ceiling."
Why this works: Turns anecdote into data. If they can't answer specifically, the story is fluff. If they can, you walk away with real benchmarks.
Push 3 — Dismiss the Salon Risk
We can help you navigate the salon channel concern — we've seen brands manage this.
What they want
To dismiss your single biggest risk with vague reassurance.
"I'd genuinely like to hear how. When brands like Olaplex had salon trust erosion after retail expansion, what did the brands who avoided that do differently? What levers worked?"
Why this works: Forces specificity. If they cite salon-exclusive SKUs or phased rollout, you've learned something real. If they hand-wave, you've confirmed they don't have a playbook — so you can't rely on them for channel protection.
Push 4 — Dangle the Carrot
The onboarding incentive is materially valuable — I really think you should consider it.
What they want
Get you to commit on the incentive alone without evaluating fit.
"Appreciate that — and that's exactly why we want to understand the full picture before deciding. An incentive only matters if the channel fit is right. What's the incentive if we're asking directly?"
Why this works: Flips the script. You're saying "name the number." Makes them reveal the actual value of the carrot.
Push 5 — Pin a Date on You
What's your timeline — when could you be ready?
What they want
A commitment date they can circle back on.
"We're focused on our salon channel and C.O. Bigelow launch this quarter. Retail expansion is a conversation we'd want to revisit when we've got the right inventory runway and fulfillment structure. That's a 12-18 month horizon for us, not Q2."
Why this works: Clear "not now" without burning the bridge. Names a realistic future window. Signals operational self-awareness — which also signals you're serious, not sloppy.
Push 6 — Close the Loop Fast
We usually need to hear back within a week on interest level.
What they want
A yes/no on a compressed timeline before you can properly evaluate.
"We can absolutely get back to you within a week on where this sits on our priority list. Sending materials over helps — a deck, sample agreement, and a couple of brand case studies. I'll review with our team and come back with a clear read."
Why this works: Agrees to a low-stakes deliverable (a response) without agreeing to a high-stakes one (a yes). And you're asking for their materials — free intel.
Push 7 — Gauge Your Options
Who else are you talking to / what's competing for your attention?
What they want
Measure urgency and competition to price accordingly.
"We've got a few prestige retail conversations in different stages — I won't name names. The honest answer is we're being selective because professional-channel protection is non-negotiable for us, and every new channel has to pass that test."
Why this works: Credible (you have options), principled (not desperate), and you've named your actual constraint — which tells them what to address if they want to win.
