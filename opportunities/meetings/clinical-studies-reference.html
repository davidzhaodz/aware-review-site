Clinical Studies 101 - The Basics

A primer for someone who has never been involved in clinical research. This is educational content designed to build foundational understanding.

What is a Clinical Study?

A systematic investigation designed to evaluate a product's safety, efficacy, or mechanism of action on human subjects. It follows a structured protocol with defined measurements and statistical analysis.

Why Run Clinical Studies?

  • Claims substantiation - Back up marketing claims with data
  • Regulatory compliance - Meet FDA/FTC standards
  • Marketing credibility - "Clinically proven" positioning
  • IP protection - Documented efficacy for formulations
  • B2B sales - Distributors and retailers value clinical data

Clinical Study vs Clinical Trial

Clinical Trial: Typically implies drug or medical device testing with FDA oversight and rigorous regulatory requirements.

Clinical Study: Broader term that includes cosmetic efficacy studies. AWARE would likely run a "clinical study" or "clinical efficacy study" since scalp care products are cosmetics, not drugs.

IRB (Institutional Review Board)

An independent ethics committee that must approve any study involving human subjects. Ensures participant safety, informed consent, and ethical research practices.

Required even for cosmetic studies. No exceptions.

Key Insight for AWARE

As a cosmetic product making efficacy claims (scalp health, microbiome balance, hair fall reduction), AWARE needs clinical studies to substantiate marketing language and support HSA/FSA eligibility applications. The goal is not FDA drug approval - it's credible, data-backed claims for B2B and DTC marketing.

Types of Studies AWARE Should Know About

Different study designs serve different purposes and budgets. Understanding the spectrum helps you choose the right approach for your claims and resources.

In-Vitro Studies

What: Lab tests on cells or tissues. No human subjects.

Example: Testing AWAREBIO Complex ingredients on cultured skin cells to measure barrier protein expression.

Pros: Cheapest, fastest, controlled environment.

Cons: Least credible for marketing claims. "Lab results may not reflect real-world use."

Instrumental Studies

What: Use devices to measure skin/scalp parameters on human subjects.

Example: Measuring scalp hydration with a corneometer, or barrier function with a TEWL device.

Pros: Moderate cost, objective data, common in cosmetics industry.

Cons: Requires equipment access, specialized technicians.

Consumer Perception Studies

What: Surveys and questionnaires from real users.

Example: "90% of users reported shinier hair after 4 weeks."

Pros: Easy to execute, real user feedback, compelling for marketing.

Cons: Considered weakest evidence. Subjective. Prone to placebo effect.

Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs)

What: Gold standard. Participants randomly assigned to product or placebo/control group. Often blinded (participants and/or evaluators don't know who gets what).

Pros: Most credible, strongest claims, publication-ready.

Cons: Most expensive, longest timeline, complex execution.

Split-Scalp Studies

What: Each participant uses product on one side of scalp, control on the other. Acts as their own control.

Example: AWARE shampoo on left side, placebo shampoo on right side. Compare scalp measurements between sides.

Pros: Highly relevant for hair/scalp products. Reduces individual variability.

Cons: Product migration (hard to keep sides separate), limited to certain endpoints.

Open-Label Studies

What: Everyone knows what they're using. No blinding.

Pros: Simpler, cheaper, faster. Good for exploratory work.

Cons: Less rigorous, more placebo effect, weaker claims.

Pilot Studies

What: Small-scale preliminary studies (10-20 people) to test feasibility and get initial data before committing to a full study.

Pros: Low cost, identifies issues early, informs full study design.

Cons: Not powered for statistical significance. Can't make strong claims from pilot data alone.

Study Design for Scalp/Hair Products

What would an actual AWARE clinical study look like? Here's the practical structure and measurements you'd likely use.

Typical Study Structure for Scalp Care:

  • Duration: 4-12 weeks (8 weeks is the sweet spot for most scalp/hair endpoints)
  • Participants: 30-60 people (minimum 30 for statistical significance)
  • Design: Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled OR split-scalp
  • Visits: Baseline (day 0), midpoint (week 4), final (week 8), optional follow-up (week 12)

Common Endpoints for Scalp Products

TEWL (Trans-Epidermal Water Loss)

What it measures: Skin barrier function. Water evaporating through the skin.

Device: Tewameter probe (non-invasive).

Result: Lower TEWL = better barrier function = healthier scalp.

Relevant for: Barrier repair claims, microbiome support claims.

Corneometry

What it measures: Skin hydration levels.

Device: Corneometer probe (capacitance-based measurement).

Result: Higher reading = more hydrated scalp.

Relevant for: Hyaluronic acid claims, hydration claims, dry scalp improvement.

Sebumometry

What it measures: Sebum (oil) production on scalp.

Device: Sebumeter (tape collection method).

Result: Can show reduction in excess oil or normalization of sebum levels.

Relevant for: Oily scalp claims, sebum regulation claims.

ASFS (Adherent Scalp Flaking Score)

What it measures: Clinical grading of dandruff/flaking severity.

Method: Dermatologist scores scalp flaking on standardized scale (0-4 or 0-10).

Result: Reduction in ASFS score = less flaking.

Relevant for: Anti-dandruff claims, scalp health claims.

Hair Pull Test

What it measures: Number of hairs that come out with gentle standardized pull.

Method: Grasp ~60 hairs, pull gently, count hairs that shed.

Result: Fewer hairs pulled = less shedding/breakage.

Relevant for: Hair fall reduction claims, strengthening claims.

Phototrichogram

What it measures: Hair density, thickness, growth rate via photography and image analysis.

Method: Shave small area, photograph over time, analyze with software.

Result: Can show increased density or thickness.

Relevant for: Hair growth claims, density improvement claims.

16S rRNA Sequencing

What it measures: DNA analysis of scalp microbiome composition (which bacteria are present).

Method: Swab scalp, extract DNA, sequence bacterial genes.

Result: Can show shifts toward beneficial bacteria or away from dysbiosis.

Relevant for: Microbiome balance claims, AWAREBIO Complex mechanism.

D-Squame Tape Stripping

What it measures: Collects skin cells for analysis of barrier proteins, lipids, and cellular health.

Method: Adhesive tape strips off outer skin layers for lab analysis.

Result: Can quantify barrier protein levels, lipid composition.

Relevant for: Barrier function claims, cellular health claims.

Subject Self-Assessment

What it measures: Participant-reported outcomes on itching, flaking, appearance, satisfaction.

Method: Standardized questionnaires (Likert scales, yes/no, visual analog scales).

Result: Subjective improvement data ("90% reported less itching").

Relevant for: Consumer perception claims, quality of life improvements.

Dermatologist Global Assessment

What it measures: Expert clinical grading of overall scalp condition.

Method: Board-certified dermatologist scores scalp health on standardized scale.

Result: Professional validation of improvement.

Relevant for: Clinical improvement claims, professional endorsement.

The Study Process - Timeline & Steps

What actually happens from concept to published results? Here's the realistic timeline for a typical scalp care clinical study.

4-8 WEEKS

Phase 1: Study Design & Planning

  • Define objectives and endpoints (what are you trying to prove?)
  • Write study protocol - detailed document specifying everything: participants, procedures, measurements, timeline
  • Statistical analysis plan - how many participants needed, what statistical tests will be used
  • Select study site (university, CRO, or dermatology practice)
  • IRB submission and approval (2-6 weeks alone - ethics review takes time)
2-4 WEEKS

Phase 2: Participant Recruitment

  • Define inclusion/exclusion criteria (age range, scalp condition requirements, no other active treatments)
  • Recruit via ads, databases, university student pools
  • Screen participants (medical history, scalp examination, eligibility check)
  • Informed consent process (participants sign legal documents understanding risks and procedures)
  • Baseline assessments (initial measurements before treatment starts)
4-12 WEEKS

Phase 3: Treatment Period

  • Participants use product per protocol (controlled regimen - exact usage instructions)
  • Regular check-ins (compliance monitoring, adverse event tracking)
  • Midpoint measurements (week 4 or midpoint visit)
  • Final measurements (end of treatment period)
  • Collect all data (instrumental readings, questionnaires, photos, samples)
4-12 WEEKS

Phase 4: Data Analysis & Publication

  • Statistical analysis by independent statistician (confirms results are significant, not due to chance)
  • Manuscript preparation (write up methods, results, discussion)
  • Peer review submission to dermatology journals (optional but adds credibility)
  • Publication timeline: 3-12 months for peer review process
  • Clinical study report delivered to sponsor (AWARE)

Total Realistic Timeline: 6-12 months from study start to published results. Plan for 9 months as a conservative estimate. AI-GENERATED

IRB approval and peer review are the two unpredictable variables - they can add weeks or months depending on committee schedules and reviewer feedback.

Important Caveats - Read This Carefully

  • You can only claim what the data shows. If the study doesn't show hair density improvement, you can't claim it. Period.
  • "Clinically tested" is weaker than "clinically proven." "Tested" means you ran a study. "Proven" means the study showed statistically significant positive results.
  • Results might be mixed. Study may show some endpoints improved (hydration) but others didn't (hair density). Be prepared for this reality.
  • Negative results are still valuable. They tell you what to focus on, what to reformulate, or what claims to avoid. Don't view negative results as failure.
  • Publication isn't guaranteed. Peer review can reject papers. Don't count on publication for commercial timeline - use the clinical study report regardless.

Key Terms Glossary

Quick reference for clinical study terminology you'll encounter in discussions with researchers, CROs, and partners.

PI (Principal Investigator)
Lead researcher responsible for the study. Usually a board-certified dermatologist or PhD with expertise in the field. Signs off on protocol, results, and publications.
IRB (Institutional Review Board)
Independent ethics committee that reviews and approves research involving human subjects. Ensures participant safety, informed consent, and ethical compliance. Required for all human studies, including cosmetic efficacy studies.
RCT (Randomized Controlled Trial)
Gold standard study design where participants are randomly assigned to treatment or control groups. Reduces bias and allows causal conclusions about efficacy.
Blinding
Single-blind means participants don't know if they're using active product or placebo. Double-blind means neither participants nor evaluators know. Reduces placebo effect and bias.
Placebo / Control
Comparison group. Placebo is an inactive product that looks identical to the test product. Control can also be "standard of care" (existing treatment) or "vehicle" (product without active ingredients).
Endpoint
What you're measuring in the study. Primary endpoint is the main outcome (e.g., hair fall reduction). Secondary endpoints are additional measurements (e.g., scalp hydration, microbiome composition).
p-value
Statistical significance measure. p < 0.05 means the result has less than 5% probability of being due to chance alone. Standard threshold for claiming statistical significance.
Inclusion / Exclusion Criteria
Rules for who can participate. Inclusion: must meet these (e.g., age 25-55, dry scalp condition). Exclusion: cannot participate if these apply (e.g., pregnant, using other scalp treatments, scalp infection).
Adverse Event
Any negative health event that occurs during the study, whether or not it's related to the product. Must be documented and reported. Examples: scalp irritation, allergic reaction, unrelated illness.
Informed Consent
Legal document participants sign before enrolling. Explains study procedures, risks, benefits, and their rights. Required by IRB. Participant must understand and voluntarily agree.
TEWL (Trans-Epidermal Water Loss)
Measurement of water evaporating through the skin. Lower TEWL = better skin barrier function. Measured with a Tewameter device. Common endpoint for scalp/skin barrier claims.
Corneometry
Measurement of skin hydration levels using electrical capacitance. Higher reading = more hydrated skin. Non-invasive probe measurement. Common for hydration claims.
Phototrichogram
Photography-based analysis of hair density, thickness, and growth rate. Involves shaving a small area, photographing over time, and using image analysis software to quantify hair parameters.
CRO (Contract Research Organization)
Company that conducts clinical studies for hire. Examples: TKL Research, BioScreen, Canfield Scientific. Faster and more flexible than academic institutions, but more expensive.
GCP (Good Clinical Practice)
International ethical and scientific quality standards for designing, conducting, recording, and reporting clinical studies. Ensures data credibility and participant protection.
Publication Bias
Tendency for studies with positive results to be published more often than those with negative or null results. Be aware when reviewing published literature - absence of published negative results doesn't mean all studies were positive.
Statistical Power
Probability that the study will detect a real effect if one exists. Depends on sample size, effect size, and variability. Underpowered studies (too few participants) may miss real effects.
Protocol Amendment
Formal change to the study protocol after IRB approval. Requires IRB review and approval. Examples: changing endpoint, extending duration, modifying participant criteria. Adds time and cost.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Practical advice for first-timers based on what typically goes wrong in cosmetic clinical studies.

Skipping the IRB

Mistake: Thinking "it's just a cosmetic study, we don't need IRB approval."

Reality: IRB approval is ethically and legally required for any human subjects research, including cosmetic efficacy studies.

Consequence: Data is not credible, cannot be published, and may expose you to legal liability.

Designing to "Prove" a Claim

Mistake: Designing the study specifically to get a predetermined result rather than testing objectively.

Reality: Good science tests hypotheses objectively. Cherry-picking endpoints or manipulating design to force positive results undermines credibility.

Better approach: Test what you genuinely believe the product does, and be prepared to adjust claims based on what the data actually shows.

Underfunding Participant Compensation

Mistake: Offering too little compensation to save costs ($25 for 8-week study with 4 visits).

Reality: Low compensation leads to high dropout rates. High dropout = incomplete data = useless study.

Guideline: $50-100 per visit is standard. For 8-week study with 3 visits, budget $150-300 per participant.

Rushing Recruitment

Mistake: Pressuring study site to enroll participants too quickly, leading to poor screening.

Reality: Wrong participants (don't meet criteria, not compliant, poor attendance) ruin data quality.

Better approach: Allow proper screening time. Quality over speed.

Making Claims Beyond the Data

Mistake: "Clinically proven to improve scalp health" when study only measured hydration, not overall health.

Reality: You can only claim what was directly measured and statistically proven.

Legal risk: FTC can challenge unsupported claims. Stick to specific language: "Shown to improve scalp hydration by X% in clinical study."

Forgetting Publication Timeline

Mistake: Assuming you can use results for marketing immediately after study ends.

Reality: If aiming for publication, peer review takes 3-12 months. Journal may require embargo on commercial use until publication.

Solution: Clarify upfront whether you need immediate commercial use or can wait for publication. Adjust strategy accordingly.

Assuming Academic = Business Timeline

Mistake: Expecting university researchers to move at startup speed.

Reality: Academic timelines are slower due to teaching schedules, committee approvals, grad student availability.

Planning: Add 50% to timeline estimates when working with universities. If speed is critical, consider CRO instead.

No Pilot Study

Mistake: Jumping straight to expensive full RCT without testing study design first.

Reality: Pilot studies (10-20 participants, 4-6 weeks) identify issues with protocol, endpoints, or product use before you commit $50K+.

ROI: $15K pilot can save $30K+ by catching design flaws early.

The Biggest Mistake: Assuming Positive Results

The most common failure is designing your marketing, packaging, and investor pitch around assumed positive results before the study is complete.

Reality check: Studies can show mixed results, null results, or even negative results. This doesn't mean the product is bad - it might mean the endpoint was wrong, the timeline was too short, or the formulation needs adjustment.

Smart approach: Have a Plan B for marketing and claims if results aren't as strong as hoped. Focus on what did work rather than overpromising on what might work.

Questions to Ask Any Study Partner

Pre-written questions to use when evaluating universities, CROs, or other study partners. These help you assess capabilities, timelines, and fit for AWARE's needs.

  1. What's your experience with cosmetic or scalp care clinical studies specifically? (Ask for examples of similar studies they've conducted)
  2. What endpoints do you recommend for our product claims? (They should understand scalp/hair measurements and be able to suggest appropriate endpoints based on your formulation)
  3. What sample size do you need for statistical significance? (Should be based on power calculation, typically 30-60 participants for cosmetic studies)
  4. What's your realistic timeline from protocol design to published results? (Get specific milestones: IRB approval, recruitment, treatment period, analysis, publication)
  5. Who handles IRB submission and approval? (Clarify if they manage this or if you need to arrange it separately)
  6. What participant demographics can you recruit? (Age range, ethnicity, hair types, scalp conditions - make sure they can access your target population)
  7. Do you handle statistical analysis in-house or outsource? (In-house is usually faster and easier to coordinate)
  8. Can we use results for marketing claims before publication? (Critical if you need immediate commercial use - some journals require embargo)
  9. What are the total costs, including any hidden fees? (IRB fees, protocol amendments, publication costs, data analysis - get all-in pricing)
  10. What happens if results are negative or mixed? (Do they still deliver the report? Can you publish negative results? What's the process?)
  11. Can we start with a pilot study before committing to the full trial? (De-risks investment - see if they offer smaller exploratory studies)
  12. Do you help with manuscript preparation and journal submission? (If publication is important, confirm they provide this support or if it costs extra)

Bonus Questions for University Partners

  • How does your academic calendar affect study timelines? (Summer breaks, finals periods, semester transitions can delay studies)
  • Can we use your institution name in marketing materials? (e.g., "Validated at USC Keck School of Medicine")
  • What are your publication requirements? (Some universities require results be submitted for publication as part of collaboration agreement)
  • Who owns the data and IP from the study? (Usually you do, but clarify upfront to avoid surprises)

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Guaranteed positive results - No legitimate researcher can guarantee outcomes before the study is run
  • No IRB mentioned - Ethical research requires IRB approval, period
  • Vague timeline - "A few months" is not a timeline. Get specific milestones
  • Unwillingness to share past studies - Reputable partners should have case studies or references
  • Pressure to commit immediately - Good partners understand this is a significant investment and give you time to evaluate

Turning Results Into Marketing Claims

A clinical study gives you data, but what you can actually SAY in marketing depends on FDA/FTC rules, the study design, and the strength of your results. This is one of the most important sections to understand before commissioning a study. [AI-GENERATED]

The Core Rule: Cosmetic vs. Drug Claims

The FDA draws a hard line between cosmetic claims (how the product affects appearance) and drug claims (treating, curing, or preventing disease). A clinical study does NOT automatically let you say anything you want. In fact, strong clinical data can actually push your product into drug territory if you overstate your claims.

  • Cosmetic claim (FDA safe zone): "Clinically shown to improve scalp hydration by 47% in 8 weeks"
  • Drug claim (triggers FDA regulation): "Clinically proven to treat seborrheic dermatitis"
  • The difference: one describes an effect on appearance/condition. The other claims to treat a disease.

What Clinical Data Lets You Say

Safe Cosmetic Claims (With Data)

  • "Clinically shown to improve scalp hydration by X%" - if corneometry data shows this
  • "Clinically tested to reduce visible flaking" - if ASFS scores improve significantly
  • "Shown to strengthen hair and reduce breakage" - if hair pull test supports it
  • "Improves scalp barrier function" - if TEWL measurements confirm
  • "Supports a balanced scalp microbiome" - if 16S rRNA data shows improvement
  • "Published research demonstrates improved scalp health markers"
  • "USC-validated scalp care technology"

Drug Claims - NEVER Say These (Even With Data)

  • "Treats seborrheic dermatitis" - treating disease = drug
  • "Cures hair loss" - cure = drug claim
  • "Prevents alopecia" - preventing disease = drug
  • "Clinically proven to regrow hair" - implies drug-level efficacy
  • "Anti-fungal" or "anti-microbial treatment" - medical action = drug

These require OTC drug registration, Drug Facts panel, and FDA compliance, regardless of what your study shows.

FTC Substantiation Standard

The FTC requires "competent and reliable scientific evidence" to back any efficacy claim. In practice this means: [AI-GENERATED - based on FTC guidance patterns]

  • At least one well-designed, controlled study - a survey or anecdotes are not enough
  • Statistically significant results (p < 0.05) - the result must be unlikely due to chance
  • Claims must not overstate the data - if 35% improvement, don't round up to "nearly 50%"
  • Study methodology must be appropriate - the study design matches the claim being made
  • A pilot study with 15 people is NOT sufficient for broad marketing claims. A well-designed RCT with 30-50 participants generally IS adequate substantiation

  • The study endpoints should directly map to the claims you want to make

After the Study - Upgrade Claims

  • Replace vague language with specific, data-backed claims
  • Add "clinically shown" or "published research" language
  • Include specific percentages and timeframes from results
  • Reference the institution: "Validated at USC Keck School of Medicine"
  • Update product pages, salon materials, and B2B pitch decks

What If Results Are Mixed?

  • You can ONLY claim what the data shows - no cherry-picking
  • If hydration improves but shedding doesn't, claim hydration only
  • "Clinically tested" is still valuable even with mixed results
  • Negative data points to reformulation opportunities
  • Even mixed results are better than no data at all

The Bottom Line

A clinical study gives you a floor of defensible claims, not a ceiling of anything you want to say. The study data determines exactly what language is safe, and you must stay within those bounds. Design your endpoints carefully - they define what claims become available to you.

This is also a great question to ask Dr. Pachorek: "What claim language would a study at USC allow us to use?" - it signals that you understand the regulatory landscape and aren't looking for a rubber stamp.
