Study Design Options

Study Design Spectrum - Know Your Options

The study design you choose determines what claims you can make AND affects AWARE's regulatory classification. These are not just academic distinctions - they have real business consequences. [AI-GENERATED]

Tier 1: Cosmetic Efficacy Study

Tests: Does AWARE improve measurable scalp/hair parameters in general-population subjects?

  • Participants: "People with self-reported scalp concerns" (no diagnosis)
  • Endpoints: hydration, barrier, microbiome, sebum, shedding
  • Claims: "Clinically shown to improve scalp hydration by X%"
  • AWARE stays classified as a cosmetic
  • Cost: $25-50K | Duration: 4-8 weeks

Tier 2: Condition-Adjacent Study ★ Recommended

Tests: Does AWARE improve symptoms in people who HAVE scalp issues, without diagnosing/treating a disease?

  • Participants: "Adults with mild-to-moderate scalp dryness and flaking" (symptom language, not disease diagnosis)
  • Same endpoints, but measured on people with real symptoms
  • Claims: "Clinically shown to reduce visible flaking by X% in subjects with dry, flaky scalps"
  • AWARE stays classified as a cosmetic
  • Cost: $30-60K | Duration: 8 weeks

Why this is the sweet spot: Gets condition-relevant data without triggering drug classification. Strongest evidence base that keeps AWARE in cosmetic territory.

Tier 3: OTC Drug Efficacy Study

Tests: Does AWARE treat a diagnosed condition (dandruff, alopecia)?

  • Participants: Diagnosed with seborrheic dermatitis, androgenetic alopecia, etc.
  • Requires ICD-10 codes, dermatologist diagnosis at screening
  • Claims: "Treats dandruff" or "reduces hair loss"
  • AWARE becomes an OTC drug - Drug Facts panel, FDA registration, cGMP
  • Cost: $75-200K+ | Duration: 8-16 weeks

This is a different business. Only pursue if AWARE wants to reformulate and register as an OTC drug product.

The Critical Distinction - Symptoms vs. Diseases:
You CAN study and claim improvement in symptoms (dryness, flaking, itching, shedding) as a cosmetic.
You CANNOT claim to treat the underlying disease (dandruff, dermatitis, alopecia) without drug classification.
"Reduces flaking" = cosmetic claim. "Treats dandruff" = drug claim. Same symptom, very different regulatory implications.

What Not to Say
Don't call AWARE a "treatment" or "medical product"
AWARE is a cosmetic, not a drug. Framing it as medical invites regulatory scrutiny. Say: "scalp wellness ritual" or "cosmetic efficacy study." Never: "treatment," "cure," "clinically proven" (until you have data).
Don't overstate the DHT/hair loss claims
Current website claims about DHT blocking and hair fall reduction are not substantiated. A physician advisor will notice. If it comes up, say: "That's exactly why we want the clinical data - to validate or refine those claims based on actual evidence."
Don't ask him to run the study personally
He's internal medicine, not dermatology. He likely doesn't do clinical research. Ask for introductions and guidance, not personal involvement in the research.
Don't promise specific study outcomes
Never say "we expect the study to prove X." Say: "We're confident in the product and want rigorous data to support that confidence - whatever the results show." Researchers respect intellectual honesty.
Don't discuss the regulatory risk in detail
The unsubstantiated claims issue is an internal priority. Don't lead with "we have an FDA problem." Frame the study as proactive credibility-building, not damage control.
