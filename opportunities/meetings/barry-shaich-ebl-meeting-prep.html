Distributor Economics - Know These Cold
Direct to Salon (Current)
~50% Margin
On a $100 MSRP product, AWARE sells direct to salon at ~$50 wholesale. No middleman. Full margin. Requires AWARE to handle all sales, education, and fulfillment.
Through Distributor
~33-38% Margin
Distributor buys at ~$32.50-$37.50 (33-38% of MSRP). They sell to salon at ~$50. Distributor takes ~$12-17 per unit as their margin. Standard indie distributor take is 15-20% on top of their cost.
Margin Breakeven Volume
~200 units/month
At <200 units/month, the margin hit (~$12-17/unit) isn't justified by the volume gain. At 200+ units across a distributor's territory, total revenue exceeds what direct-to-salon could achieve at current scale.
Barry's Intro Fee
Commission Model Ideal
If commission-based: 5-10% of first-year distributor orders is standard for a connector. On $100K of Bassett orders year 1, that's $5-10K to Barry. Retainer should only apply if he's actively managing the relationship ongoing.
The Margin Math In Full
Scenario: Bassett places AWARE with 50 salons in their territory. Average salon reorder: 10 units/month at $50 wholesale. That's $500/salon/month, or $25,000/month total ($300K/year). At 33% margin (distributor model), AWARE nets ~$10-12K/month from Bassett alone - less per unit than direct, but volume-driven. Compare: 50 new direct salons at the same rate = $25K/month at 50% margin ($12.5K net). The distributor path gives you slightly less per unit but costs far less in sales effort. At scale, the distributor model wins. The question is whether Barry can actually get you to 50 Bassett salons.

David / Tina

Why this works: Reframes equity as a compliment (not a rejection), immediately offers an alternative that's performance-aligned. Commission on distributor introductions means Barry only earns when he delivers real value. It also naturally caps your exposure.
Weaker Response
David / Tina

Why this fails: You've opened a negotiation on equity by asking "how much." Now you're in a conversation about cap tables and legal structure instead of whether Barry can actually deliver distributor placements. The correct move is to close the equity door and open the performance door.

Why this fails: You've positioned yourself as the student and Barry as the expert evaluating you. This flips the power dynamic completely. He now has license to prescribe his services as the solution to your "readiness" gap. You came in with leverage (he reached out to you) and just gave it away.

Why this fails: You've just undermined your own strategy in front of someone who sells the alternative. Saying "maybe you're right" gives Barry the opening to spend the rest of the meeting selling DTC services. You came for distributor intros and left with an Amazon proposal.

Post-Meeting Action Playbook
Why Speed Matters
The 24 hours after this meeting are the highest-leverage window. Barry is warm, the conversation is fresh, and specific follow-up signals that AWARE operates like a real business. Generic "great to meet you" emails lose the momentum. Every template below is designed to move something forward or close something cleanly.
Outcome A: Warm Bassett Relationship - He Can Intro This Month
Same-Day Action: Capture the Commitment
Before the call ends: get a specific next step on the calendar. "Can we say you'll make that intro to Ward by [date]? And then we reconnect the week after to debrief?" A verbal commitment with a date is far more durable than "I'll be in touch."
Follow-Up Email - Outcome A (Send Within 2 Hours)
Barry,

Great conversation today - really appreciate you making the time. Based on what we discussed, here's how I'm thinking about next steps:

Your side: Introduction to Ward Bassett by [DATE]. We're happy for you to frame it however makes sense given your relationship - a quick email or a three-way call, whatever feels natural.

Our side: We'll put together a concise brand summary for Ward - sell-through data, margin structure, education overview. We'll have that to you by [DATE] so you can share it ahead of or alongside the intro.

We then reconnect the week of [DATE] to debrief on how the intro landed and talk about next steps with Bassett.

Does that timeline work for you? And does Ward prefer email or phone for a first reach-out?

Looking forward to moving this forward.
Outcome B: He Needs to Reconnect / Asks for Proposal Time
Give a Deadline or It Disappears
"I'll put something together" without a deadline becomes "let me know when you have capacity." Give him a week - it's reasonable and it keeps the momentum alive. If he misses it, that's information too.
Follow-Up Email - Outcome B (Send Within 2 Hours)
Barry,

Thanks for the conversation today. It's clear you have deep relationships in this channel and we think there's a real fit.

Based on our discussion, it would be helpful to see a short written scope from you: which 2-3 distributors you'd engage, your relationship with each, the timeline, and how you'd structure the engagement. That gives us something concrete to work from on our end.

Could you send that over by [ONE WEEK FROM TODAY]? We want to move quickly - we have a few other distributor paths we're exploring and we'd rather work with someone who knows Ward than go in cold.

Thanks again.
Note: "We have other paths" Is True and Worth Saying
This is not a bluff - you do have other distributor paths you can pursue (cold outreach, CanRad referrals, trade show introductions). Mentioning it creates urgency without being aggressive. Barry's value is the warm intro; if he delays, that value decreases.
Outcome C: DTC-Only Value / Not a Current Fit

Really enjoyed the conversation today. Your track record in professional beauty is impressive, and it's clear you've built something real with EBL.

On the Pass Email: Short Is Kind
Don't over-explain a pass. Long explanations invite rebuttals. The shorter the pass email, the cleaner the close. If Barry follows up asking why, a single line suffices: "We've decided to pursue a different path for distribution right now." No need to elaborate.
Internal Debrief - Do This Within the Hour
Score the Call

Phrases That Undermine You
Why This Matters
In a meeting with a 30-year industry veteran, certain phrases instantly signal inexperience, desperation, or lack of conviction. Barry has heard every version of "we need help" from brands trying to grow. Your job is to be the brand that doesn't sound like the other 49.
Never Say This Why It Hurts You
"We really need distribution help - we can't figure it out on our own."
Desperation pricing. You've just told Barry he has all the leverage. He'll price accordingly. You're exploring options, not begging for rescue. Say: "We're evaluating a few paths into distribution and want to understand if your network is the right fit."
"We're open to anything - whatever you think is best."
Signals you have no strategy. Barry will fill the vacuum with his packaged services (DTC, Amazon). A brand that can't articulate what it wants isn't ready for distribution. Say: "We know exactly what we need - introductions to 2-3 regional distributors serving independent salons."
"We've been trying to get meetings with distributors but nobody responds."
Tells Barry your brand isn't generating pull - distributors see no demand. This massively weakens your negotiating position. If true, don't volunteer it. Say: "We're being selective about our distribution partnerships - CanRad in Canada was our first, and we want the US partner to be the right fit."
"How much would it cost for EBL to handle everything for us?"
You've just asked for the full-service pitch. Now Barry will present a $5K-15K/month retainer covering Amazon, TikTok, ads, and distribution bundled together. Unbundle the ask. Say: "We're specifically interested in your distributor relationships. What would a focused introduction arrangement look like?"
"Tina is the formulator - I handle the business side."
Diminishes Tina's authority in the room. Barry is a men's grooming guy and may default to talking "business" with David. Tina's formulation expertise IS the business differentiator. Say: "Tina built the formulations and leads our salon relationships. She's the reason we have a 96% repurchase rate."
"We're only doing about $125K right now..."
Never lead with a small number. Revenue is context-dependent - $125K with 96% repurchase and 50+ salons is a different story than $125K struggling. Frame traction, not revenue. Say: "We're at a six-figure run rate with salon reorders driving 96% of B2B revenue."
"We don't have a sell-through deck or distributor one-sheet yet."
Signals you're not distribution-ready. Even if it's true, don't reveal it before there's an intro on the table. Say: "We're putting together our distributor materials - what does someone like Bassett typically want to see?" (Let Barry tell you what to build - you learn AND avoid admitting a gap.)
"Maybe you could help us figure out our pricing for distributors."
Pricing is your most sensitive strategic lever. Sharing that you haven't figured it out gives Barry (and eventually distributors) the power to set your margins. Do the math before the meeting. Know your wholesale, distributor, and salon prices cold.
The Frame to Hold
Throughout the entire conversation, hold this frame: "We're a brand that distributors should want. We're choosing who to work with. Barry is one option we're evaluating." This isn't arrogance - it's the posture that gets you taken seriously. Barry reached out to you, not the other way around. Brands that act chosen get chosen.
