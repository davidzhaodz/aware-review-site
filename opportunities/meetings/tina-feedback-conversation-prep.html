Conversation with Tina

Before You Walk In
Your Frame
You are not criticizing Tina. You are investing in her. Tina is the most valuable asset this brand has - her trichologist credibility, her passion, her origin story. CanRad is interested because of her. This conversation is about making sure the world sees what you see, not about dimming anything.
The Real Goal
Walk out with Tina aligned on two things: (1) We need clearer structure in meetings so we don't run out of time before getting to business, and (2) working with a communication coach would be a smart investment to help us all show up sharper as we scale into bigger opportunities.
Radical Candor Reminder
You care about Tina deeply and you need to challenge her directly. Withholding this feedback would be "ruinous empathy" - protecting her feelings at the cost of the business. The most loving thing you can do is be honest. But stay on your side of the net: describe what happened and how it impacted the deal. Don't tell her what she was thinking or feeling.
Conversation Flow - 4 Phases

This conversation should take 20-30 minutes. The phases build on each other - don't skip ahead. Start warm, listen first, then build to the coach recommendation. The key: let Tina identify the problems herself before you name them.

1
Open with Her Perspective
5-7 minutes - mostly listening

Start by asking, not telling. This does two things: it shows respect, and it reveals her level of self-awareness. If she already senses something was off, you're having a collaborative conversation. If she thinks it went great, you have more work to do.

Opening:
"Hey, I wanted to debrief the CanRad call while it's still fresh. How did you feel about it? Like, what did you think went well and what would you do differently?"
Follow-up probes (only if she says it went fine):
"Did you feel like we got through everything we needed to? I noticed Kenny had to leave before we got to pricing."
Or
"What do you think Kenny walked away thinking about us?"

Listen for: Does she mention the time issue? Does she notice she spoke a lot? Does she bring up anything about structure? Her answers tell you how to calibrate the rest of the conversation.

2
Bridge - Share Your Observation
5-7 minutes - SBI framework

Use the Situation-Behavior-Impact framework. Describe what a camera would have recorded, not your interpretation of why.

If she identified some issues herself:
"I'm glad you noticed that too. Here's what I observed from my side. Kenny had 30 minutes and a hard stop. We spent about the first 15 minutes on introductions and story - which was warm and genuine - but it meant we never got to pricing or next steps. He literally had to leave before we could talk terms. That's the part that worries me."
If she thinks it went well:
"I hear you, and I agree the chemistry was great - Stuart even said the founder story is what hooked them. But I want to share something I noticed. Kenny had 30 minutes. We used about 15 of those on introductions and background that they'd already heard in previous meetings. When Kenny finally asked his main question - 'does this sell outside Beverly Hills?' - we ran long on the answer and never got to pricing. He left without us advancing the deal. That's a missed opportunity."
Key Technique
Lead with what worked. The chemistry IS real. Stuart IS hooked. The founder story IS their reason for being interested. Acknowledge all of that genuinely before pivoting to the structural issue. This isn't a compliment sandwich - it's the truth. The content is good. The packaging needs work.
Facts You Can Reference (Don't Use All of Them)
  • Kenny had a hard stop at 30 minutes and had to leave before pricing discussion
  • The introduction/origin story took ~6 minutes - material they'd heard in previous meetings
  • Kenny's main question ("sell-through outside Beverly Hills") got a 4-minute answer when it could have been 30 seconds plus data
  • We never reached the pricing or terms discussion - the things that actually move deals forward
  • This was Meeting 4 with these people - they already know who we are and love the product
3
Connect to the Bigger Picture
5 minutes - elevate from this one call

This is not about one meeting. This is about the fact that AWARE is entering a phase where the stakes of every meeting are getting higher. CanRad is a $40-50M distributor. The next conversations could be with investors, larger retailers, press. The opportunity cost of running long or not getting to business is only going to increase.

Elevating the conversation:
"Here's the thing - this isn't just about CanRad. We're entering a different phase of the business. The meetings we're taking are with bigger and bigger players. CanRad is a $40-50 million distributor with 10,000 salons. The conversations after this could be even bigger. I think we need to make sure our executive communication matches the level of the opportunities we're going after."
Making it about the business, not her:
"Your passion and expertise are our biggest advantage. Nobody else has a founder who's a trichologist with 40 years of experience. But I think there's a gap between how powerful your knowledge is and how effectively it lands in a 25-minute executive meeting. That's a skills thing, not a passion thing. And it's fixable."
4
Introduce the Coach
5 minutes - the recommendation

Position this as an investment, not a fix. CEOs of growing companies work with coaches. It's what serious founders do. Frame it as the next step in AWARE's professionalization, not as remediation.

The pitch:
"I've been thinking about this, and I actually did some research. A lot of founders at our stage work with executive communication coaches - it's pretty standard when you start having meetings at this level. I found someone named Lisa Elia who runs Expert Media Training out in LA. She works with beauty and consumer brand founders specifically - clients like Tea Drops, Beachy Cream. She has a 6-month program designed for exactly this: helping founders communicate their expertise concisely in high-stakes meetings."
If Tina seems receptive:
"She does a free consultation. I think it could be really valuable - not to change who you are or how passionate you are, but to give you frameworks for channeling all of that into these short executive meetings. Think of it like the difference between a salon consultation where you have an hour, and a 3-minute elevator pitch. Same knowledge, different format."
If Tina seems resistant:
"I get it. And I want to be clear - the reason I'm bringing this up is because I think you're our most powerful weapon in these meetings. When you nail a 90-second expert insight, it's worth more than anything else we could say. I just think a coach could help you find that 90-second version more consistently. It would be an unfair advantage for us."
Positioning Options
Pick whichever framing feels most natural:
  • - "This is what founders at our stage do" (normalization)
  • - "I want to invest in our strongest asset" (flattery that's true)
  • - "We all need to level up for what's coming" (team framing)
  • - "Espresso vs. full pot - same quality, more concentrated" (her world)
5
Close with the Next Call Prep
3-5 minutes - make it actionable

End with something concrete and forward-looking. Whether or not she commits to the coach today, get alignment on structure for the next CanRad call.

Transition to the playbook:
"Regardless of the coaching thing, I put together a call flow for our next meeting with CanRad. Basically just making sure we each have a clear role so we cover everything in 25 minutes. You'd be 'The Expert' - your moment would be a 90-second reaction to Kenny's product feedback, which is where your trichologist credibility shines the most. Want to look at it together?"

This gives you a natural way to introduce the team playbook you already created. It also lets her see the role structure as practical and respectful rather than restrictive.

Closing the conversation:
"I love where this company is going, and I love what you bring to it. I just want to make sure the world sees the version of you that I see when you're at your best - precise, authoritative, and impossible to ignore. That's worth investing in."
If She Says...

Anticipate her likely responses and have your pivot ready. Don't rehearse these word-for-word - just know where you're going.

If she agrees and is self-aware

"I know, I felt like I was rambling..." or "I could tell Kenny was losing patience."

Your move: Validate immediately. "That self-awareness is huge. That's exactly why a coach would work - you already see it. You just need someone to help you build the muscle memory to catch it in the moment." Then move straight to the coach recommendation. This is the best-case scenario.

If she gets defensive

"I was just being myself" or "They need to understand our story" or "That's how I connect with people."

Your move: Don't fight the frame. Agree with it. "You're right - your authenticity is what makes this brand special. I'm not asking you to change that. I'm asking: how do we deliver that same authenticity in a format that fits a 25-minute business meeting where the other person has a hard stop? That's a different skill than what you do in the salon, and it's learnable."

If she deflects to others

"Kenny was hard to read" or "David, you could have jumped in" or "Stuart was fine with it."

Your move: Partially agree, then redirect. "You're right that I could have managed the transitions better - that's on me too. That's actually part of why I put together the call flow document. But the bigger thing I want to address is making sure all three of us are set up to be sharp. And for you specifically, I think your expertise is so strong that a coach could help you deliver it in a way that hits even harder."

If she's hurt or emotional

"So you're saying I messed it up?" or "I feel like you're saying I'm not good enough."

Your move: Slow down. Ask "What did you hear me say?" - this surfaces the misalignment. Then clarify: "I'm not saying you messed it up. Stuart literally said the founder story is what hooked them. What I'm saying is that your expertise is so deep that it deserves to be delivered in a way that maximizes its impact. Right now, we're leaving impact on the table. A coach isn't about fixing something broken - it's about sharpening something that's already strong."

If she resists the coach specifically

"I don't need a coach" or "That feels like a lot" or "Can't we just prep better?"

Your move: Don't force it today. "Totally fair. Let's start with better prep for the next call - I have a playbook ready. But I'd love for you to at least do the free consultation with Lisa. No commitment, just a conversation. If it's not the right fit, no pressure. But I think you'd be surprised how much even one session could unlock."

Rules for Yourself

Do

  • Start by asking her perspective first
  • Use "I observed..." and "I noticed..." language
  • Acknowledge her strengths genuinely (they're real)
  • Keep it about behavior and impact, not character
  • Frame the coach as an investment, not a correction
  • Accept partial wins (even agreeing to the playbook is progress)
  • Take ownership of your part ("I should have managed transitions better")

Don't

  • Share the internal analysis document (ever)
  • Use the word "rambling" - she'll hear "you talk too much"
  • Quote specific things she said back to her (feels like surveillance)
  • Compare her to Jade or yourself ("Jade kept it short...")
  • Make this about personality ("you're a talker")
  • Bring up the "poor people" language or other red flags (save for the coach)
  • Push for a decision on the coach today if she's not ready
  • Use "but" after compliments - use "and" instead
The Hardest Part
If Tina gets emotional or defensive, your instinct will be to back off and reassure her. That's "ruinous empathy" - being nice at the expense of being honest. You can be warm AND direct. Hold the space. Let there be silence. You don't need her to agree with everything today. You need her to hear it.

The Sentence That Matters

If you could only say one thing in this entire conversation, it's this:

"Your expertise is our biggest competitive advantage. I want to invest in making sure it lands as powerfully in a 25-minute executive meeting as it does in a 45-minute salon consultation. Those are different skills, and there are people who specialize in exactly this."

Everything else is context. This is the core message.
