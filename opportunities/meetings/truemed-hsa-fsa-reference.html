HSA/FSA Fundamentals (Beginner-Friendly)

What is an HSA?

Health Savings Account - a tax-advantaged savings account paired with high-deductible health plans (HDHPs). Think of it as a 401(k) for healthcare expenses.

Triple Tax Advantage

  • Pre-tax contributions - Money goes in before taxes
  • Tax-free growth - Interest and investment gains aren't taxed
  • Tax-free qualified withdrawals - No tax when spent on qualified medical expenses

Key feature: Money rolls over year to year (no "use it or lose it"). HSAs are owned by the individual, not the employer.

2026 HSA Contribution Limits UNVERIFIED - check IRS.gov

  • Individual: $4,300
  • Family: $8,550
  • Catch-up (55+): Additional $1,000

What is an FSA?

Flexible Spending Account - employer-sponsored account that lets employees set aside pre-tax money for healthcare expenses.

Use-It-or-Lose-It Rule

FSA funds typically expire at the end of the plan year. Employers can offer either:

  • $640 carryover to next year (IRS maximum), OR
  • 2.5 month grace period

This creates urgency: ~47% of FSA holders forfeit money, totaling ~$4.5B lost annually. UNVERIFIED - industry estimates

Market Size & Opportunity

HSA Market

  • ~40M Americans have HSAs UNVERIFIED
  • ~$159B in total assets UNVERIFIED
  • Average balance: ~$4,300 UNVERIFIED
  • Rolls over year to year

FSA Market

  • ~33M Americans have FSAs UNVERIFIED
  • Use-it-or-lose-it pressure
  • Q4 spending surge (before forfeiture)
  • ~$4.5B forfeited annually UNVERIFIED

Combined addressable market: 70M+ potential customers with pre-allocated healthcare dollars actively seeking qualified products to spend on.

Why This Matters for AWARE

HSA/FSA customers are:

  • Health-conscious - They've chosen high-deductible plans to save money and take control of health spending
  • Pre-funded - They have money specifically designated for health expenses
  • Motivated to spend - Especially FSA holders facing year-end forfeiture
  • Seeking qualified products - Looking for legitimate ways to use their healthcare dollars
Section 2

What Makes a Product HSA/FSA Eligible?

IRS Section 213(d): The Core Rule

For a product or service to be HSA/FSA eligible, it must qualify as "medical care" under IRS Section 213(d). Medical care is defined as expenses for:

  • Diagnosis
  • Cure
  • Mitigation
  • Treatment
  • Prevention of disease

The Critical Distinction

Products that are "merely beneficial to general health" are NOT eligible.

Examples:

  • NOT eligible: A shampoo that makes hair shiny and soft
  • Potentially eligible: A treatment that addresses a diagnosed scalp condition (seborrheic dermatitis, alopecia, psoriasis)

The difference is medical necessity for a diagnosed condition vs. general wellness.

CARES Act (2020): Simplified OTC Access

The CARES Act changed the rules for over-the-counter (OTC) products:

  • Before 2020: OTC drugs/medicines needed a doctor's prescription to be HSA/FSA eligible
  • After 2020: OTC drugs and medicines no longer need a prescription
  • This opened the door for many wellness products to explore HSA/FSA eligibility

SIGIS/IIAS: The Gold Standard

SIGIS (Special Interest Group for IIAS Standards) and IIAS (Inventory Information Approval System) represent the highest level of HSA/FSA legitimacy.

How SIGIS/IIAS Works

  1. Product manufacturer registers eligible products with SIGIS
  2. Each product gets UPC codes that are flagged as FSA/HSA eligible
  3. At point-of-sale, the UPC is automatically approved for HSA/FSA payment
  4. No Letter of Medical Necessity needed
  5. No questionnaire, no provider review - instant approval

To get SIGIS/IIAS registered, your product typically needs to be:

  • An FDA-registered OTC drug (with Drug Facts Panel)
  • A medical device
  • A regulated health product with clear medical use

This is the most robust path but requires actual regulatory classification as a drug or device.

IRS Enforcement

Using HSA funds for non-qualified expenses carries penalties:

  • 20% penalty on the withdrawal amount (if under age 65)
  • Income tax on the amount (treated as taxable income)
  • Audit risk - IRS can review HSA spending during tax audits

This is why consumers are cautious about what they buy with HSA/FSA funds and why legitimacy matters.

How Truemed Works (Step by Step)

Truemed's model: Enable "wellness brands" (like AWARE) to accept HSA/FSA payments by providing Letters of Medical Necessity (LMNs) through licensed healthcare providers.

Step-by-Step Process

  1. Brand Onboarding (~1 week)
    • Apply on truemed.com (private Shopify app, not in App Store)
    • Truemed reviews your product claims and ingredients
    • Setup: Install Shopify app, configure payment flow in checkout
    • Cost: ~$800 one-time setup fee UNVERIFIED - may have changed
  2. Customer Experience
    • Customer visits your Shopify store and adds products to cart
    • At checkout, sees "Pay with HSA/FSA" option via Truemed widget
    • Customer completes a brief health questionnaire (~2 minutes)
    • Questions about diagnosis, symptoms, medical history
    • Truemed's licensed provider reviews and issues an LMN if medically appropriate
    • LMN fee: ~$30 per customer (one-time, valid 12 months) UNVERIFIED
    • Customer then pays with HSA/FSA card
  3. Transaction Flow
    • Truemed fees: $2 + 8% per one-time transaction
    • Subscription fees: $15/year per customer (alternative pricing)
    • Net to AWARE: Order total minus Truemed fees
    • Settlements: Standard processing timeline (similar to credit card processing)
  4. The Letter of Medical Necessity (LMN)
    • Formal document from a licensed healthcare provider
    • States that the product is medically necessary for a diagnosed condition
    • Must include: patient name, diagnosis, ICD-10 codes, specific product, medical rationale
    • Valid for 12 months (customer can make multiple purchases)
    • Customer downloads LMN for their records
    • Can be submitted to HSA/FSA plan administrator if challenged

Important: What an LMN Does NOT Do

  • Does NOT make your product "FDA approved"
  • Does NOT change your product's regulatory classification (still a cosmetic if that's what it is)
  • Does NOT guarantee IRS acceptance during an audit
  • Does NOT protect against FDA/FTC enforcement if you make unsubstantiated claims

An LMN is a medical opinion from a licensed provider, not a regulatory approval.

What Truemed Reviews During Onboarding

Truemed evaluates brands based on:

  • Product ingredients and their evidence base
  • Marketing claims and therapeutic positioning
  • Target conditions (what diagnoses can the provider write LMNs for?)
  • Clinical evidence or studies (if available)
  • Brand reputation and compliance history

Possible outcomes:

  • Approved - Truemed onboards you and their providers can issue LMNs
  • Approved with limitations - Only certain products or conditions qualify
  • Rejected - Insufficient evidence or too high risk

Truemed's Legitimacy & Concerns

Credibility Signals

Why Truemed Has Traction

  • Major funding: Raised $34M Series A from Andreessen Horowitz (a16z) in December 2025 UNVERIFIED - check Crunchbase
  • Scale: ~500+ brands reportedly on platform UNVERIFIED
  • Founder credibility: Founded by Calley Means, a health policy advocate with DC connections
  • Major brand adoption: Momentous, Thorne, Athletic Greens, and other established wellness brands
  • Licensed providers: Uses actual MDs/DOs/NPs to issue LMNs, not automated approvals

Concerns and Risks

IRS Scrutiny

March 2024: IRS publicly questioned Truemed-style LMN arrangements in communications and industry forums. UNVERIFIED - check IRS communications

Key IRS concerns:

  • "Food is not medicine" stance - IRS has pushed back on the idea that supplements, wellness products, and "functional foods" are medical expenses
  • LMN legitimacy - Are providers issuing LMNs based on actual medical evaluation, or rubber-stamping based on marketing materials?
  • Audit exposure - Consumers who use HSA/FSA for Truemed purchases could face challenges during IRS audits
  • No safe harbor - There is no formal IRS guidance blessing the Truemed model

What Can Go Wrong?

Scenario 1: IRS Audit of Customer

IRS audits a customer's HSA spending and challenges a Truemed-enabled purchase:

  1. Customer provides the LMN as justification
  2. IRS examiner questions whether the product was truly medically necessary
  3. IRS disallows the expense
  4. Customer pays 20% penalty + income tax on the amount
  5. Customer is angry at both Truemed and your brand

Scenario 2: HSA/FSA Plan Administrator Rejects Claim

Some HSA/FSA plan administrators have their own rules and may reject LMN-based claims:

  • Administrator says "we don't accept LMNs for wellness products"
  • Customer's payment is reversed or they're asked to reimburse the account
  • Customer service nightmare

Scenario 3: Regulatory Backlash

FDA or FTC scrutinizes brands using Truemed:

  • "You're accepting HSA/FSA payments based on medical claims, so you're marketing a drug without approval"
  • FDA sends warning letter demanding you stop or reclassify as a drug
  • Reputational damage

Truemed's Defenses

How Truemed justifies the model:

  • Licensed providers: LMNs are issued by actual doctors based on individual patient questionnaires
  • IRS precedent: LMNs have been accepted for certain products/services (e.g., gym memberships for specific conditions)
  • Patient autonomy: Patients have the right to spend their HSA/FSA funds as directed by their provider
  • Ingredient-level evidence: Many wellness products contain ingredients with published research supporting therapeutic benefits

Limited Audit Precedent

Truemed's model is relatively new (~2022). Key unknowns:

  • No large-scale IRS audit data on Truemed-enabled purchases yet
  • Unclear how aggressively IRS will enforce Section 213(d) for wellness products
  • No court cases or formal IRS rulings on this specific model

Bottom line: Truemed operates in a gray area with real legal and regulatory risk.

Section 5

RISKS OF LISTING ON TRUEMED BEFORE CLINICAL STUDIES

Compound Risk Scenario

This is the most critical section for AWARE's decision-making.

  1. AWARE currently makes therapeutic marketing claims ("DHT blocker," "reduce hair fall," "stimulate hair growth") that are UNSUBSTANTIATED by clinical trials
  2. Listing on Truemed requires a medical provider to state the product is medically necessary for a diagnosed condition (e.g., alopecia, seborrheic dermatitis)
  3. If AWARE can't prove its therapeutic claims with clinical data, the LMN is built on unsubstantiated marketing language
  4. This creates a chain of risk: Marketing claims → LMN justification → HSA/FSA payment → IRS/FDA audit exposure

Specific Risks to AWARE

1. FDA/FTC Risk Amplified

By accepting HSA/FSA payments through medical justification, AWARE is implicitly making even stronger therapeutic claims than its marketing.

  • FDA could view this as marketing a drug without approval
  • FTC could view therapeutic claims as deceptive if not substantiated
  • You're essentially saying "this product treats a medical condition" (via LMN) without FDA drug approval

2. IRS Audit Risk to Customers

If IRS challenges an LMN during customer audit:

  • Customer gets hit with 20% penalty + income tax
  • Customer blames AWARE for offering a "fake" HSA/FSA option
  • Negative reviews, social media backlash, loss of trust
  • Potential class action if many customers are affected

3. Reputational Risk

If IRS or media scrutinizes Truemed model and calls out specific brands:

  • Being listed without clinical backing makes AWARE vulnerable
  • "AWARE Hair uses sketchy tax loophole to sell cosmetics as medicine"
  • Damages premium, science-based brand positioning
  • Distributors and salon partners lose confidence

4. No Clinical Data to Defend Claims

If challenged by FDA, FTC, or IRS, AWARE currently has:

  • No published studies on AWARE products
  • No clinical trial data showing efficacy for hair loss or scalp conditions
  • No peer-reviewed evidence specific to AWARE formulations
  • Only ingredient-level research (not specific to AWARE's formula or dosages)

The Truemed provider is relying entirely on marketing materials and generic ingredient research, which is a weaker position.

5. Truemed May Reject AWARE

Truemed has its own risk management. Possible outcomes:

  • Rejection: "Not enough clinical evidence to support LMNs"
  • Conditional approval: "We can only issue LMNs for seborrheic dermatitis, not alopecia" (limits market)
  • Acceptance based on ingredient-level evidence: Risky for both parties

Act+Acre Precedent

Competitor Already in This Space

Act+Acre, a direct scalp care competitor, uses Flex (Truemed competitor) for HSA/FSA payments.

  • This shows there IS precedent for scalp care products in HSA/FSA space
  • Act+Acre positions products as treatments for scalp conditions
  • Unknown: What clinical data does Act+Acre have? What conditions do their LMNs cover?
  • Research opportunity: Analyze Act+Acre's positioning and Flex integration

Recommended Path (AI-GENERATED)

The safest sequence to minimize risk:

  1. Clean up therapeutic marketing claims to be defensible or remove them entirely until clinical data is available
  2. Run clinical study (even preliminary data with N=30-50 participants) showing efficacy for specific scalp conditions
  3. THEN list on Truemed with substantiated claims and clinical backing

This approach:

  • Reduces FDA/FTC enforcement risk (claims are substantiated)
  • Strengthens LMN justification (provider can cite actual AWARE study data)
  • Protects customers from IRS audit challenges
  • Gives AWARE a marketing asset (published study) to promote
  • Positions AWARE as scientifically rigorous vs. competitors

Key Terms Glossary

LMN (Letter of Medical Necessity)
A formal document from a licensed healthcare provider stating that a product or service is medically necessary for a diagnosed condition. Required for many HSA/FSA purchases of wellness products.
HDHP (High Deductible Health Plan)
A health insurance plan with a higher deductible than traditional plans. Required to open an HSA. For 2026, minimum deductible is $1,650 (individual) or $3,300 (family). UNVERIFIED - check IRS.gov
ICD-10 (International Classification of Diseases, 10th Revision)
A medical coding system used to classify diagnoses and procedures. LMNs must include ICD-10 codes to justify medical necessity (e.g., L21.0 = seborrheic dermatitis of scalp).
SIGIS (Special Interest Group for IIAS Standards)
An industry organization that maintains standards for HSA/FSA eligible products. Products registered with SIGIS can be auto-approved at point-of-sale.
IIAS (Inventory Information Approval System)
A system that uses UPC codes to automatically approve HSA/FSA transactions at point-of-sale. Eliminates need for manual review or LMNs.
MoCRA (Modernization of Cosmetics Regulation Act)
2022 FDA law that modernized cosmetics regulation. Requires facility registration, adverse event reporting, and safety substantiation. Relevant to AWARE if products are classified as cosmetics.
OTC (Over-the-Counter)
Drugs that can be sold without a prescription. OTC drugs must comply with FDA monographs, include Drug Facts Panels, and meet specific regulatory requirements.
CARES Act
2020 federal law (Coronavirus Aid, Relief, and Economic Security Act) that expanded HSA/FSA eligibility for OTC drugs and products, eliminating the prescription requirement.
Section 213(d)
IRS tax code section that defines "medical care" for purposes of HSA/FSA eligibility. Products must be for diagnosis, cure, mitigation, treatment, or prevention of disease.
Drug Facts Panel
The standardized label required on all OTC drug products. Includes active ingredients, purpose, uses, warnings, directions, and inactive ingredients. Regulated by FDA.
NDC (National Drug Code)
A unique identifier assigned to FDA-listed drug products. Required for OTC drugs. Format: labeler code + product code + package code.
FDA Monograph
A set of rules (recipe) for specific OTC drug categories. If your product fits a monograph (e.g., dandruff shampoo), you can market it as a drug without individual FDA approval. Non-monograph products require New Drug Application (NDA).
Triple Tax Advantage
The three tax benefits of HSAs: (1) Pre-tax contributions, (2) Tax-free growth/interest, (3) Tax-free withdrawals for qualified medical expenses. No other account offers all three.
Use-It-or-Lose-It
The FSA rule requiring funds to be spent by the end of the plan year or forfeited. Employers can offer either $640 carryover OR 2.5-month grace period, not both. Creates Q4 spending urgency.
Plan Administrator
The company that manages an employer's HSA or FSA plan (e.g., HealthEquity, WageWorks, Fidelity). They review and approve/deny expense claims. Different administrators have different rules.
Qualified Medical Expense
An expense that meets IRS Section 213(d) definition of "medical care" and can be paid with HSA/FSA funds without tax penalty. IRS Publication 502 lists examples.
