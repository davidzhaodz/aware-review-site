1 "Your revenue is too low for a seed round."
▼
Why they'd raise it

Most beauty VCs expect $1M+ ARR at seed. True Beauty Ventures targets ~$2M for Series A. AWARE is at $125K in 6 months = ~$250K annualized, 75% below typical thresholds.

Counter
•$250K ARR achieved with zero paid acquisition = 5-10x cheaper CAC than typical beauty brand ($85 DTC average). Revenue is capital-efficient, not anemic.
•96% B2B repurchase rate vs 25-30% beauty industry average = revenue quality over quantity. Every salon that buys stays.
•Selva Ventures invests in brands "under $10M demonstrating traction" - organic growth trajectory qualifies.
•We're not asking you to bet on revenue. We're asking you to bet on a 96% repurchase rate and a professional channel with no incumbent.
Source: Beauty Independent - Selva Ventures thesis
2 "Scalp care is too niche. The TAM is limited."
▼
Why they'd raise it

Professional-exclusive scalp care products market is ~$3.5B globally vs $151B total hair care. Salon-exclusive limits addressable market to <5% of haircare consumers.

Counter
•Scalp care growing at 7.1% CAGR (2025-2032), reaching $23.8B. Faster than general haircare (6.4%). Category is expanding, not contracting.
•Business of Fashion called haircare "the most underpenetrated category in beauty right now." Scalp care is the fastest-growing subcategory within it.
•Salon-exclusive is the starting point, not the ceiling. Crown Affair started professional, then launched at Sephora. Same playbook.
•37% of consumers now prioritize hair loss solutions, 34.5% focus on scalp care = demand expanding faster than supply.
Source: Coherent Market Insights - Scalp care forecast
3 "You haven't validated paid acquisition. Organic doesn't scale."
▼
Why they'd raise it

18M organic views is impressive but doesn't prove unit economics through paid channels. VCs remember Glossier's organic success followed by the DTC crash when paid couldn't replicate organic CAC.

Counter
•Our B2B model IS the paid acquisition alternative. Each salon = micro-influencer with a captive audience. $200 CAC per salon, $4K-12K LTV per salon per year. Payback in 1-2 orders.
•50+ salons = 50 distribution points acquiring customers at zero marginal cost. This doesn't need paid ads to work.
•Organic audience has higher intent and retention. 96% repurchase rate proves the organic-to-conversion pipeline works.
Source: Digital Marketing Group on Glossier organic strategy
4 "Salon-only distribution limits your scale."
▼
Why they'd raise it

McKinsey research shows 56% of VC-backed beauty brands (2010-2015) still generate <$100M. "Material growth requires expanding channel mixes." Single-channel risk.

Counter
•B2B-first is sequencing, not permanence. Roadmap: salons first, distributors, then DTC warm traffic, then selective retail.
•K18 started professional-channel, got acquired by Unilever, returned 80% of True Beauty Ventures' fund. Crown Affair: salon validation before Sephora launch.
•Each salon is a zero-cost customer acquisition channel. DTC-first brands pay $85 per customer. We pay $200 per salon that generates dozens of customers.
Source: BoF beauty brand scaling
5 "$55-75 is too expensive for salon take-home."
▼
Why they'd raise it

Premium professional haircare ceiling: Olaplex $30-40, K18 $75. Salon clients may balk at $55-75 for an unknown brand vs established names.

Counter
•K18 at $75 for 50ml established the precedent. AWARE at $55-75 is competitive parity, not outlier.
•96% repurchase rate IS the price acceptance data. Stylists successfully communicate value - clients buy again.
•Stylist trust transfer: "Customers are skeptical of influencers, but trust when a stylist uses products during service."
•65-80% gross margins align with beauty investor expectations. The price point supports venture economics.
Source: K18 pricing comparisons

7 "The exit path for professional scalp care isn't clear."
▼
Why they'd raise it

Recent beauty M&A favors fragrance, derm-backed skincare, general haircare. No clear acquisition precedent for professional-exclusive scalp care specifically.

Counter
•L'Oreal acquired Color Wow (professional haircare) in 2025. Henkel acquired Pravana, Joico, Zotos - all professional hair brands.
•Unilever acquired K18 (True Beauty Ventures portfolio) - returned 80% of fund capital from one deal. Direct comparable to AWARE.
•Estee Lauder acquired Bumble and bumble (salon brand). Wella acquired Briogeo. Salon-origin brands exit to strategics.
•Beauty M&A multiples: 14.9x EV/EBITDA in 2025 vs 9.8x consumer average. Premium valuations persist for beauty.
•Path: $50M revenue in 5-7 years = $150-250M acquisition value at 3-5x revenue multiple.
Source: Beauty M&A 2025

What to Bring

Product samples - Full 3-SKU set. Let them smell/touch.
One-pager - Printed + PDF. Updated with $125K / 50+ salons.
Pitch deck - iPad + PDF link. Do NOT present slide by slide.
Phone with social - 18M views, key viral videos ready.
Before/after photos - Client scalp results (with permission).
Salon testimonials - 3-4 strongest stylist quotes, printed.

What NOT to Bring

  1. Full financial model (don't share until term sheet discussion)
  2. Printed handouts of every page (feels desperate)
  3. Tina on first meeting (save for second meeting, "product deep-dive")

What NOT to Do

  1. Don't present slide by slide. Have a conversation with a deck nearby for reference.
  2. Don't trash competitors. "We admire what Jupiter is doing in DTC. We're solving for the professional channel."
  3. Don't oversell DTC potential. Your strength is B2B.
  4. Don't share full cap table or financial model in meeting #1.
  5. Don't ask "what do you think?" Ask "what questions do you have?"
  6. Don't follow up same-day. Send a substantive follow-up within 24 hours.
  7. Don't name-drop other investors unless they're committed.

Conversation Framework (30 Minutes)

Minutes 0-3
Rapport + Category
Don't start with "thanks for meeting." Start with a category insight. "Have you noticed the gap between consumer scalp care brands and what's actually available in salons? We've been living in that gap." Let them share what they know. Listen.
Minutes 3-8
The Problem (Stylist Perspective)
"Tina is a working hairstylist. She sees scalp issues every day and had nothing credible to use on clients. So she formulated her own." This is the founder-market fit moment. Make it visceral, not theoretical. Pass them the product.
Minutes 8-15
The Traction
Lead with 96% repurchase rate. "In an industry that averages 25-30%, our salons reorder at 96%." Then: $125K / 6 months, 50+ salons, zero paid, 18M organic views. Show the math: $200 CAC per salon, $4K-12K LTV per salon per year.
Minutes 15-22
The Opportunity
"No one owns clean, professional-exclusive scalp care. 7% CAGR. The acquirers are actively buying in this channel." Reference their portfolio: "You backed [X], which proved [Y]. We're building the professional-channel version." This is where you earn the right to discuss the raise.
Minutes 22-27
The Ask
"We're raising ~$1M to go from 50 salons to 100+ and sign our first distribution deal." Use of funds: Sales/BD hire (40%), inventory (25%), marketing (20%), ops (15%). Milestones: $500K-1M revenue and Series A readiness.
Minutes 27-30
Their Turn
"What questions do you have?" (Not "what do you think?") "Is this the kind of thing [Fund] would want to look at more closely?" Clear next step: "Would it be helpful to schedule a follow-up with Tina for a product deep-dive?"
