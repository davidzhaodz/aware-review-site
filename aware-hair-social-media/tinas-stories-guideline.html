Section 1

Series Overview

"Tina's Stories" is a personal storytelling series where Tina narrates life lessons over B-roll footage of herself. The content is about wisdom, not product. Products may be visible, but AWARE is rarely mentioned.

Core Concept

Tina has 40+ years of stories - immigrating to America, building 15 salons across Southern California, working with celebrities, losing everything, starting over, raising a family inside the beauty industry. Each episode extracts one life lesson from a real experience and tells it in under 80 seconds.

This is not educational content. There is no scalp science, no product demonstration, no ingredient education. This is a woman sharing what four decades taught her. The brand connection is atmospheric - Tina is filmed in real environments where AWARE products may be visible, but the story stands on its own.

Target Audiences

Primary: Women 35-65 who are drawn to authentic storytelling, life wisdom, and real women living full lives. They follow for the person, not the brand.

Secondary: Entrepreneurs and small business owners who relate to Tina's journey of building, failing, and rebuilding. Aspirational but grounded.

Tertiary: Beauty professionals who already know AWARE. The stories deepen their connection to Tina as the founder behind the products they use.

Platform Strategy

Film once, repurpose everywhere. All episodes are shot in 9:16 vertical format. Primary distribution: Instagram Reels, TikTok, YouTube Shorts. Cadence: 2-3 episodes per week, batch filmed in sessions of 6-8 episodes.

Episode Framework

Every episode follows the same five-beat structure. The timing is a guide, not a script - Tina speaks naturally and the story dictates the pace.

Episode Structure (60-80 seconds)

Hook
Setup
Tension
Insight
Close
Hook (3s) - One line that stops the scroll Setup (17s) - Set the scene with enough detail to see it Tension (25s) - The challenge, conflict, or unexpected turn Insight (20s) - What she learned, the wisdom Close (15s) - Land the lesson, universal takeaway

8 Hook Templates

Adapted for storytelling and wisdom (not educational or product hooks). Tina picks the template that fits the episode naturally.

1. The Year / The Moment
"In 1987, I made a decision that changed everything."
Anchors to a specific time. Creates instant curiosity about what happened.
2. The Contrarian Lesson
"The worst thing that happened to me was the best thing that happened to me."
Paradox creates tension. The viewer needs to know how both can be true.
3. The Person Who Changed Me
"Someone told me something 30 years ago that I think about every single day."
People lean in when they sense a piece of wisdom coming from experience.
4. The Confession
"I've never told anyone this, but..."
Vulnerability signals this will be real, not rehearsed. Use sparingly.
5. The Number
"After 15 salons, 40 years, and one big failure, here's what I know."
Specific numbers signal real experience. Grounds the story in credibility.
6. The Question Nobody Asks
"Do you know what nobody tells you about building something from nothing?"
Invites the viewer into a private conversation. Feels exclusive.
7. The Quiet Realization
"I was 50 years old when I finally understood something my mother told me."
Reflective, not dramatic. Signals depth rather than spectacle.
8. The Before/After
"I used to believe success meant one thing. Now I know it means something completely different."
Transformation arc in one sentence. Viewer wants to know what changed.

Structure Notes

The timing bar above is a guide. Some episodes will be 55 seconds, some 85 seconds. The structure is meant to help Tina organize her thoughts, not constrain her delivery. If a story naturally runs longer in tension, let it. If the insight is brief and punchy, that's fine. The one non-negotiable: the hook must land in the first 3 seconds. Everything else flexes.

Voice & Delivery Guide

Storytelling Tina is different from Educational Tina. This section defines exactly how.

Storytelling Tina vs. Educational Tina

ToneClinical, authoritative, peer mentorReflective, matter-of-fact, personal
Eye contactDirect to camera, teachingShifts between camera and looking away (remembering)
PacingSteady, informational rhythmSlower, with pauses for emphasis

EnergyConfident expert energyKitchen-table conversation energy
EmotionControlled, professionalAllowed to surface naturally

Tone Calibration

Reflective, not nostalgic. She's not romanticizing the past. She's extracting useful truth from it. The audience should feel like they're getting something they can use, not just hearing someone reminisce.

Matter-of-fact, not dramatic. Tina doesn't need to perform emotions. She states what happened and what it taught her. The simplicity of the delivery is what makes it powerful. "I lost three salons in one year" hits harder when said calmly than when said with dramatic music.

Accomplished but accessible. She's built an empire, worked with celebrities, started over multiple times. But the stories land because she tells them like anyone could have lived them. No bragging, no humblebragging. Just: "This is what happened. Here's what I took from it."

Delivery Tips

  • Pauses are your power. After the hook line, pause for a beat. After the key insight, pause again. Silence makes the audience lean in.
  • Look away when remembering. When telling a story from the past, briefly look slightly off-camera (up and to the side). It signals genuine recall, not recitation.
  • Use your hands naturally. Tina's hands are expressive. Let them move. Close-ups of hands (especially during emotional moments) make great B-roll cut-aways.
  • Start calm, build to the lesson. The hook is quiet, the setup is conversational, the tension builds naturally, and the insight has weight. Don't start at high energy.
  • End decisively. The last line should feel like a period, not an ellipsis. Land the lesson and stop. No trailing off, no "anyway, just something I was thinking about."

Phrases That Fit

Natural for Tina

  • "Something I learned the hard way..."
  • "Nobody told me this. I had to figure it out."
  • "Looking back, I understand now that..."
  • "The truth is simpler than you'd think."
  • "I was wrong about that for a long time."
  • "That's the part nobody sees."
  • "If I could tell my younger self one thing..."

Avoid These

  • "Hey guys, welcome back..."
  • "I want to share something vulnerable..."
  • "In today's video I'm going to talk about..."
  • "Like and subscribe if this resonates..."
  • "Let me tell you about my journey..."
  • "I'm so blessed and grateful..."
  • Any product mention or brand name

B-Roll Shot List

Every episode pairs Tina's narration with relevant B-roll. No more than 5-8 seconds of Tina talking to camera before a B-roll cut. These categories provide a library to pull from.

Salon Life

  • Tina's hands working through hair
  • Close-up of scissors, combs, tools
  • Walking through the salon space
  • Setting up a station in the morning
  • Product bottles on shelves (AWARE visible, not featured)
  • Mirror reflections of the salon

Daily Life

  • Morning routine - coffee, getting ready
  • Driving through SoCal streets
  • Walking in a neighborhood or park
  • Sitting at the kitchen table
  • Looking out a window, quiet moments
  • Simple household moments

Hands Close-Ups

  • Hands resting on a table while talking
  • Hands gesturing during conversation
  • Hands holding a cup of tea or coffee
  • Hands turning pages of an old photo album
  • Hands arranging something with care

Old Photos / Memory

  • Printed photographs on a table
  • Photo albums being opened
  • Close-ups of specific photos (if available)
  • Tina looking at photos, remembering
  • Family photos - Tina and Jade together

Movement / Transition

  • Slow walking shots from behind
  • Opening or closing a door
  • Sunlight moving through a room
  • Hands on a railing, looking at a view
  • Getting into or out of a car

AWARE Environment

  • Products on a shelf (subtle, background)
  • The AWARE workspace or office
  • Product bottles catching light (no labels facing camera)
  • Tina in a setting where AWARE is naturally present
  • Never staged product placement

Filming Tips for Authentic Look

  • Natural light always. No ring lights, no studio setups. Use window light, outdoor light, salon lighting. The warmth and imperfection is the look.
  • Handheld is fine. Slight camera movement feels real. Tripod is OK for talking-to-camera segments, but B-roll should have organic motion.
  • Real locations only. Tina's actual salon, her actual kitchen, her actual neighborhood. Never a rented studio or generic backdrop.
  • Wardrobe variety. Two to three outfit changes per batch filming session. Neutral, warm tones that match Tina's natural aesthetic. Nothing that reads as "costume" or "TV appearance."
  • No makeup for performance. Tina should look like herself on a normal day. Not undone, not overdone. The goal is: she looks exactly like this when she's not on camera.

What to Avoid

  • Ring lights or studio lighting setups
  • Posed product placement shots
  • Over-produced transitions or effects
  • Background music that drowns out her voice
  • Any setting that looks like a brand photoshoot
  • Stock footage or generic B-roll
Section 6

Do's and Don'ts Checklist

Quick reference for tone, content, and brand alignment. Print this page for filming day.

Do

  • Tell stories from real experience
  • Use specific details (years, places, numbers)
  • Pause after key moments
  • Look away from camera when remembering
  • Land the lesson in one clear sentence
  • Show genuine emotion if it surfaces
  • Keep language simple and direct
  • Let silence do the work
  • Film in real, everyday locations
  • Use natural light
  • Wear what you'd normally wear
  • Speak like you're at a kitchen table
  • End decisively - period, not ellipsis
  • Include B-roll cuts every 5-8 seconds

Don't

  • Mention AWARE, products, or brand
  • Include any CTA, product link, or pitch
  • Use marketing language or buzzwords
  • Talk about scalp science or ingredients
  • Film in a studio or with ring lights
  • Stage product placement in shots
  • Script word-for-word (talking points only)
  • Use trending audio or viral sounds
  • Start with "Hey guys" or "Welcome back"
  • Over-explain or add a moral
  • Perform vulnerability (let it happen or don't)
  • Compare yourself to other creators
  • Use hustle culture language
  • Add text overlays beyond hook line

Section 8

Batch Filming Guide

Film 6-8 episodes per session. Each session follows the same structure to maximize Tina's time and energy while producing varied, natural content.

Session Structure

Pre-Session (30 minutes)
Review episode cards together. Tina marks which episodes feel ready, which need discussion, which to skip. Talk through hook lines out loud. Confirm wardrobe and setting for first block. Light check and camera setup.
Block 1 (45 minutes, 3-4 episodes)
Start with the episodes that have the most energy - stories Tina is excited to tell. Film hook first, then talking points, then general B-roll. Two takes maximum per episode. If it doesn't feel natural in two takes, table it.
Break (15 minutes)
Wardrobe change. Review footage briefly. Hydrate. Let Tina decompress - the emotional stories are coming next.
Block 2 (45 minutes, 3-4 episodes)
Heavier or more emotional episodes in the middle (Resilience, Motherhood pillars). End the block with a lighter episode so Tina doesn't leave drained.
Wrap (15 minutes)
Capture extra B-roll: hands, walking, quiet moments, environmental shots. These fill the library for future episodes. Brief debrief: what felt good, what to adjust next time.

Energy Management

Slot Energy Level Best Pillar Fit
First (Block 1, opening)Fresh, enthusiasticEmpire, Celebrity
Middle (Block 1, end)Warmed up, flowingImmigration, Wisdom
After break (Block 2, opening)Reset, reflectiveResilience, Motherhood
Final (Block 2, end)Tiring, keep it lightWisdom (lighter topics), Empire

Wardrobe & Setting Variety

Two to three outfit changes per session creates the illusion of different days. Settings should rotate between available locations: salon, home/kitchen, outdoor. All outfits should be clothes Tina actually wears - nothing purchased specifically for filming.
